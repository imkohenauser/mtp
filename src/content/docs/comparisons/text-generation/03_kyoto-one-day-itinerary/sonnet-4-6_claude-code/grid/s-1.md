Kyoto, summer, one day — here it is, everything at once.

Start at **Fushimi Inari-taisha** the moment it opens, 5:30 AM or even earlier. Beat the heat. Beat the crowds. The thousand torii path in low morning light is genuinely otherworldly — the corridor of orange gates stretching up the mountain, mist if you're lucky, silence if you're early enough. Walk at least to Yotsutsuji, the halfway lookout, maybe further. Two hours, wear good shoes, bring water.

Come down, grab breakfast at a small kissaten near Fushimi station — coffee, toast, egg. The area has a few old-school ones. Don't overthink it, just walk until something looks right.

Train or bus to **Arashiyama** next. Hit the bamboo grove first — narrow path, crowds build fast, get there before 9 AM if possible. Then immediately cross into **Tenryu-ji**, the moss gardens and the painted ceiling dragon (it's in the Hatto hall, do not skip it). After, walk the canal path toward **Jojakko-ji** or **Nison-in** — quieter temples up the hillside, stone steps, maple trees. These get almost no visitors despite being excellent. Wander.

Lunch in Arashiyama — **yudofu** (tofu hot pot) is the local thing, very Kyoto, mild and perfect for a hot day. Several places near Tenryu-ji serve it. Reservation probably not needed for lunch at most spots, but worth calling ahead for dinner anywhere good.

Afternoon: bus or taxi to **Kinkaku-ji** — yes, it's crowded, yes, it's gold, yes, it's worth it for about 20 minutes of stunned staring at the reflection. Then walk or bus to **Ryoan-ji**, the famous rock garden. Early afternoon light in summer hits the raked gravel hard. Sit on the veranda and actually look at it for ten minutes. Most people walk through in two.

Late afternoon: take the bus to **Nishiki Market** in central Kyoto — narrow covered arcade, vendors selling pickled vegetables, grilled skewers, fresh tofu, sesame crackers, dried fish, weird chewy snacks. Eat as you walk. This is the stomach-filling buffer before dinner.

Dinner: **Gion** district. You want to be here at dusk anyway — the old machiya townhouses, the lanterns, the occasional maiko. Eat somewhere that requires advance reservations: kaiseki is the peak Kyoto experience, multi-course, seasonal, painstaking, quiet. Places like **Hyotei** or **Kikunoi** (both Michelin-starred) need reservations weeks or months in advance. Book before you leave home or you will be eating ramen, which is also fine but not the same.

After dinner: wander Gion. The streets around **Hanamikoji-dori** especially. If you want a drink, there are quiet bars tucked into alleys — sake bars, whisky bars, tiny counter places seating eight people.
