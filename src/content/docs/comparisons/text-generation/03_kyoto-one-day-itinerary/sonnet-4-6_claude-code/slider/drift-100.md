Kyoto in summer. The word itself carries a kind of weight — the heat there is legendary, a humid press that turns temple gravel into something almost liquid-looking by afternoon. June through August, Kyoto doesn't just get warm; it gets *saturated*.

There's a moment, maybe around 6am, when the Philosopher's Path along the canal is still cool and the light comes sideways through maple leaves. That might be the truest version of it. Nanzen-ji is only a short walk from the northern end of the path — the great sanmon gate, the aqueduct cutting through the trees like a ruin that forgot to be ruined. The afternoon problem in Kyoto summer is the sun between 1 and 4pm. It's not really sightseeing time; it's tea-and-rest time. Somewhere like the Hosoo textile showroom in Nishijin, or a kissaten with old jazz records, or — if you want to lean into the heat — the covered shopping streets of Teramachi or Shinkyogoku, which have a pleasantly timeless scruffiness, paper fans and traditional snacks alongside the usual tourist drift.

Then evening resurrects the city. Fushimi Inari after 5pm, once the tour groups have thinned. The famous vermillion gates stretch up the mountain in a tunnel that keeps going longer than you expect. Most visitors turn back at the first plateau; if you keep climbing, the crowds dissolve and it becomes something stranger and more personal. The city spreads below through gaps in the trees.
