#### Subverting Victorian Conformity
During the Victorian era, children's literature existed primarily to instruct and moralize. Carroll, however, subverted this tradition by writing a story devoid of obvious moral lessons. Instead of lecturing the reader, he chose to celebrate the wild, untamed imagination of childhood. By portraying the adult-like characters of Wonderland as tyrannical, frantic, or downright foolish, the book vindicates the child's perspective in an overly structured world.

This linguistic chaos is anchored by a surprising structure, which stems from the author's academic background.

#### Charles Dodgson’s Dual Identity
Behind the pseudonym Lewis Carroll was Charles Lutwidge Dodgson, a lecturer in mathematics at Christ Church, Oxford. He created this tale during a rowing trip with the young Liddell sisters, including the real-life Alice. Consequently, the absurd scenarios Alice faces are actually mathematical jokes and logical paradoxes in disguise. His training in geometry and algebra allowed him to construct a nonsense world that operates on its own strict internal logic. He made chaos systematic.

#### Satirizing the New Mathematics
Scholars suggest that the nonsensical rules of Wonderland satirize the emerging mathematical theories of the mid-19th century. For instance, the Caterpillar's advice to "keep your temper" holds a double meaning of maintaining proportion, referencing mathematical scaling. Similarly, the Cheshire Cat's disappearance, leaving only a grin, mirrors the abstract concepts of symbolic algebra where numbers are removed, leaving only relations. Through these clever parodies, the story becomes a playground for intellectual ideas.
