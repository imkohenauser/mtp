There's a moment, early in *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*, when a little girl chases a waistcoat-wearing rabbit down a hole — and Lewis Carroll simply never brings her back.

Not really. Not the same girl, anyway.

What follows is one of the strangest, funniest, most quietly radical books ever written. Alice tumbles into a world that operates on its own internal logic — a logic that is *almost* sensible, almost rule-governed, almost polite, and therefore completely, gloriously insane. A caterpillar perched on a mushroom demands to know who she is. A cat dissolves into nothing but its grin. A queen sentences everyone to beheading for the mildest of offenses, and somehow the garden party goes on.

But here's what makes it more than a fairy tale: **Alice pushes back.**

She's not a passive little girl swept along by wonder. She argues with the Caterpillar. She calls out the Mad Hatter's terrible logic at his eternal tea party. She stands up to the Queen of Hearts. In a world of nonsense, she keeps reaching for sense — and Carroll keeps snatching it away, which is both hilarious and, if you're paying attention, a little bit profound.

Because Wonderland isn't random. It's a funhouse mirror held up to the adult world — its arbitrary rules, its petty authority, its social rituals that everyone performs without anyone knowing why. The trial at the end, with its verdict delivered before the evidence, lands differently once you've sat through a few meetings.

Carroll writes with the precision of the mathematician he was and the mischief of someone who genuinely delighted in children's company. The wordplay is dazzling — puns that work on three levels, logic puzzles embedded in riddles, poems that skewer Victorian sentimentality with a straight face. "Why is a raven like a writing desk?" the Hatter asks. He never answers. He was never going to.

And underneath all the wit is something genuinely dreamlike — not the safe, wish-fulfillment kind of dream, but the *real* kind. The kind where the rules keep shifting, where you can't quite remember who you are, where something absurd fills you with inexplicable dread. Alice spends much of the book uncertain whether she's shrinking or growing, whether she's herself or someone else entirely. For a story aimed at children, it is quietly terrifying in the best possible way.

It is also, at about 100 pages, one of the most efficiently brilliant things ever committed to paper.

Read it as a child and you'll love the chaos. Read it as an adult and you'll recognize the world.
