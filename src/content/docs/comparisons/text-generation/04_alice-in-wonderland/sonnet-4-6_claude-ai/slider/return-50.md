There is a girl who falls down a hole.

That's the whole story, in a way — and also not the story at all. Because what happens *inside* the hole is the entire point. Alice falls, and as she falls, she has time to wonder whether she'll fall straight through to the other side of the earth. She has time to take a jar of marmalade off a passing shelf and politely put it back empty so she doesn't drop it on someone below. She is, even in freefall, very sensible.

Wonderland rewards her sensibility by making it completely useless.

What Carroll wrote in 1865 is one of the strangest and most quietly terrifying books in the English language — dressed up as a children's story, which is either a brilliant disguise or a brilliant joke or both. Nothing in Wonderland follows rules, except that everyone *insists* on rules that contradict each other. The Queen of Hearts sentences people before their trials. A tea party is frozen in time because the Mad Hatter offended Time itself, and now it is always six o'clock, and the cups are always dirty, and no one can leave. A Cheshire Cat disappears piece by piece, and the last thing that remains — hovering in the air, patient and slightly smug — is its smile.

Alice, for her part, keeps trying to make sense of things. She recites her lessons and gets them wrong in unsettling ways. She wonders, when she keeps changing size, whether she's still herself at all — and this, it turns out, is a question nobody in Wonderland can answer for her. When she finally grows large enough to frighten everyone, she does. When she decides the whole thing is a pack of cards, it dissolves.

That's what the book is *actually* about: a child discovering that authority is performance, that logic is a shared agreement that can be withdrawn at any moment, that the people who seem most confident about how the world works are often running on pure nonsense. Carroll dressed this up in riddles and puns and a very fast white rabbit. He made it delightful. He made it funny. He also made it strange in a way that lingers.

You will finish it in two hours and think about it for years.

On the other hand — perhaps Alice doesn't escape Wonderland at all. She wakes up on a riverbank and tells her sister the dream, and her sister imagines it too, and passes it forward. The story has been spreading ever since, dream to dream, and you haven't read it yet, which means Wonderland is still waiting, patient as a Cheshire smile, for you to fall in.
