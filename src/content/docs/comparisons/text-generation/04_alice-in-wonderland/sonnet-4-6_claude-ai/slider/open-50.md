The Mad Hatter isn't *crazy*. He's trapped in a tea party at 6 o'clock forever because of a quarrel with Time — and if you think about it, that makes a strange kind of sense. Alice keeps trying to apply her rules — her lessons, her manners, her reasonable expectations — and Wonderland keeps dissolving them. Carroll wrote this for a real child, told aloud on a rowing trip on the Thames, and you can still feel that in the prose — the way it keeps glancing sideways at the reader with a kind of shared amusement, the way it indulges itself and doubles back. Alice herself is sharper than she's usually depicted: skeptical, impatient, sometimes a little rude, occasionally on the edge of real fear before she rallies.

It's a short book. You could read it in an afternoon. The question it leaves you with — whether Wonderland was a dream, or whether the waking world is the stranger place — it doesn't quite answer.
