**First…** Alice's Adventures in Wonderland is a book that hides its real magic behind its surface strangeness. **Next…** the way to make someone want to read it is to show that the weirdness isn't decoration — it *is* the argument. **Therefore…** tell it as an act of seduction, not summary.

---

**1. The premise is a trap (in the best way)**

A girl follows a rabbit into a hole. Simple enough. But within three pages, Carroll has dissolved the floor under your feet and you realize: *this book has no floor.* Alice falls for what feels like minutes — long enough to wonder aloud whether she'll come out in Australia, long enough to take a jar of marmalade off a shelf and politely put it back rather than drop it, because dropping it might hurt someone below. She doesn't know if there *is* a below. She puts the jar back anyway. That detail alone tells you everything about who Carroll was.

**2. Alice is not who you think**

Most people remember Alice as a passive little girl in a pinafore, swept along by chaos. She isn't. She argues. She corrects people's grammar. She tells the Caterpillar — a three-inch creature sitting on a mushroom blowing hookah smoke in her face — that he is being rather *rude*. She gets things wrong constantly and doesn't much care. She is curious in the way children actually are: not sweetly, but almost aggressively, with no patience for things that don't make sense, and also no particular fear of them.

**3. The logic is real**

This is the thing nobody warns you about. Carroll was a mathematician, and Wonderland runs on genuine logic — it's just that the axioms are different. When the Queen of Hearts says sentence first, verdict afterward, she isn't being absurd for the sake of absurdity. She's applying a perfectly consistent legal philosophy, just not ours. The Mad Hatter's tea party is frozen at 6 o'clock because he quarreled with Time, and Time is sulking — which is both ridiculous and, once stated, completely airtight. You start to wonder who decided *our* logic was the right one.

**4. It is very, very funny**

Not charming-funny. Actually funny. Carroll writes jokes that land like a surgeon's cut: *"Take some more tea," the March Hare said to Alice, very earnestly. "I've had nothing yet," Alice replied in an offended tone, "so I can't take more." "You mean you can't take less," said the Hatter. "It's very easy to take more than nothing."* The Hatter wins that argument, and you know it.

**5. What it's actually about**

Alice is a child moving through a world run by adults whose rules are arbitrary, whose authority is absurd, and who expect deference anyway. She keeps looking for the logic, keeps trying to be polite, and keeps finding that the rules change or were never rules at all. By the end, when she finally shouts *"You're nothing but a pack of cards!"* — it lands like a revolution. She didn't go mad. The world was mad. She just took long enough to see it.

---

**Conclusion:** You should read *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* not because it is a beloved classic, but because it is a short, sharp, funny book that has been quietly asking a dangerous question for 160 years — *who decided any of this makes sense?* — and never once blinks while asking it.
