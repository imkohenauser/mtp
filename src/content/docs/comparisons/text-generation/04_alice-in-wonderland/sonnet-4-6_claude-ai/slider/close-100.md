That's the whole plot, really. A bored child on a summer afternoon sees a rabbit in a waistcoat checking a pocket watch, decides to follow it, tumbles into a rabbit-hole, and lands in a world where nothing — not size, not logic, not manners, not time — behaves the way it should.

What happens next is the part that's impossible to summarize without ruining it, because *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* isn't really a story about events. It's a story about a mind encountering a world that refuses to make sense. Alice meets a Caterpillar who asks her who she is, and she realizes she doesn't quite know. She meets a Cheshire Cat who vanishes until only his grin remains. She sits down to a tea party where no one is allowed to move to a clean cup because it's always six o'clock, and time stopped at tea, and it will never not be tea time again. She stands before a Queen who sentences people to death before the verdict, and somehow this is the most recognizable thing that has happened to her all day.

Carroll was a mathematician, and it shows — not in any dry way, but in the precision of the absurdity. Every broken rule is broken *correctly*. Every piece of nonsense is internally consistent. The Mad Hatter isn't random; he is rigidly logical within a logic that simply isn't yours. That is what makes the book so unsettling, and so funny, and so strange to reread as an adult: you realize Wonderland isn't chaotic at all. Everyone there is following rules. Alice just doesn't know them. And the horror — played always for laughs — is that neither does she know herself.

She shrinks. She grows. She almost drowns in her own tears. She is nine inches tall and then nine feet. Through all of it she remains stubbornly, hilariously, very properly *Alice* — trying to remember her lessons, minding her manners, insisting that things ought to make sense even as they manifestly do not.

The book is 163 years old and it has never been surpassed at what it does. Not the films, not the sequels, not the countless retellings. The original 150 pages are a kind of miracle: a dream written down by someone who understood that the best dreams don't resolve, they *escalate*, until the dreamer wakes up and wonders, for just a moment, which side of the hole is real.

**Conclusion:** Read it in a single sitting, ideally on an afternoon when you have nothing urgent to do. It will take you ninety minutes and leave you thinking about it for considerably longer — about identity, about authority, about the strange fact that the rules of the world only hold because everyone agrees to pretend they do. Alice stops pretending. That's the whole book. That's everything.
