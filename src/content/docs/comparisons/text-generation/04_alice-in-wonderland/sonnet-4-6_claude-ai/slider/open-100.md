There's a girl who falls down a hole.

That's the whole story, in one sentence — and somehow, it is also the wrong sentence entirely, because nothing that happens inside that hole is something you could have predicted from the outside.

She falls, and the falling takes a very long time. Long enough for her to look at the shelves on the walls of the hole as she passes them. Long enough to wonder, quite calmly, whether she will come out the other side of the Earth. Long enough for her to start a conversation with herself about geography, and to realize she doesn't quite know where the antipodes are, and to decide that probably doesn't matter, because she's falling.

Lewis Carroll wrote *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* in 1865 for a real child, on a real riverboat, making things up as he went. The child asked him to write it down. She must have recognized something.

What she recognized, maybe, is this: Wonderland is not a dream. It runs on logic — fanatical, rigorous, uncompromising logic — applied to a world where the premises are simply different. The Queen of Hearts wants to behead everyone. The Cheshire Cat has opinions. The Mad Hatter is stuck at a tea party not because he's mad, but because of a legal dispute with Time. Everything follows. Nothing makes sense. Both of those things are true simultaneously, which is its own kind of sense.

Alice herself is maybe the strangest character in the book. She is not afraid. Or rather — she is afraid, and she does things anyway, and she argues with everyone, and she is wrong sometimes, and right sometimes, and she has terrible manners about mushrooms, and at one point she almost cries herself into a lake and has to swim out of it. She is seven years old. She is one of the most philosophically interesting characters in the English language.

There are ways to read this book as satire — Victorian education, Victorian logic, Victorian table manners taken to their conclusion. There are ways to read it as mathematics made narrative (Carroll was a mathematician). There are ways to read it as something approaching existentialism, a child wandering through a world with no stable rules, trying to hold her sense of self together when everything around her keeps insisting she must be someone else.

There's also a way to read it as just — terribly, strangely, specifically *funny*, in a way that hasn't aged, which almost never happens.

The Dormouse has a story inside a story inside a treacle well. The Pigeon accuses Alice of being a serpent, and has a reasonable argument. A baby turns into a pig, and Alice reflects that it was always a rather piglike baby, so this is probably fine.

Here is maybe the real question: What kind of hole would you have to fall down to end up somewhere with rules like that? And once you found it — would you want to go back up?
