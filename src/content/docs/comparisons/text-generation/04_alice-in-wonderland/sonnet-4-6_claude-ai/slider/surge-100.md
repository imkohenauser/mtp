She falls.

And falls. And falls and falls and *falls* — long enough to notice the furniture on the walls, long enough to wonder if she'll fall straight through the earth, long enough to eat some marmalade from a passing jar. She's already stopped being surprised and it's only page four.

That's the trick. That's the whole trick of this book. Carroll doesn't give Alice time to panic or recover or explain. He just keeps going. He moves like a fever dream that knows exactly where it's headed while appearing to go nowhere at all. A caterpillar smokes and demands to know who you are. Fair question. A duchess's baby turns into a pig — casually, completely, without ceremony. There's a Cheshire Cat who disappears one piece at a time, leaving only the smile, and the smile is somehow the most logical thing about any of it.

There is a trial for stolen tarts that makes less sense than any real trial and somehow far more. The Mad Hatter and the March Hare are stuck at tea forever because time stopped at six o'clock and they can't get past a quarrel they had with it. They just rotate through the seats. Alice, though. Alice is the thing people forget to mention when they talk about this book. She is not passive. She is not sweet. But here is the thing — here is why it has lived for over a hundred and fifty years — it doesn't feel like nonsense when you're inside it. It feels like the truest description of what it's like to be small in a world designed by and for people who have forgotten what it's like to be small. The rules keep changing. The adults are irrational and certain of themselves. Every door is either too big or too small. You need a key you don't have and when you have it you're the wrong size and when you're the right size you've lost the key.

You've been there. That's the thing. You've been there.

Read it.
