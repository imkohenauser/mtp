That sentence shouldn't work. It's too simple. Nothing in it earns your attention. And yet — it's the opening move of one of the strangest, most precisely imagined novels in the English language, and 160 years later people are still falling in with it.

Alice's Adventures in Wonderland is not a children's book that adults can enjoy. The Cheshire Cat vanishes leaving only its grin because a grin is the part of a cat that matters. The Mad Hatter's tea party is frozen at six o'clock because time stopped and there is nothing to be done about that. The horror is not that Wonderland is irrational. The horror is that it is rational — just not *Alice's* rational.

She tries, methodically and with genuine effort, to apply everything she knows, and nothing works, and she keeps trying anyway. She is, in this way, the most recognizable character in the book.

There is a trial at the end. There are flamingos used as croquet mallets. There is a caterpillar who asks the one question nobody has an answer to. There is a conversation with a Duchess about everything having a moral.
