Here is the story of *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* — told to make you want to pick it up.

A girl falls down a rabbit hole.

That's the setup, and Carroll gives it to you in about thirty seconds. No preamble, no world-building exposition. Alice is bored on a riverbank, she spots a white rabbit muttering *"Oh dear! Oh dear! I shall be too late!"* — and she follows it. Down she goes.

What she finds at the bottom is a world that operates on its own internal logic, and that logic is *dream logic*: everything makes a kind of sense, until suddenly it doesn't, and then it makes a different kind of sense. A bottle says DRINK ME and Alice drinks it. She shrinks. A cake says EAT ME and she eats it. She grows. The rules exist, but they answer to no one.

Carroll was a mathematician, and it shows. Wonderland isn't random — it's *inverted*. A trial is held where the verdict comes before the evidence. A queen plays croquet with live flamingos as mallets and hedgehogs as balls. A tea party is frozen in time at six o'clock because the Mad Hatter once quarreled with Time itself. The humor is bone-dry, syllogistic, and genuinely strange. It has aged better than almost any Victorian children's book precisely because it isn't moral. Nobody learns a lesson. Nothing is tidied up.

What makes you want to read it is Alice herself. She is not brave in the sword-and-shield sense. She is curious, slightly exasperated, and *stubbornly reasonable* in a world designed to be unreasonable. She talks back to queens. She corrects grammar. When a Caterpillar sitting on a mushroom blowing smoke rings demands to know who she is, she says — with complete sincerity — that she's not entirely sure, because she's changed so many times that morning. That answer is more honest than most adults manage in a lifetime.

The book is 96 pages. You could read it in two hours. You will find it funny in a way you didn't expect, and unsettling in a way you can't quite explain, and you will understand for the first time why it has never stopped being read since 1865.
