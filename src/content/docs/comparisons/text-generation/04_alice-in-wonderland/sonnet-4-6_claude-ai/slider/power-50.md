Alice's Adventures in Wonderland is the strangest novel ever written, and that is precisely why it has outlasted nearly every other book from 1865.

Most children's stories are about growing up — learning to be good, to be brave, to fit in. Carroll's book is about something stranger and more honest: the experience of being a child in a world that doesn't make sense, run by adults whose rules are arbitrary and whose logic is broken. Alice doesn't fix Wonderland. She survives it, largely by trusting her own mind when everyone around her insists she's wrong.

The plot is deceptively simple. A girl falls down a rabbit hole and wanders through an impossible country — shrinking and growing, talking to caterpillars, playing croquet with flamingos, attending a tea party that never ends. But Carroll packs each scene with something modern readers feel immediately: the absurd social pressure to perform sanity inside systems that are themselves insane. The Queen of Hearts screams "Off with their heads!" at everything. The Cheshire Cat is the only one who admits that everyone here is mad. The Mad Hatter's tea party is stuck at six o'clock forever because he quarreled with Time itself.

What makes you want to *read* it rather than just know the story is the language. Carroll's prose is precise, funny, and slightly dangerous — he understands that nonsense only works when it follows its own internal logic perfectly. The puns don't feel like jokes; they feel like the world momentarily revealing how unstable it actually is. When the Mock Turtle explains that he studied "Reeling and Writhing" instead of reading and writing, it's funnier on the page than any description of it can be.

Alice herself is the best part. She is polite, curious, occasionally frustrated, and utterly undefeated. She talks back. She argues with queens. She tells the court, at the end, exactly what she thinks of them.

Read it because it's short — two or three hours. Read it because the Disney movie is a pale copy. Read it because there is no other book quite like it, and there won't be.
