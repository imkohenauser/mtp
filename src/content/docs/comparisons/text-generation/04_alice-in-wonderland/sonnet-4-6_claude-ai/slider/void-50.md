There is a girl who falls.

Not trips. Not stumbles. *Falls* — all the way down, past shelves of marmalade and maps of places she doesn't recognize, into a country that has no obligation to make sense.

The country obliges anyway.

---

Wonderland is not a dream. Carroll never says it's a dream. Pay attention to that.

It is a place where a baby becomes a pig because it is more honest that way. Where a Queen sentences you before the verdict and the verdict before the crime, because that is how systems work if you look at them clearly enough. Where a Cheshire Cat vanishes piece by piece until only the smile remains — which is exactly what happens to certain kinds of confidence.

Alice is seven. She argues with everyone. She is almost always wrong about the rules and almost always right about the point.

---

The book is short. You could read it in two hours.

What stays afterward is harder to describe. A trial that collapses under its own absurdity. A caterpillar asking *Who are you?* and not accepting the answer. A Mad Hatter stuck at a tea party that never ends because time stopped and it is always six o'clock.

These things meant something in 1865. They mean something now. The machinery is different. The logic is identical.

---

Alice eventually stands up.

She looks at the cards flying toward her and says: *You're nothing but a pack of cards.*

Everything falls apart. She wakes up.

The question the book leaves open — quietly, without underlining it — is whether waking up was the right move.
