**What it is:** *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* is Lewis Carroll's 1865 novel about a bored, curious English girl who tumbles into a world where nothing behaves the way it should. **① What happens**

Alice follows a White Rabbit in a waistcoat — because of course she does — and falls for a very long time into a very strange place. She drinks a potion that shrinks her, eats a cake that makes her enormous, nearly drowns in her own tears, and argues with a Caterpillar sitting on a mushroom who asks, with genuine philosophical menace, *"Who are you?"*

She crashes a tea party frozen in time. She plays croquet with flamingos as mallets and hedgehogs as balls, while the Queen of Hearts screams "Off with their heads!" at everyone and no one. She meets a cat that fades away until only its grin remains — floating in the air, grinning at her.

Then she wakes up. It was a dream.

**② Why it's stranger than you've heard**

The Disney version taught you it's whimsy and color. Carroll's original is something darker and funnier and more unsettling. The humor is dry and savage — the kind that makes you laugh and then wonder why you laughed. Adult characters talk to Alice like she's an idiot; she talks back like *they're* the idiots. Everyone is absolutely certain of rules that make no sense. No one apologizes.

Carroll was a mathematician. Wonderland isn't random — it's *logically inverted*. The madness is rigorous. That's what makes it so disorienting: you keep trying to find the system, and there is one, but it runs exactly backwards from the world you know.

**③ Why it still matters**

The book is about what it feels like to be small in a world made by and for people who already know all the rules — and who refuse to explain them. Alice keeps trying to be polite, to follow logic, to make sense of things. Wonderland keeps refusing. That experience — of being earnest and reasonable in a world that is neither — is not a children's experience. It's a *human* one.

You've already lived a version of this story. Carroll just had the courtesy to make it funny.
