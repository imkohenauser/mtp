There was a girl who fell down a hole.

Not a metaphor. An actual hole, in an actual field, on an actual afternoon when she was bored of sitting beside her sister and bored of a book with no pictures and bored of the whole flat reasonable surface of the world — and then a white rabbit ran past in a waistcoat, checking a pocket watch, and she followed it.

The hole went down a long way. Long enough to notice the shelves of marmalade on the walls going past. Long enough to wonder, falling, whether she would come out the other side of the earth entirely. Long enough to start talking to herself, which she had always been good at.

At the bottom: a world built like a dream that forgot to apologize for itself.

There is a pool made of her own tears. A caterpillar on a mushroom, smoking, asking *who are you* as if it were not a strange question to ask a child but the only question worth asking. A duchess with a baby that becomes a pig. A tea party held in permanent defiance of time, because time stopped at six o'clock and so it is always teatime now and always will be, and the cups keep moving around the table, unwashed.

There is a Cheshire Cat who disappears leaving only the smile behind. Hanging in the air. Grinning at nothing.

There is a queen who solves every problem the same way.

And there is Alice, who is seven years old and absolutely furious about most of this, and who refuses — even here, even underground, even surrounded by creatures who make no sense and play by rules that shift without warning — she *refuses* to stop being herself.

That is the whole book. A girl who stays herself in a place designed to make that impossible.

Carroll wrote it in 1865 for a real child named Alice Liddell, during a rowing trip on the Thames. He told it out loud, improvising, while the oars dipped in the water. She asked him to write it down.

It is 180 pages. You could read it in an afternoon. The afternoon you spend on it will be stranger than any you have spent recently, and the strangeness is not frightening — it is the strangeness of logic pushed past the edge of its own map, language examined until it starts to wobble, and a child who looks at all of it and says: *well, this is very curious.*
