There is a little girl who, one summer afternoon, decides that a book without pictures is not worth reading. This is the first sentence of *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*, more or less, and Carroll places it there as a joke — because the book you are holding is exactly the kind she means to dismiss. But here is the thing: Alice does not read a book. She falls into one.

She falls because she follows a white rabbit. She follows the rabbit because it is wearing a waistcoat and checking a watch, and this is strange, and she is the kind of child who notices what is strange and leans into it rather than away. This distinction — the child who chases rather than retreats — is not incidental to the story. It is the entire engine.

On the surface, what follows is nonsense. A girl shrinks and grows, attends a party where time has stopped, plays croquet with flamingos, and stands trial for a theft she did not witness. These episodes resolve into nothing. Nobody is rescued. No lesson is delivered. The narrative refuses the shape that stories are supposed to have, the one where trouble accumulates toward meaning and meaning arrives like a gift.

But beneath the nonsense is something stranger and more unsettling: a child trying to apply logic to a world that has decided logic is optional. Alice does not lose her mind in Wonderland. She insists on keeping it. She corrects the Hatter's syllogism. She challenges the Queen's sentence-before-verdict. She demands that words mean what they mean, that rules apply equally, that cause precede effect. The world ignores her. The world wins. And this — not the flamingos, not the playing cards, not the Cheshire Cat dissolving into just a grin — is what makes the book genuinely disturbing.

Carroll was a mathematician. The derangements of Wonderland are not random; they are the specific derangements of logic when its foundations are removed. A world where "I mean what I say" and "I say what I mean" are not the same sentence is not a dream world. It is language examined until it collapses. The Mad Hatter's tea party is an infinite loop — they move around the table to clean cups that never get washed. Eternity as housekeeping. Carroll saw something about the relationship between systems and meaning that most adults, pressed by the urgency of their appointments, choose not to look at directly.

Alice looks directly. She is seven, and she has not yet learned that you are not supposed to ask the questions she asks. Why is a raven like a writing desk? The Hatter does not know. He posed the riddle with no answer in mind, because the point was never the answer. The point was the posing — the performance of meaning without its substance. Alice finds this intolerable. So do we, reading alongside her. Carroll has constructed a philosophical trap and put a child in it as our proxy, and we feel her indignation as our own because her indignation is correct. She is right. Wonderland is wrong. And yet Wonderland persists.

This is the book's final depth: Alice wakes up, shakes off the dream, and runs in to tea. The world she returns to — the one with pictures and conversations and a sensible sister reading on the bank — is implicitly contrasted against Wonderland and found safe, restored, ordinary. But Carroll does not let this comfort settle. The sister, in the closing lines, imagines Alice grown up, still carrying the wonder of her childhood — and the passage is tender and slightly sad, because the reader understands what Carroll understands: Wonderland is not where the strangeness lives. The strangeness is here, always has been, in every rule that cannot explain its own foundation, every authority that cannot justify its own decree, every word that means whatever its speaker needs it to mean at the moment. Alice went underground and found us.

Read it. Not because it is charming — though it is — but because Carroll wrote a children's book in which a child is the only sane person in the room, and had the courage to let her lose anyway.
