There is one thing Lewis Carroll never tells you: the rabbit hole has no bottom.

Alice doesn't fall into a fantasy. She falls into a world that runs on logic — but the wrong logic. Every creature she meets is operating on perfectly consistent internal rules. The Mad Hatter isn't mad. He's trapped in a moment of time and responding to that fact with complete rationality. The Queen of Hearts isn't cruel for cruelty's sake. What he wrote down was a dream about a girl who keeps being told she is the wrong size, says the wrong things, remembers the wrong poems, and doesn't know the right rules — and who, despite all of that, keeps going. Not because she is brave. Because she is *curious*. Curiosity is the only compass that works in Wonderland, and it is the only one Alice ever carries.

The book is short. You can read it in two hours. But the images stay: the Cheshire Cat fading until only its smile remains. The trial where the sentence comes before the verdict. The caucus-race where everyone runs in circles and everyone wins. These aren't nonsense. They are the clearest pictures ever drawn of how institutions actually work, how language is used to confuse instead of clarify, how authority defends itself.

Read it because it's funny. Read it because it's strange. But notice, when you're done, that you understand something you didn't before — and that you can't quite say what it is. That's the rabbit hole. Carroll put it there on purpose.
