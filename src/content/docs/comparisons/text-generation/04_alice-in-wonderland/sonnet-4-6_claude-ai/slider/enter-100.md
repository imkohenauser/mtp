There is a book that was written in 1865 and has never once felt old.

It begins with a girl who is bored. Genuinely, specifically, mortally bored — sitting beside her sister on a riverbank, watching her read a book with no pictures in it. You know that feeling. Carroll knew you'd know it. And then a White Rabbit runs past wearing a waistcoat and checking a pocket watch, and Alice doesn't think *that's impossible* — she thinks *how interesting,* and she follows him.

This is the first secret of the book: Alice is not amazed by Wonderland. She is *annoyed* by it. She tries to apply logic to a world that has none, and the comedy comes from how earnestly she keeps trying. The Caterpillar is rude. The Queen of Hearts is insufferable. The Mad Hatter is hosting a tea party he cannot leave because he had a quarrel with Time itself. Alice treats all of this the way a sensible nine-year-old girl would: with polite exasperation, mounting indignation, and the occasional burst of genuine tears.

That's what the summaries never tell you. Carroll's Wonderland isn't dreamy and soft. It's *sharp*. The dialogue crackles. The logic is inverted but internally consistent — a kind of mathematics of nonsense, where "I mean what I say" and "I say what I mean" are treated as genuinely different propositions, and the difference matters. You will read an argument between Alice and the Cheshire Cat and realize, slightly uncomfortably, that the Cat is winning.

The other thing they don't tell you: the book is funny. Not whimsically funny, not fairy-tale funny — funny in the way that makes you read a line aloud to whoever is nearby. The Mock Turtle, who is sad because he used to be a real turtle. The Duchess, whose only moral is that everything has one. The trial, which proceeds in exactly the way trials feel when you are the one who has done nothing wrong.

And underneath all of it — underneath the wordplay and the madness and the creatures who cannot give a straight answer — there is something genuinely strange and true. Alice keeps changing size. She keeps forgetting who she is. She stands in a hall full of locked doors, too large, then too small, then weeping a lake of her own tears and swimming through it. Carroll wrote this for a real child, on a real afternoon, rowing a boat. But he also, somehow, wrote it about the experience of being a mind inside a world that doesn't quite fit.

You don't need to have loved it as a child. You need to have been curious, and occasionally annoyed, and confident that you are making sense even when you aren't.

Open it. The Rabbit is already gone.
