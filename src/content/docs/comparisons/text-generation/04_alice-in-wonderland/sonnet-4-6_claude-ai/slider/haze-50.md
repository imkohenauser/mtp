There's a story that might have come from a dream — and like a dream, you remember it sideways, in colors and feelings rather than plot.

A girl falls. Not dramatically, not screaming. She just *falls*, down and down through a rabbit hole, past shelves of marmalade and clocks that may or may not matter, until she lands somewhere that never quite explains itself.

Wonderland isn't an adventure in the way we usually mean. There's no map, no villain with a plan, no prophecy that names Alice the chosen one. What Carroll seems to be doing — and this might be reading too much in, or too little — is writing a book that thinks the way a child thinks. Not simply, but *associatively*. One thing leads to another not because it must, but because it sort of rhymes with it, or contradicts it, or reminds you of something else entirely.

Alice herself is marvelous. She's curious without being reckless, polite without being a pushover. She keeps trying to apply the rules she knows to a world that has quietly decided rules are suggestions. She never stops being herself, even as everything around her tries to make her doubt who that is.

That question floats through the whole book, loosely: *Who are you?* The caterpillar asks it. The mushroom implies it. The growing and shrinking and changing and being told she's wrong about her own memories — it all circles back to the same soft uncertainty. Not terrifying. Just... hovering.

Lewis Carroll wrote this for a real girl named Alice, on a boat, one summer afternoon in 1862. It was a story told out loud before it was ever written down, which might be why it still feels spoken somehow — like something meant to be heard in a voice you almost recognize.

You could read it in two hours. You might find yourself thinking about it for considerably longer.
