That's where it begins — not with a quest, not with a villain's scheme, not with a war or a prophecy or a hero who has been chosen. Just a girl, bored on a riverbank, who sees a white rabbit pull a pocket watch from his waistcoat and run past muttering that he's late, and instead of doing the sensible thing, she follows him down a hole.

What Lewis Carroll understood, in 1865, is that the most terrifying logic is the logic of dreams. Wonderland doesn't disobey rules. It obeys *its* rules, perfectly, with complete sincerity — and the rules simply have nothing to do with the world you came from. A baby becomes a pig. A cat becomes nothing but a grin. A queen sentences everyone to death, and everyone is very polite about it. At a tea party that never ends because time has been offended, Alice is offered wine and then told there isn't any.

"But then why offer it?" she asks.

Nobody answers. The conversation has already moved on.

Carroll wasn't writing a children's book, exactly. He was writing a book for a child named Alice Liddell, yes, and it has children in it, and talking animals, and an illustrated edition. But the thing underneath — the thing that made it last a hundred and fifty years — is something closer to philosophy, or terror, or a joke that keeps changing shape the longer you think about it. Who *is* Alice? Wonderland keeps asking her. She keeps trying to answer and finding she isn't sure anymore. She's grown too big. She's shrunk too small. She started the day knowing her multiplication tables and now she can't remember if cats eat bats or bats eat cats, and both seem equally possible.

The book is short. You could read it in an afternoon. It doesn't waste time on backstory or world-building in the modern sense — Wonderland explains itself by refusing to explain itself, and somehow that works better. You understand the Queen of Hearts immediately and completely without being told a single thing about her history, because you've met her. You've met the Cheshire Cat, who gives directions that are technically true and completely useless. You've met the Caterpillar on his mushroom, smoking, asking "Who are you?" in a way that makes the question feel genuinely hard.

And then there's Alice herself, who is the best part. She is brave in the way that only bewildered people can be brave — not because she's fearless, but because she keeps going anyway. She argues back. She corrects people's grammar. When the Queen shouts "Off with her head!" Alice says, quietly, that it's nonsense, and the court shuffles uncertainly. She is seven years old and she does not flinch, even when she probably should.

The story ends, technically, with a trial. There are playing-card soldiers and stolen tarts and the Knave of Hearts who may or may not have eaten them. Alice by this point has eaten enough mushroom to be eleven feet tall, and she tells the whole court that they're nothing but a pack of cards, and they rise up and fly at her face, and she wakes up on the riverbank with leaves in her hair.

Her sister brushes them away and calls her in for tea.

And there's this moment — a very small moment, easy to miss — where the sister sits alone after Alice runs off, and half-dreams of Wonderland herself. Hears the creatures, sees the pool of tears, feels the whole ridiculous backwards world of it.
