## The Book Where Logic Goes to Die

Here is what you need to know about *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*: it is not a children's book that adults can also enjoy. It is a book about the complete, spectacular failure of reason — written by a mathematician who loved reason more than almost anything — and it is one of the strangest, funniest, most quietly terrifying things ever put on paper.

Alice falls. That's how it begins. Not in a dramatic way, not screaming — she falls slowly, noticing things on the walls of the rabbit hole, wondering if she'll come out in Australia, wondering what latitude and longitude are, wondering whether cats eat bats or bats eat cats, and falling asleep mid-thought. She falls for so long she's already half-bored by the time she lands. This is the first signal that Carroll is doing something no one had done before: the adventure begins with a child who is *unimpressed by the extraordinary* and instead uses it as an opportunity to think very carefully about very small things.

What she encounters at the bottom is not fantasy in the usual sense. There are no dragons, no prophecies, no chosen-one destiny. Instead there is a world governed entirely by *rules* — rules that are real, internal, and completely consistent — and which nevertheless produce total chaos. The Mad Hatter's tea party is not mad because the Hatter is irrational. It is mad because he is *too* rational: time stopped at six o'clock, so they move around the table forever to use clean cups, because that is the logical solution to the problem of no clean cups. The Queen of Hearts doesn't contradict herself. She applies her one rule — *Off with their heads!* — with perfect consistency. The Caterpillar doesn't give Alice confusing answers. He gives her *precise* answers that simply refuse to connect to the questions she's actually asking.

Carroll was a logician. He spent his professional life at Oxford writing treatises on formal logic. And what he understood, maybe better than anyone, is that logic without *shared premises* is just elaborate nonsense. Wonderland is a place where everyone is following the rules perfectly — and none of the rules are the same rules. Alice spends the entire book being told she's wrong, by creatures who are, by their own internal lights, entirely right.

This is why the book has survived. Not because of the imagery (though the imagery is extraordinary — there is a grin that remains after the cat has vanished, which is one of the great images in all of English literature). Not because of the wordplay (though Carroll's puns and portmanteaus are things of violent genius). It survives because it captures something true about how it *feels* to be a person applying sense to a world that isn't obliged to make any.

You know that feeling? You're in a meeting, or an argument, or a bureaucratic process, and everyone is following the rules, and nothing makes sense, and the harder you try to reason your way through it the more lost you become, and you can feel your grip on what is real beginning to very slightly slip? Carroll named that feeling in 1865. He named it with a seven-year-old girl in a blue dress who keeps trying to be polite.

There are books you read for plot. There are books you read for beauty. *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* you read to be ambushed — to discover, somewhere between the Mock Turtle's weeping and the trial where the evidence is a poem that doesn't mean anything, that someone got there before you. Someone looked at the whole absurd apparatus of rules and logic and social performance, and laughed at it with such precision and such love that the laughter has not stopped echoing.

It will take you an afternoon. It will stay with you for decades.
