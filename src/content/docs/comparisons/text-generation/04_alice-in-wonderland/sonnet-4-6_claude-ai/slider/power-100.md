There is only one reason you haven't read *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*: you think you already know it.

You don't.

You know the images — the white rabbit, the tea party, the cards. You know the Disney softening, the merchandise, the aesthetic. What you don't know is the book Lewis Carroll actually wrote in 1865, which is stranger, funnier, darker, and more electrically alive than any adaptation has dared to show you.

Here is what the book actually is: a seven-year-old girl falls into a world that operates on dream logic — and she refuses to be broken by it.

Wonderland is not whimsical. It is *hostile*. Every creature Alice meets is rude, dismissive, or actively trying to humiliate her. The Caterpillar interrogates her like a bored bureaucrat. The Duchess is violent. The Queen sentences people to death for sport. The Mad Hatter's tea party isn't charming — it's a trap, an endless loop of bad manners and nonsense designed to exhaust anyone who tries to make sense of it.

Alice makes sense of it anyway. That's the entire point.

She is not passive. She talks back. She corrects the Queen's grammar. She tells the court that they are nothing but a pack of cards. A small child, standing in a hall full of monsters with power over life and death, and she *argues* with them. Carroll wrote this in 1865 and gave us one of literature's most quietly radical protagonists: a girl who refuses to accept that absurd authority deserves her submission.

The language is the other thing no one warns you about. Carroll was a mathematician at Oxford, and the book is built on logic the way a proof is — except the logic is inverted, recursive, and hilarious. "You should say what you mean," the March Hare tells Alice. "I do," she replies, "at least I mean what I say — that's the same thing." "Not the same thing a bit!" The Hatter is right. Carroll makes you feel the difference in your bones. The jokes land because the reasoning is *precise* in its wrongness.

It takes two hours to read. Two hours, and you will have one of the strangest, sharpest, most quotable experiences in English literature sitting inside you permanently.

Read it before you read about it. Read it before you watch anything. Open the first page — *Alice was beginning to get very tired of sitting by her sister on the bank* — and don't stop until she wakes up.

You will feel, at the end, exactly like Alice coming back from a dream: certain something important just happened, certain the world looks slightly different, certain you want to go back.
