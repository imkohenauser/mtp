There's a girl who falls.

Not trips — *falls*. Through a rabbit hole, past shelves of marmalade and empty picture frames, tumbling so slowly she has time to wonder whether she'll come out the other side of the earth, where people walk upside down and call everything by different names.

She lands in a corridor lined with tiny doors.

This is where Lewis Carroll does something almost cruel: he gives Alice the key to a door she's too large to fit through, and a bottle labeled DRINK ME that makes her small — but then she's forgotten the key on the table, now impossibly high above her. The frustration is precise and dreamlike and *real*, the specific helplessness of being almost able to reach the thing you want. You know this feeling. You've had it. Carroll just gave it a location.

Wonderland isn't a happy place, incidentally. It's rude and illogical and full of creatures who interrupt, who ask unanswerable questions, who put you on trial before they've committed any crime. The Caterpillar smokes and demands to know who you are. The Cheshire Cat fades until there is nothing left but a smile, hanging in the air — which the book points out is the most remarkable thing, a grin without a cat, because you can imagine one much more easily than the other.

The Mad Hatter is having tea at a table that is always 6 o'clock. Time, you see, has stopped for him — permanently tea-time, permanently that particular minute — and Carroll drops this in almost casually, as if everyone knows what it means to be stuck at a moment you can never leave.

Every paragraph in this book is like that. Jokes that aren't jokes. Logic that holds together perfectly until you realize it has no floor. A queen who shouts "Off with their heads!" and means it, and somehow this is also funny.

Alice herself is the still point of the spinning world. She's curious and irritable and prone to quoting lessons incorrectly. She cries a lake of tears and then nearly drowns in it. She argues with a pigeon who refuses to believe she isn't a serpent. She keeps her composure through things that have no composure to keep.

You will read it and feel, page after page, the specific sensation of a dream where the rules keep shifting just before you learn them — and you will want to keep reading anyway, because Alice keeps going, and because Carroll writes with this lightness that conceals something stranger and darker underneath, the way a very calm person sometimes frightens you more than an agitated one.

It is 165 pages. You could read it in an afternoon.

You should.
