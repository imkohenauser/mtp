A girl named Alice is bored beside a riverbank one afternoon when a white rabbit in a waistcoat rushes past her, checks a pocket watch, and mutters that he is late. This is, on the surface, an unremarkable event. Alice follows him anyway. She always was too curious for her own good.

And when she lands — she is in a world that is not mad, exactly, but *wrong* in a very precise and specific way. Everything in Wonderland obeys its own internal logic. It's just that the logic belongs to someone else, someone who has never explained the rules, and never will.

Lewis Carroll was a mathematician, and it shows. Wonderland is not chaos. It is order seen from the wrong angle — like a reflection in water, correct in every detail but backwards in every way that matters. The Queen of Hearts sentences first and holds trials after. The Cheshire Cat disappears until only its smile remains, grinning at you from empty air. A Mad Hatter hosts a tea party frozen at a single moment in time because Time itself is having a quarrel and has stopped cooperating. You understand why all these things are *wrong*. What unsettles you is how perfectly they make sense *on their own terms*.

And Alice — Alice is the sanest person in the book, which turns out to be the loneliest thing imaginable. She is polite. She has been well-raised. She knows how conversations are supposed to go. Every creature she meets ignores these conventions completely, not from cruelty but from utter incomprehension that any other way exists. She argues with a caterpillar. She is insulted by a duchess. She plays croquet with a flamingo as a mallet and a hedgehog as the ball, and the hedgehog keeps wandering away. Nothing works. Nothing holds still. She grows too tall and then too small. Her identity itself begins to feel unreliable.

The genius of Carroll is that none of this is played as horror — though it could be. It is played as *comedy*, which is somehow more disturbing. Because you laugh, and then you stop, and you think: *wait.* You recognize the frustration of adults who don't explain their rules. You recognize the disorientation of being a child in a world built for other people. You recognize the particular madness of social conventions — tea parties, trials, games — conducted with tremendous seriousness for reasons no one can quite justify.

And you realize that Carroll wasn't writing about a fantasy world.

He was writing about *this* one.

---

**Conclusion:** Read *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* because no other book has ever made absurdity feel so precise, or sanity feel so strange. It is one hundred and sixty years old and it has not dated by a single afternoon. The rabbit hole is still open. The door is still your size. Everything that waits on the other side of it — the wonder, the frustration, the dream-logic, the disorientation, the grinning dark, and the girl who keeps her chin up through all of it — is still exactly as Lewis Carroll left it. In light of the above: follow the rabbit. You already want to.
