There is a girl who falls.

That's the whole story, really — a girl who falls, and keeps falling, and the world she lands in doesn't make sense, and she keeps asking it to, and it won't, and she keeps asking anyway. That's the whole story. Except the falling takes a very long time, long enough to notice the shelves of marmalade going past, long enough to wonder if she'll fall straight through the earth and come out somewhere where people walk upside down, which seems to her a geographical concern worth addressing.

The rabbits and the cards and the mad tea party — everyone tells you about those. What they don't tell you is that *Alice is annoyed.* Wonderland is not a dream sequence set to soft light; it is an argument she cannot win because nobody is following the rules, and she is nine years old and she *knows* the rules and she keeps citing them and the Cheshire Cat keeps grinning that grin from a branch and then disappearing, leaving only the grin, which continues to grin in the absence of a cat to attach itself to. This is — Lewis Carroll wants you to understand — technically, logically, grammatically possible.

The Hatter is having a tea party that never ends because he had a quarrel with Time, and Time stopped for him at six in the afternoon, and so they move around the table forever, always to a clean cup, never doing dishes. By the way, this is probably the saddest thing in the book. Carroll never announces this. He just lets it sit there on the page and moves on.

A queen who sentences first and verdicts later. A duchess who holds a baby that turns into a pig. A caterpillar sitting on a mushroom, smoking, asking *who are you?* which is the question Alice has been trying to answer the whole book without realizing she was being asked it. Who are you. She's gotten bigger and smaller, she's given wrong answers she thought were right, she's been perfectly sensible in a world that treats sensibility as a kind of rudeness. Who are you, the caterpillar asks. She doesn't quite know yet.

The logic, that's the thing nobody warns you about. Carroll was a mathematician. The nonsense is *rigorous.* When the Mad Hatter says there's no room at the table and Alice looks and the table is enormous and practically empty, that's not whimsy — that's a statement with an internal consistency you could argue about at length, and some people have. When the Red Queen explains that here you must run as fast as you can just to stay in the same place, Carroll did not know he was describing late capitalism, but here we are.

The trial at the end is a trial about a stolen tart. The jury fills in their names when the judge tells them to record the verdict. The verdict was decided before anyone spoke. Alice finally gets angry — actually angry, spine-straightening, voice-raising angry — and says the whole thing is nonsense, which it is, and then she wakes up, because that's what you do when you finally say the true thing out loud.

She was curious, that's what started it. She saw a rabbit with a waistcoat and a watch and she thought: *I wonder.* That's the whole engine. The whole book runs on wondering. You should read it, incidentally, not because it is strange, but because it is the most faithful account ever written of what it feels like to be small in a world built by and for someone else — to follow the rules and find them arbitrary, to ask sensible questions and get nonsense back, to be told you are the wrong size, the wrong age, in the wrong place, and to keep going anyway because the alternative is not going anywhere at all, and something might be at the end of this, something might make sense eventually, and wouldn't it be dreadful to stop just before finding out what it was.
