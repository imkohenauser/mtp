What if the best way to understand *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* isn't to know what happens — but to feel the particular vertigo of a world where the rules keep changing before you've learned what they were?

Alice falls down a rabbit hole. Simple enough. Except the hole takes so long to fall through that she begins to wonder, mid-fall, whether she'll come out the other side in Australia. She notices a jar of marmalade on the way down. She replaces it on a shelf — carefully, politely — because dropping it might hurt someone below. There is no one below. She doesn't know that. She's being reasonable in an unreasonable situation, which is exactly what the book is *about*, and we're barely past the first page.

There's a rabbit who's late. A caterpillar who asks "Who are you?" in a tone that implies Alice should already know, and be ashamed she doesn't. A queen who treats beheadings as punctuation. A Cheshire Cat who vanishes until only the smile remains — and that might be the most philosophically unsettling image in all of English literature. Carroll doesn't explain it. He just... leaves it there, grinning.

The book doesn't ask you to sympathize with Alice so much as *track* her. She argues with mushrooms. She corrects her own recitations and produces strange new poems instead. She grows enormous in a tiny room and floods it with her tears, then swims through her own weeping. She holds her own at a trial that is, structurally, a tea party.

What's strange is how *logical* it all is — or wants to be. Everyone in Wonderland is following some rule, just not the same rule as anyone else. The Mad Hatter and the March Hare have a perfectly coherent reason for always staying at the same point in tea time. The Queen of Hearts genuinely believes the beheadings are just. The problem isn't chaos — it's that *every* character has their own internal consistency, and they all insist Alice should already understand it.

Carroll was a mathematics lecturer at Oxford. He thought carefully about nonsense. There's a difference, his book implies, between *meaningless* and *differently meaningful*, and most of Wonderland falls into the second category.

So — does that make you want to read it to find the logic, or to find the dream? Or to find what it felt like to be seven years old and told that adults made sense, and to quietly suspect they didn't?
