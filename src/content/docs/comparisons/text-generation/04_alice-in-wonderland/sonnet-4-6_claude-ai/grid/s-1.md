What if I told you the most famous children's book ever written isn't really a children's book?

It's a fever dream. A riddle with no answer. A joke where you never find out what it means. Alice's Adventures in Wonderland by Lewis Carroll — published 1865, still unsolved — drops a perfectly ordinary seven-year-old girl into a world that runs on rules she can't find, logic she can't follow, a trial that decides guilt before evidence, a queen who sentences before verdict, a cat that leaves its smile behind when it goes. Alice falls down a rabbit hole. Fine. Then she grows enormous and shrinks and floods a room with her own tears and swims in them and the animals hold a committee meeting to get dry and the meeting accomplishes nothing. She meets a caterpillar on a mushroom smoking a hookah who demands she justify her existence. She goes to a tea party stuck at six o'clock forever because the host insulted Time and Time stopped cooperating. She plays croquet with flamingos for mallets and hedgehogs for balls and the wickets are soldiers who keep wandering off. Nothing works. Nothing is supposed to work. That's not a bug it's the whole point it's the condition of being alive and bewildered and seven years old in a world built by people who seem to know what they're doing but, upon examination, absolutely do not.

Carroll wrote this for a real girl, Alice Liddell, making it up aloud during a boat trip. She asked him to write it down. He did. And somehow what he wrote down was: authority is absurd, language is a trap, identity is unstable, logic is a weapon the powerful use on the powerless, and the correct response to all of this is to look the Queen of Hearts in the eye and say *you're nothing but a pack of cards.*

It's 90 pages. It will take you two hours. You will finish it and feel like you dreamed it. You'll want to read it again to catch what you missed. You will miss it again. That's the point. That is entirely the point.
