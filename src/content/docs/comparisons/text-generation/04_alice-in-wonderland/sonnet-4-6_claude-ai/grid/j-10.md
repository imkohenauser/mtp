There is a moment, somewhere near the beginning of *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*, where a little girl falls down a rabbit hole and passes a jar labeled **ORANGE MARMALADE** — which turns out to be empty, and she politely puts it back on a shelf as she falls, because it would be rude to drop it on someone below.

Lewis Carroll published this in 1865 and it has never been explained. Not really. People have tried — it's a dream, it's a drug trip, it's a satire of Victorian education, it's a mathematician's joke about formal logic. All of these are true and none of them are the point. The point is the feeling: the particular vertigo of being very small in a world that operates on rules you almost understand, rules that keep changing, rules that the people enforcing them seem to have forgotten the purpose of.

What Carroll understood — what no one has replicated since, not really — is that nonsense done *correctly* is not chaos. It has its own terrible grammar. The Queen of Hearts screams "Off with their heads!" not randomly but at precisely the moment that authority feels most cornered and most absurd. The Cheshire Cat doesn't give Alice bad advice; he gives her *perfectly accurate* advice that is completely useless. And she's right. She was always right. That was never the question.

The book is 96 pages. You can read it in two hours. But you will find yourself quoting it for the rest of your life, in situations you won't be able to explain, whenever the world goes strange and you need a word for what it's doing.
