Carroll published *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland* in 1865. A little girl falls down a rabbit hole and finds herself in a world where nothing behaves the way it should.

That is the plot. It is also almost entirely beside the point.

The rabbit hole is not a metaphor. The Cheshire Cat's grin does not symbolize anything in particular. The Queen of Hearts screaming "Off with their heads!" is not an allegory for political tyranny — or if it is, Carroll didn't care much. What the book actually is: a sequence of encounters, each more logically unhinged than the last, in which Alice — sensible, literal-minded, slightly exasperated — tries to apply the rules she knows to a world that has entirely different ones.

The Mad Hatter's tea party is stuck at six o'clock forever because he had an argument with Time. The Caterpillar sits on a mushroom smoking a hookah and asks Alice who she is, which she finds she cannot answer. A baby turns into a pig. A court tries a knave for stealing tarts using evidence that is obviously fabricated and procedures that are openly absurd, and everyone treats this as normal.

Carroll was a mathematics lecturer at Oxford. The nonsense is not random — it is precise. The jokes are about logic. The wordplay is exact. "Take some more tea." "I've had nothing yet, so I can't take more." "You mean you can't take *less*. It's very easy to take *more* than nothing." This is a valid point, technically. The book is full of valid points made by completely mad people in a world that runs on perfectly consistent rules — just not the ones Alice (or you) expects.

What makes you want to read it is this: it is very short, it is very funny, and it is stranger than you remember. Most people know the Disney version. The book is weirder, sharper, and darker around the edges. Alice is not passive. She argues back. She gets annoyed. She grows and shrinks and nearly drowns in her own tears and keeps going with the particular stubbornness of a child who believes the world ought to make sense, even when it clearly doesn't.

It takes about two hours to read. It was written in 1865 and has not aged, because nonsense doesn't age.
