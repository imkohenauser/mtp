Is this even the right question — *making* someone want to read *Alice in Wonderland*?

The book has been continuously in print for over 160 years. Children encounter it without being sold on it. Adults return to it without being persuaded. Maybe the pitch isn't needed.

And yet.

---

Here's what the story actually is: a seven-year-old girl falls down a rabbit hole and spends an afternoon in a world where nothing works the way it should. That's the whole plot. There is no villain to defeat, no lesson to learn, no love to find. Alice simply wanders — growing too big, shrinking too small, offending creatures she doesn't understand, following logic that goes nowhere, attending a tea party that never ends because the host broke his watch.

Carroll wrote it as a gift for a real child, Alice Liddell, told aloud on a summer boat trip in 1862. It reads that way — like someone making things up in real time, following a dream's grammar rather than a story's. The Red Queen runs faster to stay in the same place. The Cheshire Cat disappears until only its grin remains. The Mad Hatter declares it's always six o'clock because Time stopped speaking to him after a quarrel.

None of it means anything. All of it means something.

That's the thesis: a delightful nonsense book, a children's classic, a Victorian curiosity.

---

But here's the antithesis: *Alice* is also the most precise portrait ever written of what it feels like to be a child in an adult world.

Every adult Alice meets speaks with absolute authority about things that make no sense. They issue rules she cannot follow, correct her for violations she didn't know existed, and look at her with contempt for her confusion. The Queen of Hearts sentences people to beheading before the verdict. The Caterpillar demands she explain herself while smoking and offering no explanation of his own. The Duchess dispenses morals that don't fit the situation. Everyone acts as though their private logic is universal law.

Alice, to her immense credit, keeps arguing back. She insists things don't make sense when they don't. She refuses to be entirely diminished. And when the whole edifice finally collapses — when the Queen shrieks "Off with their heads!" at the shuffling card soldiers — Alice says, clearly and at last: *"You're nothing but a pack of cards."*

And she wakes up.

The real problem Carroll was solving wasn't *how do I entertain a child* but *how do I take a child's experience seriously without condescending to it?* His answer: build a world from the inside out, where the child's perception — that adult authority is arbitrary, that social rules are absurd, that the world is deeply, vertiginously strange — is simply true. Not a metaphor. Just true.

That's why it has lasted. Not because it's whimsical. Because it's honest.

Read it — or reread it — and notice that it isn't the story you remember. It's sharper, and funnier, and stranger, and lonelier. Alice is more competent than you think, and more isolated. Wonderland is more hostile than it seems, and more seductive. And the rabbit hole, when you fall down it again, is deeper than it was the first time.
