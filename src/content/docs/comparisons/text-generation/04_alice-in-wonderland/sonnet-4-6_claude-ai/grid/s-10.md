There is a girl who falls.

Not forward, not sideways — *down*, in the way that only happens in dreams and the truest kind of stories, where the falling takes so long you begin to furnish the walls with clocks and marmalade jars and the reasonable question of whether you'll come out the other side, or simply arrive somewhere else entirely.

She follows a rabbit in a waistcoat. She follows it because she can. Because something in her — maybe the same something that lives in you — finds a hole in the ordinary day and leans over the edge just a little too far.

What waits below isn't danger, exactly. It might be danger. It tends to wear the shape of dinner parties where no one agrees what the rules are, of queens who mean exactly what they say and nothing they mean, of creatures who speak in riddles not to confuse you but because that's simply how the truth comes out when it's very old and very strange.

There's a Caterpillar on a mushroom, asking *who are you*, and the question lands differently than it should. There's tea perpetually being poured at a table set for someone who left. There's a cat that disappears and leaves only its smile behind — which might be the most honest thing in Wonderland, a smile without its source, floating where the warmth was.

Alice grows larger and then smaller and then large again. She is always too much or not enough for the room she's in. She argues with things that shouldn't talk and fails to argue with things that absolutely should be stopped. She maintains her manners through circumstances that do not deserve them. She is, in this way, both absurd and heroic.

The trial at the end is a trial of nothing, conducted by people with no authority over anything, and yet it feels — it really does feel — like something important is being decided.

Lewis Carroll wrote this story for a real girl on a real river on a real afternoon in 1862, and somehow — through some alchemy that prose poetry can't quite explain — it became a place. A geography. Somewhere you can go when the world insists too loudly on making sense.

You've been there before, probably. In the hour before sleep or the minute after something bewildering. You recognized the hedgehog-mallets and the paint-by-numbers roses without ever having read a word of it.

Reading it, you finally get to *stay* for a while.
