English is a West Germanic language that developed in Britain after Germanic-speaking peoples settled there in the early Middle Ages. Its history is usually divided into four main phases: Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English.

**1. Old English**
English began after the 5th century CE, when Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related Germanic groups migrated to Britain. Their dialects formed Old English.

Old English was close to other Germanic languages, with complex grammar, grammatical gender, case endings, and vocabulary very different from modern English. Words such as `house`, `bread`, `water`, `strong`, `father`, and `night` come from this Germanic base.

Latin also influenced Old English through Christianity, especially after the mission of Augustine in 597 CE. Words connected to religion, learning, and administration entered the language.

**2. Norse Influence**
From the 8th to 11th centuries, Vikings from Scandinavia settled in parts of Britain. Old Norse strongly affected English, especially in northern and eastern England.

Common words such as `sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `take`, `they`, `their`, and `them` reflect Norse influence. Norse also helped simplify English grammar because Old English and Old Norse speakers needed practical communication between related but different languages.

**3. Middle English**
The Norman Conquest of 1066 changed English deeply. The ruling elite spoke Norman French, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English survived as the language of ordinary people, but absorbed thousands of French words.

This is why English often has Germanic everyday words and French or Latin formal equivalents:

- `ask` / `question`
- `kingly` / `royal`
- `freedom` / `liberty`
- `cow` / `beef`
- `pig` / `pork`

During Middle English, grammar simplified further. Many Old English endings disappeared, word order became more important, and the language became more recognizable to modern readers. Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is the major literary example.

**4. Early Modern English**
From roughly 1500 to 1700, English changed into a form much closer to today’s language. The Great Vowel Shift altered pronunciation, especially long vowels, which helps explain why English spelling often does not match pronunciation.

The printing press, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms. The Renaissance brought many Latin and Greek words into English, especially in science, philosophy, medicine, and literature.

Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong to this period and helped shape English style and vocabulary.

**5. Modern English**
From the 18th century onward, English spread globally through British trade, colonization, science, and later American political, economic, and cultural power.

Modern English absorbed words from many languages, including Hindi, Arabic, Spanish, Dutch, French, Indigenous American languages, and many others. Examples include `shampoo`, `coffee`, `canyon`, `yacht`, `tomato`, and `kangaroo`.

**Summary**
English began as a Germanic language in early medieval Britain. It was reshaped by Norse contact, transformed by French after the Norman Conquest, expanded by Latin and Greek during the Renaissance, and globalized through empire, migration, commerce, science, and media. Its modern character comes from this layered history: Germanic grammar and core vocabulary, large French and Latin word stock, and worldwide borrowing.
