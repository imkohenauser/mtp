English is less a single language with a clean birth certificate than a long argument between peoples, invasions, trade, class, empire, printing, and convenience.

Start before “English”: its deep ancestor is **Proto-Indo-European**, the reconstructed language family behind Latin, Greek, Sanskrit, Celtic, Germanic, Slavic, and many others. One branch became **Proto-Germanic**, spoken in northern Europe. From that came the West Germanic languages: relatives of modern German, Dutch, Frisian, and English. Frisian is often mentioned because it preserves some similarities to early English, though modern English has wandered very far.

The usual historical starting point is the **5th century**, after Roman power withdrew from Britain. Germanic-speaking groups, traditionally called the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**, migrated into Britain from what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and nearby regions. Their dialects became **Old English**. The word “English” itself comes from the **Angles**: *Englisc*, “language of the Angles,” and *England*, “land of the Angles.”

Old English was not “Shakespeare but older.” It was a heavily inflected Germanic language, closer in feel to Old Frisian or Old High German than to modern English. A sentence from *Beowulf* can look almost foreign to present-day readers. It had grammatical gender, case endings, stronger verb inflections, and a core vocabulary that still survives in basic words: *house, bread, night, wife, child, king, water, earth, speak, come, go*.

Then came Christianity. From the late 6th century onward, Christian missions brought **Latin** vocabulary into English: words connected to religion, learning, writing, and institutions. Some Latin had already entered through contact with the Roman world, but Christianization deepened it. Words like *school, priest, monk, candle,* and *altar* belong to that broad layer.

A major disruption came with the **Vikings**. From the late 8th century onward, Norse-speaking settlers arrived, especially in northern and eastern England, the region known as the **Danelaw**. Old Norse and Old English were related Germanic languages, close enough for contact but different enough to reshape vocabulary and grammar. English borrowed everyday Norse words such as *sky, egg, window, take, give, they, them,* and *their*. That last set is important: even pronouns changed. Some scholars think Norse contact helped erode Old English’s case endings because speakers of related dialects needed simpler shared forms.

Then 1066: the **Norman Conquest**. William of Normandy defeated Harold at Hastings, and England’s ruling class became French-speaking. For centuries, England had a social split: English among common people, Norman French and later Anglo-French in law, court, administration, and aristocratic culture, with Latin still powerful in church and scholarship.

This is why English has so many double vocabularies:

- Germanic: *kingly*  
- French: *royal*  
- Latin: *regal*

Or:

- *ask* from Old English  
- *question* from French/Latin  
- *interrogate* from Latin

The Norman period led into **Middle English**, roughly 1100-1500. Grammar simplified. Case endings weakened. Word order became more important. French vocabulary flooded in: *court, judge, prison, government, beauty, color, peace, army, beef, pork, mutton*. Incidentally, the animal/meat distinction often reflects class contact: peasants tended the *cow, pig,* and *sheep*; elites ate *beef, pork,* and *mutton*, from French.

By the time of **Chaucer** in the late 14th century, English had reasserted itself as a literary and administrative language, but it was no longer Old English. It was a hybrid: Germanic bones, French clothing, Latin pockets.

Then came **Early Modern English**, roughly 1500-1700. Three big things mattered.

First, the **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms, especially around London usage. Not completely, though; English spelling remained chaotic because pronunciation kept changing.

Second, the **Great Vowel Shift** transformed long vowel pronunciation. This is one reason English spelling feels so strange. Words were often written in older ways while their sounds moved. For example, the vowel in *time* used to sound more like “teem,” then shifted toward its modern pronunciation.

Third, the Renaissance brought a huge wave of **Latin and Greek** vocabulary: *democracy, philosophy, radius, species, temperature, education, encyclopedia*. Some people objected to these “inkhorn terms,” seeing them as pretentious imports. Another approach could have been a more Germanic English, but prestige and scholarship pulled English toward classical vocabulary.

Shakespeare, the King James Bible, maritime expansion, colonialism, science, commerce, and later industrialization all pushed English outward. From the 17th century onward, English became a global language through the British Empire, settler colonies, slavery, trade, missionary activity, and later American economic and cultural power. That global history produced many Englishes: American, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, Caribbean, and many others. So “English” today is not only one standard language; it is a family of global varieties with different pronunciations, idioms, vocabularies, and political histories.

| Modern English | c. 1700-present | empire, science, global spread, standardization and variation |

Its prestige today is not proof of elegance or purity; it is evidence of history, force, adaptation, and accident.
