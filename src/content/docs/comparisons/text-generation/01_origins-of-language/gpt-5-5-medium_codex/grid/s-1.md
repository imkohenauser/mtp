English begins as a Germanic language, not a Romance language, even though today it is packed with Latin and French vocabulary. Its deepest ancestor is Proto-Indo-European, the prehistoric source of languages such as Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, Celtic, Germanic, and Slavic. From that came Proto-Germanic, then the West Germanic dialects spoken by peoples around the North Sea: Angles, Saxons, Jutes, Frisians. In the 5th century, after Roman power withdrew from Britain, these groups settled in what is now England. Their dialects became Old English. Hard, inflected, Germanic. Words like `house`, `bread`, `water`, `earth`, `wife`, `child`, `king`, `night`.

Old English, roughly 450-1100, was not “modern English with old spelling.” It had grammatical gender, case endings, stronger verb inflections, and a vocabulary much closer to German or Dutch. The epic *Beowulf* belongs here. Then came Viking settlement from the 8th to 11th centuries, bringing Old Norse. This mattered hugely: Norse gave English words like `sky`, `egg`, `window`, `take`, `they`, `their`, `them`; it also helped wear down complex endings because Norse and Old English speakers had related but different forms and needed practical mutual understanding. Contact simplified grammar. Trade, war, marriage, farming, daily life. Language got rubbed smooth.

Then 1066. The Norman Conquest. French-speaking rulers took over England, and for centuries England had a linguistic split: French for court, law, aristocracy; Latin for church and scholarship; English for ordinary life. This is why English has layered vocabulary: `kingly` from Old English, `royal` from French, `regal` from Latin. Same idea, different social register. Law gives us `court`, `judge`, `jury`, `crime`; government gives `parliament`, `council`, `tax`; cuisine gives `beef`, `pork`, `mutton`, while the animals remained Germanic: `cow`, `pig`, `sheep`. Power leaves words behind. Period.

Middle English, roughly 1100-1500, emerged from this collision. It lost many Old English endings, fixed word order more strongly, absorbed thousands of French terms, and became recognizable in writers like Chaucer. After that came Early Modern English, roughly 1500-1700: the printing press helped standardize spelling, London’s dialect gained prestige, the Renaissance poured in Latin and Greek words, and the Great Vowel Shift changed pronunciation dramatically. That is one reason English spelling is so strange: many spellings were fixed while pronunciations kept moving. `Name`, `time`, `house`, `food`, `blood` drifted in different ways. Spelling fossilized. Sound escaped.

Modern English then expanded with empire, science, trade, migration, slavery, colonization, printing, dictionaries, mass education, radio, film, the internet. It borrowed constantly: `pajamas` from Hindi/Urdu, `bungalow` from Gujarati/Hindi, `coffee` through Arabic and Turkish, `algebra` from Arabic, `piano` from Italian, `kindergarten` from German, `robot` from Czech, `tsunami` from Japanese. English became global not because it was “better,” but because British imperial power and later American economic, military, technological, and cultural power carried it outward.

So the short version: English is a West Germanic language born in early medieval Britain, reshaped by Norse contact, transformed by Norman French, enriched by Latin and Greek learning, standardized by print and education, and globalized by empire and modern media. Its history is not a straight line. It is invasion, contact, class, trade, sound change, spelling chaos, borrowing, simplification, expansion. A Germanic skeleton wearing a French-Latin coat, with pockets full of words from everywhere.
