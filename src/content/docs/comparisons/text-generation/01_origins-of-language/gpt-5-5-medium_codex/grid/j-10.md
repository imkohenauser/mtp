English began as a **West Germanic language** brought to Britain in the early Middle Ages, then developed through repeated contact with Celtic, Latin, Norse, French, and global languages.

**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**

Before English existed in Britain, most people spoke **Brittonic Celtic languages**, related to modern Welsh, Cornish, and Breton. From 43 CE, much of Britain was part of the Roman Empire, so **Latin** was used in government, the army, trade, and Christianity. Latin left some early place-name and vocabulary traces, but it did not become the everyday language of most people.

**2. Old English: Germanic settlement**

After Roman rule weakened in the 5th century, Germanic-speaking groups from the North Sea region settled in Britain: traditionally called the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**. Their dialects formed **Old English**, spoken roughly from the 5th century to 1066.

Old English was much more Germanic-looking than modern English. It had grammatical gender, case endings, stronger verb inflections, and vocabulary closer to Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and Old Norse. Words like *house, bread, water, earth, night, wife, child,* and *king* come from this Germanic core.

**3. Christian Latin influence**

From the late 6th century, Christian missionaries reintroduced Latin learning. This brought religious and scholarly vocabulary, including words connected with church life, education, writing, and administration. Words such as *school, priest, bishop,* and *minster* reflect this phase, sometimes directly from Latin, sometimes through earlier Germanic contact.

**4. Norse influence: Vikings and Danelaw**

From the late 8th century onward, Scandinavian settlers and raiders brought **Old Norse**, especially in northern and eastern England. Because Old English and Old Norse were related Germanic languages, their speakers could probably understand each other to some degree.

Norse influence was unusually deep. English borrowed everyday words such as *sky, egg, knife, window, they, them,* and *their*. The pronouns *they/them/their* are especially important: basic grammar, not just vocabulary, was affected. Norse contact may also have helped simplify English endings, because mixed communities benefited from a more streamlined grammar.

**5. Middle English: Norman Conquest and French**

The major turning point came in **1066**, when William of Normandy conquered England. For centuries afterward, the ruling elite used **Norman French**, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English survived as the language of the majority, but it absorbed enormous French influence.

This created many familiar layers in English vocabulary:

Germanic everyday core: *ask, kingly, house, work, freedom*  
French/Latin prestige terms: *question, royal, mansion, labor, liberty*

This is why English often has near-synonyms with different tones: *begin* vs. *commence*, *help* vs. *assist*, *holy* vs. *sacred*. Middle English also lost many Old English inflections, moving toward the word-order-based grammar we know today.

**6. Late Middle English and standardization**

By the 14th and 15th centuries, English had regained prestige. Writers like **Geoffrey Chaucer** showed that English could be a literary language. The dialect of London, influenced by the Midlands and by the political and commercial importance of the capital, became increasingly important.

The arrival of printing in England in **1476**, through William Caxton, helped stabilize spelling and written forms, though English spelling remained inconsistent because pronunciation kept changing.

**7. Early Modern English: Shakespeare, the Bible, and sound change**

From about 1500 to 1700, English entered its **Early Modern** period. This is the language of Shakespeare and the King James Bible. It is recognizable to modern readers but still contains older grammar and vocabulary.

A major pronunciation shift, the **Great Vowel Shift**, changed the sound of long vowels. This is one reason English spelling and pronunciation often do not match neatly. For example, spellings were becoming fixed while pronunciation was still moving.

During the Renaissance, English also borrowed heavily from **Latin and Greek**, especially for science, medicine, philosophy, and literature: *radius, species, philosophy, temperature, democracy,* and many others.

**8. Modern English: empire, science, and global spread**

From the 17th century onward, English spread through colonization, trade, migration, slavery, missionary activity, science, and later American economic and cultural power. It absorbed words from many languages: *tea* from Chinese, *shampoo* from Hindi, *kangaroo* from an Australian Aboriginal language, *sofa* from Arabic/Turkish/Persian routes, *boomerang, chocolate, tomato,* and many more.

Modern English also split into major regional standards: British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, and others. Today English is not one single uniform thing; it is a global language family with many national and local varieties.

In short: English is basically **Germanic in its grammatical and core vocabulary foundation**, deeply shaped by **Norse and French**, heavily enriched by **Latin and Greek**, and globally expanded through centuries of contact with languages around the world.
