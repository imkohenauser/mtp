The English language developed through several major historical layers rather than from one single source.

**1. Indo-European Roots**

English ultimately belongs to the **Indo-European** language family, the same broad family that includes Latin, Greek, Sanskrit, Persian, German, Russian, Hindi, Spanish, and many others.

Within that family, English is part of the **Germanic** branch, alongside German, Dutch, Frisian, Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, and Icelandic.

**2. Old English, c. 450-1100**

English began when Germanic-speaking peoples, especially the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**, migrated to Britain after the decline of Roman rule in the 5th century.

Their dialects formed what we call **Old English**.

Old English looked very different from modern English. A sentence like “The king gave the warrior a ring” might have appeared with more complex endings on nouns and adjectives, closer in structure to German or Latin than to modern English.

Important influences during this period included:

- **Celtic languages**, though surprisingly little Celtic vocabulary entered everyday English
- **Latin**, through Christianity and earlier Roman contact
- **Old Norse**, from Viking settlement, giving words such as `sky`, `egg`, `they`, `them`, `their`, `take`, and `law`

**3. Middle English, c. 1100-1500**

The biggest turning point came in **1066**, when the Normans conquered England. The ruling elite spoke **Norman French**, while ordinary people continued speaking English.

For several centuries, England was linguistically layered:

- Latin: church, scholarship, law
- French: court, government, aristocracy
- English: common speech

This is why English has many French-derived words, especially in law, government, food, art, and status:

- `court`, `judge`, `jury`, `parliament`
- `beef`, `pork`, `mutton`
- `beauty`, `honor`, `royal`

During Middle English, grammar also simplified. Many Old English case endings disappeared, and word order became more important. Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* belongs to this period.

**4. Early Modern English, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of **Shakespeare** and the **King James Bible**.

Several things changed English dramatically:

- The **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms.
- The **Great Vowel Shift** changed the pronunciation of long vowels, which helps explain why English spelling and pronunciation often seem mismatched.
- Renaissance learning brought many words from **Latin and Greek**, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.

Words such as `radius`, `species`, `temperature`, `democracy`, and `philosophy` reflect this learned vocabulary layer.

**5. Modern English, c. 1700-present**

Modern English expanded globally through the British Empire, trade, migration, science, technology, and later American cultural and economic influence.

It absorbed words from many languages, including:

- Hindi/Urdu: `shampoo`, `pajamas`, `bungalow`
- Arabic: `alcohol`, `sugar`, `coffee`
- Spanish: `canyon`, `patio`, `ranch`
- Indigenous American languages: `tomato`, `canoe`, `tobacco`
- Japanese: `tsunami`, `karaoke`, `emoji`

Today English is often described as a **Germanic language with a heavily mixed vocabulary**. Its core grammar and most basic words are Germanic: `mother`, `father`, `house`, `water`, `come`, `go`, `eat`, `sleep`. But much of its formal, academic, legal, and technical vocabulary comes from French, Latin, and Greek.

Another way to view English is as a language shaped by contact: invasion, trade, religion, empire, migration, print culture, and global media all left marks on it.
