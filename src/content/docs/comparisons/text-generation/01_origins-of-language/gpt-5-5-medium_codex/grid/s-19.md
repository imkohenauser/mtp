English began as a Germanic language brought to Britain in the 5th century CE by settlers from northwestern Europe, especially Angles, Saxons, and Jutes. Their dialects gradually replaced much of the Latin and Celtic speech used in lowland Britain after Roman rule ended. The word “English” itself comes from the Angles: *Englisc*, the speech of the Angles.

Old English, roughly 450-1100, was very different from modern English. It had grammatical gender, many case endings, and a vocabulary mostly Germanic. A sentence from *Beowulf* looks closer to German or Icelandic than to present-day English. During this period, Viking settlement added many Old Norse words, especially everyday ones like *sky*, *egg*, *take*, *they*, *their*, and *them*. Norse also helped simplify English grammar because related but different Germanic dialects were being used side by side.

The Norman Conquest of 1066 changed English deeply. The ruling class spoke Norman French, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English survived as the language of common life, but it absorbed thousands of French words: *court*, *judge*, *government*, *beef*, *beauty*, *royal*, *army*. This produced Middle English, roughly 1100-1500, the language of Chaucer. English grammar became simpler, word order became more important, and vocabulary became a layered mix: Germanic words for daily life, French words for law and culture, Latin words for learning.

From about 1500 onward, Early Modern English emerged. The printing press helped standardize spelling and grammar, especially around the London dialect. The Renaissance brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary: *education*, *democracy*, *temperature*, *philosophy*. Shakespeare and the King James Bible helped spread powerful models of English style, though they did not “create” modern English by themselves.

A major sound change also occurred between roughly 1400 and 1700: the Great Vowel Shift. Long vowels changed pronunciation, which is one reason English spelling often feels strange today. Words like *time*, *house*, and *name* were once pronounced more like their spellings suggest in older European languages.

Modern English expanded with British colonialism, trade, science, migration, and later American cultural and technological influence. It borrowed from hundreds of languages: *tea* from Chinese varieties, *shampoo* from Hindi, *algebra* from Arabic, *piano* from Italian, *kindergarten* from German, *robot* from Czech. At the same time, English developed many regional forms: American, British, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, Caribbean, and many others.

So English is Germanic at its core, heavily reshaped by Norse and French, enriched by Latin and Greek, and globalized through empire, commerce, media, and technology. Its history is less a straight line than a series of contacts: warriors, monks, merchants, printers, colonizers, migrants, broadcasters, programmers...
