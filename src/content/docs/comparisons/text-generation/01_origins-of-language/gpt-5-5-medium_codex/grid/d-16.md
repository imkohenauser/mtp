**1. Before English: Celtic Britain And Rome**

Before English existed, Britain was mainly home to Celtic languages related to modern Welsh, Cornish, and Gaelic. From 43 CE, much of Britain was ruled by Rome, so Latin entered administration, religion, trade, and urban life. But Latin did not replace local Celtic speech across the island.

**2. Old English, c. 450-1100**

English began with the arrival of Germanic groups usually identified as Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related peoples from areas of present-day northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

Their dialects formed **Old English**, also called **Anglo-Saxon**. It was a Germanic language, much closer in structure to Old High German or Old Norse than to present-day English. It had grammatical gender, many inflections, and freer word order.

Examples of Old English vocabulary still basic today include:

- `mann` → man
- `wif` → wife
- `hus` → house
- `cyning` → king
- `eorþe` → earth

Christianization from the late 6th century brought many Latin religious and scholarly words. Everyday words such as `sky`, `egg`, `they`, `their`, `them`, `take`, and `get` reflect Scandinavian contact.

**3. Middle English, c. 1100-1500**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 changed English profoundly. The new ruling elite spoke Norman French, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English continued as the language of the majority, but for centuries it existed alongside French and Latin.

This produced a huge influx of French vocabulary, especially in government, law, war, fashion, food, and culture:

**4. Early Modern English, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of Shakespeare, the King James Bible, and the Renaissance. It is mostly readable today, though spelling, pronunciation, and some grammar differ.

Several forces shaped this period:

- The **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms.
- The **Great Vowel Shift** changed the pronunciation of long vowels, creating many gaps between English spelling and pronunciation.
- Renaissance scholarship brought many Latin and Greek words into English, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.

Words such as `temperature`, `encyclopedia`, `democracy`, `species`, and `atmosphere` reflect this learned borrowing.

**The Big Pattern**

English began as a Germanic language, but its development was unusually shaped by contact. Its core grammar and most basic everyday words are Germanic. Its legal, political, literary, and learned vocabulary is heavily French, Latin, and Greek. Its modern global forms reflect centuries of migration, empire, trade, education, media, and technology.

That is why English feels hybrid: structurally Germanic, lexically mixed, historically layered, and globally diversified.
