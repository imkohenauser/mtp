From that broad family came the **Germanic** branch, and from West Germanic came the dialects that would eventually become English. Old English was highly inflected, with grammatical gender, case endings, stronger verb classes, and word forms that make it look closer to German or Icelandic than to modern English. A sentence from Old English is often barely intelligible to a modern speaker.

Then came the first great reshaping: **Christianization and Latin literacy**. From the late 6th century onward, Latin entered English through the Church, education, manuscripts, theology, and administration. This did not replace English, but it gave it a learned vocabulary and connected it to European intellectual culture. Words connected with religion, learning, and institutional life often came through Latin or Latin-mediated channels.

The next major force was **Old Norse**, brought by Viking settlement from the 8th to 11th centuries. Norse and Old English were related Germanic languages, close enough for contact but different enough to cause simplification and borrowing. English took in words such as *sky, egg, law, window, take, give, they, their,* and *them*. The borrowing of pronouns is especially revealing: languages do not usually borrow core grammar casually. It suggests deep bilingual contact, intermarriage, and everyday coexistence.

The most dramatic rupture came in **1066**, with the Norman Conquest. For centuries afterward, England had a French-speaking ruling elite, a Latin-writing Church and bureaucracy, and an English-speaking majority. By around 1100-1500, English had become **Middle English**. Its grammar simplified sharply: many case endings weakened or disappeared, word order became more important, and the language became less synthetic and more analytic. Chaucer’s English, from the late 14th century, is recognizably ancestral to ours, though still foreign in spelling, pronunciation, and many forms. The transition to **Early Modern English** came roughly from 1500 to 1700. Several forces converged. The **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped stabilize spelling and spread London-based forms. English became not merely a national language but a world language, though that global status came through unequal power as much as through literary or practical merit.

So the history of English is not a straight line from “simple old language” to “modern global language.” It is a record of contact. Its basic grammar and oldest vocabulary are Germanic; its elite, legal, administrative, and literary vocabulary was heavily French; its scholarly and technical registers are deeply Latin and Greek; its modern global vocabulary is imperial and cosmopolitan. On the surface, English looks irregular because it is messy.
