**Origins and Development of English**

English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain by Anglo-Saxon settlers in the 5th century CE. These groups, mainly Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, came from areas of present-day Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

**Old English: c. 450-1100**

Old English was heavily Germanic. It had complex grammar, grammatical gender, and many inflections. Works such as *Beowulf* were written in this period.

Major influences:
- Latin, through Christianity
- Norse, through Viking settlement
- Celtic languages, though their influence on English vocabulary was limited

**Middle English: c. 1100-1500**

After the Norman Conquest in 1066, French became the language of government, law, and the elite in England. English absorbed thousands of French words, especially in law, politics, art, food, and social rank.

Old English grammar simplified during this period. Word order became more important as inflections weakened.

Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is a major Middle English work.

**Early Modern English: c. 1500-1700**

This period saw major standardization. The printing press, introduced by William Caxton in 1476, helped spread London-based written English.

The Great Vowel Shift changed English pronunciation significantly. This is one reason English spelling often does not match modern pronunciation.

William Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong to this period and helped shape English style and vocabulary.

**Modern English: c. 1700-present**

English expanded through British colonialism, trade, science, and later American political, economic, and cultural influence.

It borrowed words from many languages, including Hindi, Arabic, Spanish, Indigenous American languages, and African languages.

Today, English is a global language with many major varieties, including British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, and Singaporean English. Its history is defined by Germanic roots, heavy French and Latin influence, simplified grammar, and large-scale borrowing.
