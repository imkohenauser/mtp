Is “English” one continuous language, or a series of rebuilt languages sharing a name?

**Thesis: English Developed Through Layering**

English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain in the 5th century by Anglo-Saxon settlers from areas around modern northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands. Their dialects became **Old English**, a language much closer to German than to modern English. Words like `house`, `bread`, `water`, `earth`, `man`, and `wife` come from this Germanic core.

From the 8th to 11th centuries, Viking settlement added **Old Norse** influence, especially in northern and eastern England. Common words such as `sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `they`, `them`, and `their` reflect this Scandinavian contact.

The biggest transformation came after the **Norman Conquest of 1066**. French-speaking Normans ruled England, so French became the language of law, government, aristocracy, and high culture. English survived among the broader population but absorbed thousands of French and Latin-derived words: `court`, `judge`, `government`, `beauty`, `beef`, `royal`, `crime`.

By around 1150-1500, this produced **Middle English**, the language of Chaucer. Grammar simplified: many Old English case endings disappeared, word order became more important, and vocabulary became heavily mixed.

From about 1500 onward, **Early Modern English** emerged. The printing press, Renaissance learning, exploration, and Shakespeare’s era expanded vocabulary massively. Latin and Greek supplied learned words like `democracy`, `philosophy`, `temperature`, and `education`. During this period, pronunciation also shifted dramatically in the **Great Vowel Shift**, which helps explain why English spelling often looks older than its pronunciation.

By the 18th and 19th centuries, dictionaries, grammar books, empire, trade, science, and later American influence helped create **Modern English** as a global language.

**Antithesis: English Is Less a Lineage Than a Contact Language**

Suppose the opposite: English did not simply “develop” from Old English in a neat family-tree line. Instead, it was repeatedly disrupted by conquest, migration, class division, literacy, empire, and technology.

From this view, English is historically unusual because its basic grammar remains Germanic, but its vocabulary is extraordinarily hybrid. Everyday words are often Germanic: `eat`, `sleep`, `child`, `go`, `come`. Formal, legal, academic, and literary words are often French, Latin, or Greek: `consume`, `repose`, `infant`, `proceed`, `arrive`.

That is why English often has layered synonyms:

- Germanic: `ask`
- French: `question`
- Latin: `interrogate`

Or:

- Germanic: `kingly`
- French: `royal`
- Latin: `regal`

So the better framing may be: English is not just “descended” from Anglo-Saxon. It is a Germanic language that survived centuries of pressure, absorbed its conquerors’ vocabulary, simplified much of its grammar, and then globalized through print, empire, science, migration, and media. Its history is less like a straight river and more like a language repeatedly remade while keeping its oldest foundations.
