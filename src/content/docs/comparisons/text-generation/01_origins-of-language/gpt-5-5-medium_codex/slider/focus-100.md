English is a **West Germanic language** that developed in Britain after Germanic-speaking peoples settled there in the fifth century CE. Its history is usually divided into four major stages: **Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English**.

**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**

Before English existed in Britain, much of the island was inhabited by speakers of **Celtic languages**, ancestors of Welsh, Cornish, and Gaelic. From 43 CE, much of Britain was part of the Roman Empire, so **Latin** was used in administration, the army, trade, and Christianity.

Latin left some traces, but the basic structure of English did not come from Latin or Celtic. English began mainly with Germanic settlers.

**2. Old English: Germanic Origins, c. 450-1100**

After Roman authority weakened in Britain in the early fifth century, groups from the North Sea coast migrated to Britain, especially the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**. They spoke closely related **West Germanic dialects**.

These dialects became **Old English**, also called **Anglo-Saxon**.

Old English was much closer to modern German or Dutch than to present-day English. It had:

| Feature | Old English |
|---|---|
| Grammar | many case endings |
| Word order | more flexible than modern English |
| Vocabulary | mostly Germanic |
| Examples | *cyning* “king”, *hus* “house”, *mann* “person/man” |

The name **English** comes from the **Angles**: *Englisc* meant the language of the Angles, and *Englaland* later became England.

Old English was also influenced by:

| Source | Influence |
|---|---|
| Latin | church, learning, administration: *school*, *priest*, *monk* |
| Old Norse | Viking settlement: *sky*, *egg*, *take*, *they*, *their*, *them* |

The Norse influence was especially important because Old Norse and Old English were related Germanic languages. Contact between speakers likely helped simplify some grammar endings.

**3. Middle English: Norman French Influence, c. 1100-1500**

The biggest turning point was the **Norman Conquest of 1066**. William of Normandy conquered England, and the ruling elite spoke **Norman French**, while Latin remained important in church and scholarship.

For several centuries, England had a layered language society:

| Domain | Main Language |
|---|---|
| common people | English |
| aristocracy and law | French |
| church and scholarship | Latin |

This transformed English vocabulary. Thousands of French words entered English, especially in government, law, fashion, cuisine, art, and social rank:

| French-derived words | Native Germanic equivalents |
|---|---|
| royal | kingly |
| beef | cow |
| pork | pig |
| justice | law |

During Middle English, English grammar also became simpler. Many Old English case endings disappeared, and word order became more fixed.

The best-known Middle English writer is **Geoffrey Chaucer**, whose *Canterbury Tales* shows English becoming a major literary language again.

**4. Early Modern English: Standardization and Expansion, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of **Shakespeare** and the **King James Bible**. It is recognizably English, though still different from today’s form.

For example, words like *name*, *time*, *house*, and *mouse* were once pronounced more like their spellings in older European-style vowel systems.

**Core Summary**

English began as a **Germanic language** brought to Britain by Anglo-Saxon settlers. Later, printing, the Renaissance, colonial expansion, and global trade turned it into a standardized and then worldwide language.
