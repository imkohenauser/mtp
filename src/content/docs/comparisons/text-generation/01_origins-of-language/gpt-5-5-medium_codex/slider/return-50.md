**Thesis: English Is a Layered Hybrid**

English developed through repeated contact, conquest, trade, and cultural prestige. Its core began as a Germanic language, but its modern form is the result of many historical layers.

The earliest roots lie in **Proto-Indo-European**, the reconstructed ancestor of many European and South Asian languages. One branch became **Proto-Germanic**, which later split into languages including Old Norse, Gothic, German, Dutch, and English.

Around the **5th century CE**, Germanic-speaking peoples, mainly the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**, migrated to Britain after Roman authority weakened. Their dialects formed **Old English**, a strongly inflected Germanic language. Words such as *house*, *water*, *bread*, *stone*, *father*, and *mother* reflect this foundation.

From the **8th to 11th centuries**, Viking settlement brought **Old Norse** influence, especially in northern and eastern England. Norse contributed everyday words such as *sky*, *egg*, *knife*, *they*, *their*, and *them*. It also helped simplify grammar because related Germanic speakers needed to communicate across dialects.

The greatest shift came after the **Norman Conquest of 1066**. Norman French became the language of government, law, aristocracy, and high culture, while English remained the language of most common people. Over several centuries, English absorbed thousands of French words: *court*, *judge*, *parliament*, *beauty*, *royal*, *beef*, and *mutton*. This created many English doublets: Germanic *kingly* beside French *royal*, or Germanic *ask* beside French *question*.

By roughly **1100-1500**, English had become **Middle English**. Its grammar was simpler than Old English, word order became more important, and its vocabulary was heavily mixed. Geoffrey Chaucer’s writing in the 1300s belongs to this period.

From about **1500 onward**, **Early Modern English** emerged. The printing press, wider literacy, the Renaissance, and renewed interest in Latin and Greek brought many scholarly words: *education*, *democracy*, *philosophy*, *temperature*, *species*. Shakespeare and the King James Bible helped stabilize and popularize many forms of English.

A major sound change, the **Great Vowel Shift**, also transformed pronunciation between roughly the 1400s and 1700s. This is one reason English spelling often looks older than its pronunciation. For example, many long vowels changed sound while spellings stayed relatively fixed.

From the **17th century onward**, colonial expansion spread English globally. Contact with languages in North America, the Caribbean, South Asia, Africa, Australia, and elsewhere added words such as *tomato*, *canoe*, *bungalow*, *shampoo*, *kangaroo*, and *jungle*. Modern English then diversified into many world varieties: British, American, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, Australian, Caribbean, and more.
