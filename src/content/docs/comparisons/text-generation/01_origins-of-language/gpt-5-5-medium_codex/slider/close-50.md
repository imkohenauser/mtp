English developed through several major historical layers.

**Origins**

English is a **West Germanic language**. Its closest older relatives include Frisian, Dutch, German, and the extinct languages of the early Germanic peoples. Its deeper ancestry goes back to **Proto-Germanic**, and before that to **Proto-Indo-European**, the reconstructed ancestor of many European and South Asian languages.

**Old English**

English began taking shape in Britain after the **5th century CE**, when Germanic-speaking groups, especially the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, migrated from areas around present-day northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

Their dialects became **Old English**, spoken roughly from **450 to 1100**. It was much more heavily inflected than modern English and looked closer to German or Icelandic than to present-day English. Words like *house*, *water*, *strong*, *bread*, *king*, and *night* come from this Germanic base.

Old English was also influenced by:

- **Latin**, through Christianity and scholarship
- **Norse**, through Viking settlement, giving words like *sky*, *they*, *them*, *take*, and *law*

**Middle English**

A huge turning point came in **1066**, when the Normans conquered England. The ruling elite spoke Norman French, while ordinary people continued speaking English. Over time, English absorbed thousands of French words, especially in law, government, war, cuisine, fashion, and literature.

This produced **Middle English**, roughly **1100 to 1500**. Grammar simplified: many Old English endings disappeared, word order became more important, and vocabulary became a blend of Germanic everyday words and French/Latin learned words.

For example:

- Germanic: *ask*, *kingly*, *begin*
- French/Latin: *question*, *royal*, *commence*

Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is a famous Middle English work.

**Modern English**

From about **1500 onward**, English entered its modern phase. Several forces shaped it:

- The **printing press**, introduced by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and grammar.
- The **Great Vowel Shift** changed the pronunciation of long vowels, which is one reason English spelling often feels irregular today.
- The **Renaissance** brought many Latin and Greek words into English, especially in science, philosophy, medicine, and the arts.
- The expansion of the British Empire spread English globally and brought in words from many languages, such as *shampoo*, *bungalow*, *tea*, *kangaroo*, and *tomato*.

By the time of Shakespeare, English was recognizably modern, though still different in vocabulary, spelling, and some grammar.

**Global English**

In the modern era, English became a world language through British imperial power, American economic and cultural influence, science, technology, aviation, diplomacy, and the internet. Today it exists in many major varieties, including British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Singaporean, Nigerian, and many others.

**Conclusion**

English began as a Germanic language brought to Britain in the early Middle Ages, was reshaped by Norse and especially Norman French, standardized during the printing and Renaissance periods, and later became global through empire, trade, science, and media. Its history explains why English has Germanic grammar at its core, a vast French and Latin vocabulary, irregular spelling, and many regional varieties.
