English developed through several major layers of contact, conquest, and standardization.

**Origins**

English is a **West Germanic language**. Its deepest roots are in the languages spoken by Germanic peoples in northern Europe. In the 5th and 6th centuries, groups traditionally called the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes** migrated to Britain after the decline of Roman rule. Their dialects formed **Old English**.

Old English was Germanic in grammar and vocabulary. It had grammatical gender, case endings, and many words that still survive: `man`, `wife`, `house`, `water`, `earth`, `strong`, `come`, `go`.

**Old English, c. 450-1100**

Old English was not one single uniform language. It had regional dialects, especially **West Saxon**, **Mercian**, **Northumbrian**, and **Kentish**. It was heavily Germanic, but it absorbed some Latin vocabulary from Christianity after the conversion of the Anglo-Saxons beginning in the late 6th century.

Then came Scandinavian influence. Viking settlement in northern and eastern England brought **Old Norse** into close contact with Old English. This gave English words such as `sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `take`, `they`, `them`, and `their`. Norse contact also helped simplify English grammar.

**Middle English, c. 1100-1500**

The biggest turning point was the **Norman Conquest of 1066**. The ruling elite spoke Norman French, while most ordinary people continued speaking English. For several centuries, England was linguistically layered: English among the common population, French in law and aristocratic life, Latin in church and scholarship.

This transformed English vocabulary. French supplied thousands of words in law, government, food, literature, religion, and culture: `court`, `judge`, `parliament`, `crime`, `beauty`, `beef`, `pork`, `royal`, `service`.

Grammar also changed. English lost many Old English inflections and became more dependent on word order and prepositions. By Chaucer’s time in the late 14th century, English was recognizably closer to the modern language, though still quite different.

**Early Modern English, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English emerged during the Renaissance, printing, exploration, and religious reform. The **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped stabilize spelling and promote the London dialect as a standard.

This period also saw the **Great Vowel Shift**, a major change in pronunciation. Long vowels shifted upward in the mouth, which is one reason English spelling and pronunciation often seem mismatched today.

Writers such as **Shakespeare** and translators of the **King James Bible** helped shape literary and idiomatic English. Latin and Greek loanwords entered heavily through science, medicine, philosophy, and education.

**Modern English, c. 1700-present**

Modern English became more standardized through dictionaries, grammars, schools, publishing, and empire. Samuel Johnson’s dictionary in 1755 was especially influential.

British colonial expansion spread English across North America, the Caribbean, Africa, South Asia, Australia, and beyond. These regions developed their own varieties: American English, Indian English, Nigerian English, Australian English, and many others.

Today English is a global language with a Germanic core, large French and Latin vocabulary, Norse influence, and many later borrowings from languages around the world. Its history is less a straight line than a record of repeated contact: Germanic settlement, Christian Latin, Viking Norse, Norman French, Renaissance classical learning, empire, migration, and global media.
