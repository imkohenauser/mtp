**Origins**

English belongs to the West Germanic branch of the Indo-European language family. Its closest major relatives include Frisian, Dutch, and German. Before English existed, the ancestors of the English-speaking peoples lived around what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

After the Roman Empire withdrew from Britain in the early 5th century, Germanic-speaking groups, mainly the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, migrated to Britain. Their dialects gradually became **Old English**. The word “English” itself comes from the **Angles**, whose name also gives us “England,” originally “Angle-land.”

**Old English, c. 450-1100**

Old English was very different from modern English. It had grammatical gender, many case endings, and a much freer word order. A modern English speaker cannot usually read it without study.

A famous example is *Beowulf*, written in Old English. Its vocabulary was mostly Germanic: words like *house*, *bread*, *water*, *strong*, *night*, *man*, *wife*, and *king* come from this older layer.

Christian missionaries brought Latin influence, especially religious and scholarly terms. Later, Viking settlement added many Old Norse words, especially in northern and eastern England. Words like *sky*, *egg*, *window*, *take*, *they*, *their*, and *them* reflect Norse influence. Incidentally, even the pronouns *they/their/them* are borrowed, which is unusual because pronouns are usually very resistant to replacement.

**Middle English, c. 1100-1500**

The biggest turning point came in 1066, when the Normans conquered England. The Norman ruling class spoke a variety of French, while ordinary people continued speaking English. For centuries, England was linguistically layered: Latin for church and scholarship, French for law and aristocracy, English for everyday life.

This transformed English vocabulary. Many paired words still reveal this social history: Germanic *cow*, *sheep*, and *pig* for the animals tended by commoners; French-derived *beef*, *mutton*, and *pork* for the meat served at elite tables.

During Middle English, many Old English endings disappeared, word order became more fixed, and the language moved closer to the structure we recognize today. Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is the classic literary landmark of this period.

**Early Modern English, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of Shakespeare and the King James Bible. It is mostly understandable to modern readers, though some grammar and vocabulary feel old-fashioned.

This period saw the **Great Vowel Shift**, a major change in pronunciation. Long vowels moved around, which helps explain why English spelling often seems disconnected from pronunciation. The printing press also helped standardize spelling, especially around the London dialect, though pronunciation kept changing after many spellings had become fixed.

The Renaissance brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary into English: words connected to science, philosophy, medicine, literature, and government. English became unusually comfortable with synonyms from different sources: *kingly* from Old English, *royal* from French, *regal* from Latin.

**Modern English, c. 1700-present**

By the 18th and 19th centuries, English expanded globally through British trade, colonization, naval power, science, and later American economic and cultural influence. It absorbed words from many languages: *shampoo* from Hindi, *pyjama* from Persian/Urdu, *kangaroo* from Guugu Yimithirr, *bungalow* from Bengali, *tsunami* from Japanese, *robot* from Czech.

Modern English also diversified into many world varieties: British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, Caribbean, and many others. English is now less a single national language than a global language family with shared roots and many local forms.

So the short version is: English began as a Germanic language brought to Britain after Rome, was reshaped by Norse contact, heavily re-vocabularized by Norman French and Latin, standardized through print and education, and globalized through empire, migration, science, commerce, and media. Its mixed character is not accidental; it is the main story of the language.
