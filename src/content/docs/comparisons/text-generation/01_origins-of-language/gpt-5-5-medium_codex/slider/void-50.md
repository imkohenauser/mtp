English developed from the languages of Germanic-speaking peoples who migrated to Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries CE: mainly Angles, Saxons, and Jutes. Their dialects formed **Old English**, a West Germanic language related to Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and Old High German.

Old English was heavily inflected, with grammatical gender, cases, strong and weak verbs, and a vocabulary mostly Germanic. Examples include words like `house`, `bread`, `water`, `king`, `wife`, and `night`.

Christianization from the late 6th century brought Latin vocabulary: `school`, `priest`, `minster`, `altar`. Viking settlement from the 8th to 11th centuries added Old Norse influence, especially in northern and eastern England. Common Norse-derived words include `they`, `them`, `their`, `sky`, `egg`, `take`, and `law`.

The major break came after the **Norman Conquest of 1066**. Norman French became the language of government, law, court culture, and aristocracy, while English remained the language of most of the population. Over several centuries, English absorbed large amounts of French vocabulary: `court`, `judge`, `parliament`, `army`, `beauty`, `beef`, `pork`.

This period produced **Middle English**. Grammar simplified: many case endings disappeared, word order became more important, and grammatical gender largely vanished. Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* in the late 14th century is the best-known literary example.

From about 1500, **Early Modern English** emerged. The printing press, introduced by William Caxton in 1476, helped stabilize spelling, though pronunciation continued changing. The **Great Vowel Shift** altered long vowel sounds, creating many mismatches between English spelling and pronunciation.

The Renaissance added many Latin and Greek words, especially in science, philosophy, medicine, and literature: `radius`, `species`, `temperature`, `democracy`, `theory`. Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong to this phase.

**Modern English** expanded globally through British colonization, trade, migration, and later American political, economic, and cultural influence. It borrowed from many languages: `shampoo` from Hindi, `kangaroo` from Guugu Yimithirr, `tomato` from Nahuatl via Spanish, `piano` from Italian, `robot` from Czech.

Its present form is a layered language:

Germanic core grammar and everyday vocabulary  
Norse influence in pronouns and common verbs  
French vocabulary in law, government, cuisine, and culture  
Latin and Greek vocabulary in scholarship and science  
Global borrowings from empire, trade, and modern contact

That mixture is why English often has near-synonyms from different sources: `kingly` Germanic, `royal` French, `regal` Latin.
