English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries by Anglo-Saxon settlers: Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related groups from what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands. Before that, much of Britain spoke Celtic languages, and Latin had been present through Roman rule and Christianity. But the core of English came from Germanic speech: words like `house`, `bread`, `water`, `mother`, `father`, `night`, `land`, `king`.

Old English, roughly 450-1100, was highly inflected, closer in grammar to modern German than to modern English. It had grammatical gender, case endings, strong and weak verbs, and flexible word order. Texts like *Beowulf* belong to this period. Then came Viking settlement from the 8th to 11th centuries. Old Norse, another Germanic language, mixed with Old English, especially in northern and eastern England. This gave English words like `sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `take`, `they`, `them`, `their`. Even basic grammar shifted. Big change.

The decisive rupture came in 1066 with the Norman Conquest. The ruling class spoke Norman French; the Church and scholarship used Latin; ordinary people continued speaking English. For centuries, England was multilingual. French supplied huge numbers of words for law, government, food, fashion, war, literature, and aristocratic life: `court`, `judge`, `parliament`, `beef`, `mutton`, `beauty`, `army`, `peace`. This is why English often has Germanic everyday words beside French or Latin formal ones: `ask` vs. `question`, `kingly` vs. `royal`, `holy` vs. `sacred`, `freedom` vs. `liberty`.

Middle English, roughly 1100-1500, was the result: simplified grammar, fewer endings, more fixed word order, massive French vocabulary. Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is Middle English. Recognizable, but still distant.

Then Early Modern English, roughly 1500-1700, brought the printing press, standard spelling pressures, Renaissance learning, and the Great Vowel Shift. The Great Vowel Shift changed the pronunciation of long vowels, which is one reason English spelling looks so strange today: spellings often preserve older pronunciations while speech moved on. Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong here. English absorbed Latin and Greek vocabulary at scale: `education`, `temperature`, `democracy`, `philosophy`, `species`, `radius`.

Modern English developed from about 1700 onward through empire, science, trade, colonization, migration, and global media. Then American English, Indian English, Caribbean English, African Englishes, Singapore English, Australian English, and many others became major varieties in their own right.

So English is Germanic at its core, French and Latin in much of its learned and official vocabulary, Norse in some basic grammar and pronouns, and globally hybrid in its modern form. Its history is not a straight line. It is invasion, settlement, class division, trade, scripture, printing, empire, science, migration, internet. Layer after layer. That is why English can say `begin`, `commence`, and `initiate` and mean nearly the same thing, with different social weight.
