**Thesis: English Is Fundamentally Germanic**

English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain in the 5th century CE by Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related groups from what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

This early language, now called **Old English**, was closely related to Old Frisian and Old Saxon. Its basic grammar and everyday vocabulary were Germanic:

- `come`, `go`, `see`, `eat`
- `I`, `you`, `he`, `she`, `we`

Old English had grammatical gender, noun cases, richer verb endings, and freer word order than modern English. A sentence from Old English often looks almost like a foreign language to modern readers.

Christianization from the late 6th century brought Latin influence, especially in religious and scholarly vocabulary: `priest`, `school`, `altar`, `mass`, `monk`.

Then came the Vikings. From the 8th to 11th centuries, Norse-speaking settlers entered northern and eastern England. Old Norse and Old English were related, so contact was intense. English absorbed Norse words such as:

- `sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `window`
- `they`, `them`, `their`
- `take`, `give`, `get`

This contact also helped simplify English grammar. When related languages are spoken side by side, complex endings often erode because speakers rely more on word order and shared vocabulary.

**Antithesis: Modern English Is Not “Purely Germanic”**

The biggest rupture came in **1066**, when William of Normandy conquered England. For centuries afterward, England’s ruling class used varieties of French, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English survived as the language of the majority, but it changed dramatically.

This produced **Middle English**, the language of Chaucer. English grammar became simpler: many case endings disappeared, grammatical gender faded, and word order became more fixed. At the same time, French vocabulary flooded in, especially in areas of power and prestige:

By the late Middle Ages, English reemerged as the language of government and literature, but it was no longer the same language as Old English.

**Rebuilding the Picture**

A better way to frame English is this:

English is a **Germanic language with a heavily mixed vocabulary and a globally expanded history**.

From around 1500 onward, **Early Modern English** developed. This was the language of Shakespeare, the King James Bible, and the printing press. Modern English then spread through empire, migration, commerce, science, aviation, computing, and American cultural influence. Today there is no single center of English. There are many major Englishes: British, American, Indian, Nigerian, Australian, Singaporean, Caribbean, and others.
