English developed through layers of contact, conquest, migration, and cultural prestige. Its history is usually divided into Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English.

**Old English** began after Germanic-speaking peoples, mainly Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, settled in Britain from the 5th century onward. Their West Germanic dialects displaced or absorbed much of the earlier Celtic linguistic landscape in what became England. Old English was highly inflected, closer in structure to modern German or Icelandic than to present-day English. Words like `house`, `water`, `strong`, `night`, and `king` come from this Germanic base.

That Germanic foundation soon absorbed outside influence. Christianization from the late 6th century brought Latin vocabulary connected to religion, learning, and administration, such as `priest`, `school`, and `altar`. Later, Viking settlement from Scandinavia added many Old Norse words, especially in northern and eastern England. Everyday words like `sky`, `egg`, `take`, `they`, `their`, and `them` reflect this Norse contact.

The largest turning point came with the Norman Conquest in 1066. Norman French became the language of the ruling elite, law, government, and high culture, while English remained the language of most ordinary people. Over several centuries, this produced **Middle English**, a language with a Germanic grammatical core but a greatly expanded French and Latin vocabulary. This is why English often has pairs like `kingly` from Germanic roots, `royal` from French, and `regal` from Latin.

By the time of Chaucer in the late 14th century, English had re-emerged as a major literary and administrative language, but it had changed substantially. Many Old English case endings had weakened or disappeared, word order became more important, and the vocabulary had become much more mixed.

**Early Modern English** emerged roughly from the late 15th century. The printing press, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms, especially around the London dialect. At the same time, the Renaissance brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary into English, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature. Shakespeare’s English belongs to this period: recognizable today, but still grammatically and semantically different from current usage.

A major sound change also occurred between the 15th and 18th centuries: the **Great Vowel Shift**. For example, the written forms of words like `time`, `house`, and `name` reflect older spelling conventions more than modern phonetics.

**Modern English** expanded globally from the 17th century onward through British colonization, trade, migration, science, and later American cultural and economic influence. As English spread, it developed major regional varieties: British, American, Australian, Indian, Caribbean, African, Singaporean, and many others. Each has contributed vocabulary, pronunciation patterns, and idioms.

So English is not a single “pure” descendant of one source. It is fundamentally Germanic in grammar and core vocabulary, heavily shaped by Norse, massively enriched by French and Latin, later influenced by Greek and many world languages, and globally diversified through empire, commerce, media, and migration. Its history is a history of contact.
