The English language developed through repeated contact, conquest, migration, and borrowing. Its history is usually divided into four broad stages.

**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**

Before English existed, Britain was home to Celtic languages, related to modern Welsh, Cornish, Breton, Irish, and Scottish Gaelic. From 43 CE, much of Britain was ruled by Rome, so Latin influenced place names, administration, religion, and trade. But Latin did not become the everyday language of most people in Britain.

**2. Old English: Germanic Origins**

English began after the Roman withdrawal, especially from the 5th century onward, when Germanic-speaking peoples migrated to Britain: the Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and others. Their dialects formed what we call Old English.

Old English was a West Germanic language, related to Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and more distantly to modern German and Dutch. It was highly inflected, meaning word endings carried much grammatical information.

Example: the Lord’s Prayer in Old English begins “Fæder ure,” recognizable as “Father our,” but most Old English is difficult for modern English speakers to read without study.

Important influences during this period included:

- Celtic languages: relatively little vocabulary, but some place names and possible grammatical influence.
- Latin: especially through Christianity after the mission of Augustine in 597 CE.
- Old Norse: from Viking settlement beginning in the late 8th century.

Old Norse had a major effect because Norse and Old English were related enough for contact and mixing. Everyday words such as `sky`, `egg`, `window`, `take`, `they`, `them`, and `their` came from Norse.

**3. Middle English: Norman French and Simplification**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 radically changed English. The ruling elite spoke Norman French, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship. English survived as the language of the common population, but it absorbed thousands of French words.

This is why English often has Germanic everyday words beside French or Latin-derived formal words:

- `ask` vs. `question`
- `kingly` vs. `royal`
- `begin` vs. `commence`
- `cow` vs. `beef`
- `sheep` vs. `mutton`

During Middle English, grammar also simplified. Many Old English case endings disappeared, word order became more important, and pronunciation changed. Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* in the late 14th century is a famous Middle English work: partly recognizable, but still noticeably different from modern English.

**4. Early Modern English: Printing, Renaissance, and Expansion**

Early Modern English developed from around 1500 to 1700. Several forces shaped it.

First, the printing press, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms, especially around the London dialect.

Second, the Renaissance brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary into English, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.

Third, pronunciation changed dramatically in the Great Vowel Shift, roughly from the 15th to 17th centuries. Long vowels moved in pronunciation, which helps explain why English spelling often looks older than modern pronunciation.

Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong to this period. Their language is mostly understandable today, though some vocabulary and grammar feel archaic.

**5. Modern English: Global Spread and Borrowing**

From the 17th century onward, English spread through colonization, trade, migration, science, and later American political, economic, and cultural influence. It became established in North America, the Caribbean, Africa, South Asia, Australia, New Zealand, and many other regions.

Modern English kept borrowing widely:

- from Hindi: `shampoo`, `bungalow`, `pajamas`

- from Italian: `opera`, `piano`
- from Indigenous American languages: `canoe`, `tobacco`, `chocolate`

Today English is not one single uniform language but a global family of varieties: British English, American English, Indian English, Nigerian English, Singapore English, Australian English, and many more.

**Conclusion**

English began as a Germanic language brought to Britain by early medieval migrants. It was reshaped by Norse settlement, transformed by Norman French after 1066, enriched by Latin and Greek during the Renaissance, and globalized through empire, trade, migration, and modern media. Its unusual character comes from this layered history: Germanic grammar and core vocabulary, heavy French and Latin borrowing, and a long habit of absorbing words from almost everywhere.
