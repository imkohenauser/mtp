**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**

Before English existed, Britain was largely Celtic-speaking. Languages related to Welsh, Cornish, and Breton were spoken across much of the island.

From 43 CE, Rome ruled much of Britain. Latin was used in administration, the army, towns, and Christianity, but it did not replace the local Celtic languages. When Roman power withdrew in the early 5th century, Britain became politically fragmented.

**2. Old English: Germanic Origins**

English began after Germanic-speaking peoples migrated to Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries. These groups are traditionally called the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**.

Their dialects formed **Old English**, also called **Anglo-Saxon**. It was closely related to Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and other West Germanic languages.

Old English was very different from Modern English. It had grammatical gender, case endings, more flexible word order, and many words that no longer survive. Still, many of the most basic English words come from Old English:

A famous Old English text is *Beowulf*.

**3. Christianization and Latin Influence**

Latin influence would return repeatedly throughout English history.

**4. Viking Influence: Old Norse**

From the late 8th century, Scandinavian Vikings raided, settled, and ruled parts of England. Their language, **Old Norse**, was related to Old English, so the two languages could influence each other deeply.

For centuries, England had a layered linguistic society:

- English: common people, daily life

By the time of **Middle English**, roughly 1100-1500, English had lost many Old English inflections and had absorbed huge amounts of French vocabulary. Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is the best-known Middle English work.

**6. Early Modern English: Printing, Renaissance, Shakespeare**

From about 1500 to 1700, English became **Early Modern English**.

Several major changes happened:

- The **printing press**, introduced by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms.
- The **Renaissance** brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary into science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.
- Writers such as **Shakespeare** used and expanded English with great flexibility.
- The **King James Bible** of 1611 helped shape literary and religious English.

Many learned words entered English during this period:

- `education`
- `democracy`
- `temperature`

- `philosophy`
- `medicine`

For example, words like `name`, `time`, `house`, and `food` preserve older spellings while pronunciation moved on.

**7. Modern English: Empire, Science, and Global Spread**

From about 1700 onward, English became **Modern English**.

- Hindi/Urdu: `shampoo`, `bungalow`, `pajamas`

- Australian languages: `kangaroo`, `boomerang`

In North America, English developed distinct forms, especially **American English**, shaped by settlement, immigration, indigenous contact, and political independence.

In the 19th and 20th centuries, English became increasingly important in science, diplomacy, business, aviation, computing, entertainment, and the internet. The global influence of the British Empire was later reinforced by the economic and cultural power of the United States.

**8. Why English Is So Mixed**

English is structurally Germanic, but lexically hybrid.

Its core grammar and most basic words are Germanic:

- `I am`
- `you are`
- `we have`

So English has a layered vocabulary: plain Germanic words, French words of social and legal power, and Latin/Greek words of scholarship.

**Conclusion**

English began as the Germanic speech of Anglo-Saxon settlers in early medieval Britain. It was reshaped by Celtic contact, Roman Christianity, Viking settlement, the Norman Conquest, medieval French, Renaissance Latin and Greek, printing, colonial expansion, and modern globalization.
