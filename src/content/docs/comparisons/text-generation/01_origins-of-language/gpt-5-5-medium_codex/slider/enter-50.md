The English language is a Germanic language that developed in Britain through repeated contact, conquest, migration, and cultural exchange. Its history is usually divided into four broad stages: Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English.

**1. Origins: Germanic Roots**

English began as a West Germanic language. Its closest early relatives were the languages spoken by peoples from the North Sea region of what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

In the 5th and 6th centuries, groups traditionally called the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes migrated to Britain after the decline of Roman rule. Their dialects gradually displaced many of the Celtic languages spoken in much of what became England.

The word “English” itself comes from the Angles: *Englisc* meant the language of the Angles, and *England* meant the land of the Angles.

**2. Old English: c. 450-1100**

Old English was very different from modern English. It had grammatical gender, many inflectional endings, and a flexible word order, more like modern German or Icelandic than present-day English.

Example: *Beowulf*, the famous epic poem, was written in Old English. A modern English speaker cannot usually understand it without study.

Old English vocabulary was mostly Germanic. Basic words such as *house*, *water*, *bread*, *man*, *wife*, *child*, *night*, *strong*, and *sing* come from this older Germanic layer.

During this period, English was influenced by:

- **Latin**, through Christianity and scholarship: words like *school*, *priest*, *altar*
- **Old Norse**, through Viking settlement: words like *sky*, *egg*, *knife*, *take*, *they*, *them*, *their*

The Norse influence was especially important because Old Norse and Old English were related languages. Contact between speakers may have helped simplify English grammar.

**3. Middle English: c. 1100-1500**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 transformed English. William the Conqueror and his followers spoke Norman French, and for centuries French became the language of the ruling class, law, administration, and high culture in England.

English survived as the language of ordinary people, but it absorbed thousands of French words.

This is why English often has pairs of words with different social tones:

- Germanic: *kingly*  
- French: *royal*  
- Latin: *regal*

Or:

- Germanic: *ask*  
- French: *question*  
- Latin: *interrogate*

Middle English also lost many Old English inflections. Word order became more important, and the language began to look more like modern English.

Geoffrey Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is the best-known literary work in Middle English. It is still difficult for modern readers, but much more recognizable than Old English.

**4. Early Modern English: c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of Shakespeare and the King James Bible. It is recognizably English, though some grammar, spelling, and meanings differ from today.

Several major forces shaped this period:

- **The printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and grammar.
- **The Renaissance** brought many Latin and Greek words into English, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.
- **The Great Vowel Shift** changed English pronunciation dramatically. Long vowels shifted in sound, which helps explain why English spelling often does not match pronunciation.

For example, words like *time*, *house*, *name*, and *meet* changed pronunciation while many spellings remained older.

**5. Modern English: c. 1700-present**

Modern English expanded globally through British colonialism, trade, migration, science, and later American cultural and economic influence.

English absorbed words from many languages, including:

- Hindi: *shampoo*, *bungalow*, *pajamas*
- Arabic: *algebra*, *coffee*, *sugar*
- Spanish: *tomato*, *canyon*, *ranch*
- Indigenous American languages: *tobacco*, *canoe*, *hurricane*
- Japanese: *tsunami*, *karaoke*, *emoji*

Today, English is used as a first language, second language, official language, or global lingua franca across much of the world. It has many major varieties, including British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, and Caribbean Englishes.

**Big Picture**

English began as a Germanic language brought to Britain by early medieval settlers. It was reshaped by Norse contact, transformed by Norman French, enriched by Latin and Greek learning, standardized through print, and globalized through empire, commerce, migration, and media.

That is why English has a Germanic grammatical core but an unusually large and layered vocabulary: everyday words often come from Old English, legal and aristocratic words from French, scholarly words from Latin and Greek, and global words from centuries of contact with other languages.
