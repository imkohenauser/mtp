English developed in layers, shaped by migration, conquest, trade, scholarship, empire, and global media.

**Origins**
English belongs to the **Germanic branch** of the Indo-European language family. Its closest major relatives are **Frisian, Dutch, German, and the Scandinavian languages**.

Before English existed, Britain was mostly home to **Celtic languages**. After the Roman withdrawal from Britain in the early 5th century, Germanic-speaking peoples from northwestern Europe migrated into Britain: mainly **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**. Their dialects became **Old English**.

**Main Historical Stages**

| Period | Dates | Main Features |
|---|---:|---|
| **Old English** | c. 450-1100 | Germanic core; complex grammar; words like *house, water, bread, king* |
| **Middle English** | c. 1100-1500 | Heavy French influence after 1066; grammar simplified; Chaucer |
| **Early Modern English** | c. 1500-1700 | Printing, Renaissance vocabulary, Shakespeare, King James Bible |
| **Modern English** | c. 1700-present | Standardization, science/empire/global spread, huge vocabulary growth |

**Old English**
Old English was not simply “old-fashioned modern English.” It was much more like German in structure, with grammatical gender, case endings, and flexible word order. A sentence from *Beowulf* is largely unreadable to modern English speakers without training.

Its vocabulary was mainly Germanic: *man, wife, child, earth, sun, moon, eat, drink, sleep, fight*.

**Viking Influence**
From the 8th to 11th centuries, Scandinavian settlers brought Old Norse into contact with Old English. This added everyday words such as *sky, egg, knife, take, they, them,* and *their*. Norse contact also helped simplify English grammar because related but different languages were being used side by side.

**Norman Conquest**
The biggest turning point came in **1066**, when the Normans conquered England. For centuries, the ruling class used **Norman French**, while Latin remained important in church, law, and scholarship.

This created a layered vocabulary:

| Germanic English | French/Latin-derived English |
|---|---|
| ask | question |
| kingly | royal |
| freedom | liberty |
| hearty | cordial |
| begin | commence |

This is why English often has several near-synonyms with different tones: plain, formal, legal, literary, or technical.

**Middle English**
By the time of **Geoffrey Chaucer** in the late 1300s, English had changed greatly. Many Old English endings had disappeared, word order became more fixed, and French vocabulary had entered law, government, food, art, and social life: *court, judge, parliament, beef, pork, beauty, music*.

**Early Modern English**
Around 1500, English entered its early modern phase. Three forces mattered especially:

1. **The printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms.
2. **The Renaissance** brought many Latin and Greek words into English: *education, atmosphere, encyclopedia, democracy*.
3. **Writers and translators**, especially Shakespeare and the King James Bible, helped shape literary and idiomatic English.

This period also includes the **Great Vowel Shift**, a major pronunciation change. It explains many odd spelling-pronunciation mismatches, such as why *name, time,* and *house* are pronounced differently from what older spelling patterns might suggest.

**Modern English**
From the 18th century onward, dictionaries, grammars, schools, newspapers, and publishing strengthened standard written English. Samuel Johnson’s dictionary in **1755** was especially influential.

Then British colonial expansion and later American economic, scientific, military, and cultural power spread English globally. English absorbed words from many languages, including:

| Source | Examples |
|---|---|
| Hindi/Urdu | *pajamas, shampoo, bungalow* |
| Arabic | *alcohol, algebra, coffee* |
| Spanish | *canyon, ranch, mosquito* |
| Indigenous American languages | *tobacco, chocolate, canoe* |
| Japanese | *tsunami, karaoke, emoji* |

**Why English Is So Mixed**
English is Germanic in its basic structure and everyday vocabulary, but it has enormous French, Latin, Greek, Norse, and global influence. A simple sentence often shows this mixture:

> “The king asked a question about royal authority.”

*King* and *asked* are Germanic; *question, royal,* and *authority* come through French/Latin.

So English is not a “Romance language,” even though much of its formal vocabulary is Romance-derived. Its grammatical skeleton and oldest everyday words are Germanic.
