English developed from several layers of language contact over about 1,500 years.

**1. Germanic Origins**

English began as a West Germanic language. Its closest early relatives were Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and other Germanic languages spoken around what is now northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

In the 5th and 6th centuries, Germanic-speaking peoples, traditionally called the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, migrated to Britain after Roman rule weakened. Their dialects displaced much of the Latin- and Celtic-speaking culture in what became England.

The word **English** comes from **Angle**: the “land of the Angles” became **England**, and their language became **English**.

**2. Old English: c. 450-1100**

Old English was highly Germanic. It had grammatical gender, case endings, strong and weak verbs, and a word order more flexible than modern English.

A sentence in Old English can look almost like a foreign language to modern readers. For example, *Faeder ure thu the eart on heofonum* means “Our Father, who art in heaven.”

Old English vocabulary was mostly Germanic: words like *house*, *bread*, *water*, *earth*, *wife*, *child*, *king*, *night*, and *strong* come from this layer.

Christianization from the late 6th century added many Latin words, especially for religion, learning, and church life.

**3. Norse Influence: c. 800-1100**

Viking settlement brought Old Norse into contact with Old English, especially in northern and eastern England.

Norse influence added common words such as *sky*, *egg*, *knife*, *window*, *they*, *them*, *their*, *take*, and *give*. This contact also helped simplify English grammar, because Old English and Old Norse were related but not identical. Speakers likely reduced complex endings to make communication easier.

**4. Norman French and Middle English: c. 1100-1500**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 changed English deeply. The ruling class spoke Norman French, while Latin remained important in church, scholarship, and law. English continued as the language of most ordinary people, but it absorbed thousands of French words.

French influence is why English has pairs such as:

- *kingly* from Old English, *royal* from French
- *ask* from Old English, *question* from French
- *cow* from Old English, *beef* from French
- *freedom* from Old English, *liberty* from French

During Middle English, many Old English case endings disappeared, word order became more fixed, and the language moved much closer to modern structure. Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* belongs to this period.

**5. Early Modern English: c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English emerged during the Renaissance, printing, overseas exploration, and the rise of London as a political and cultural center.

The printing press, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and written forms, though English spelling remained inconsistent because pronunciation kept changing.

A major sound change, the **Great Vowel Shift**, altered the pronunciation of long vowels. This is one reason English spelling often does not match modern pronunciation. For example, words like *name*, *time*, *house*, and *meet* changed pronunciation while many spellings stayed older.

This was also the period of Shakespeare and the King James Bible, both of which strongly influenced literary and religious English.

**6. Modern English: c. 1700-present**

Modern English expanded through empire, trade, science, migration, and later global media. It borrowed words from many languages: *pajamas* from Hindi/Urdu, *shampoo* from Hindi, *safari* from Swahili, *kindergarten* from German, *piano* from Italian, *robot* from Czech, and many others.

British colonial expansion spread English to North America, the Caribbean, Africa, South Asia, Australia, and elsewhere. Over time, distinct varieties developed: American English, British English, Australian English, Indian English, Nigerian English, Singapore English, and many more.

Today English is a global lingua franca in business, science, aviation, technology, diplomacy, and popular culture. It remains Germanic in its basic grammar and everyday vocabulary, but its word stock is heavily shaped by Norse, French, Latin, Greek, and many later borrowings.
