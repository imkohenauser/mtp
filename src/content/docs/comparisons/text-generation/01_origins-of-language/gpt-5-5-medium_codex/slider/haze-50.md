English began as a Germanic language, but its history is more like a river fed by many tributaries than a straight line.

**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**

Before English existed, Britain was home to Celtic-speaking peoples. Languages related to modern Welsh, Cornish, and Breton were spoken across much of the island. Then Rome conquered much of Britain in 43 CE, bringing Latin with it. Latin influenced place names, administration, Christianity, and later learning, but it did not become the everyday language of most people in Britain in the way it did in parts of continental Europe.

**2. Old English: The Anglo-Saxon Foundation**

English proper begins after the Roman withdrawal, especially from the 5th century onward, when Germanic-speaking groups crossed the North Sea: Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and others. Their dialects formed what we now call **Old English**.

Old English was much closer to German, Dutch, or Frisian than to modern English. A sentence from Old English can feel almost like a foreign language. It had grammatical gender, many case endings, and a more flexible word order.

Words from this layer are still the bones of English:  
`house`, `bread`, `water`, `earth`, `night`, `wife`, `child`, `king`, `strong`, `sing`.

**3. Norse Influence: Vikings and Everyday Speech**

From the late 8th century, Viking raids and settlements brought Old Norse into contact with Old English, especially in northern and eastern England. The two languages were related, so they could rub against each other in daily life.

Norse gave English many ordinary words:  
`sky`, `egg`, `knife`, `window`, `take`, `give`, `they`, `them`, `their`.

This contact may also have helped simplify English grammar. When related languages meet in marketplaces, farms, and mixed households, endings can wear down, and word order can begin to carry more of the meaning.

**4. The Norman Conquest: French Over English**

The great turning point came in **1066**, when William of Normandy conquered England. For centuries afterward, the ruling class used Norman French, the Church and scholars used Latin, and common people continued speaking English.

This created a layered vocabulary. English kept many everyday Germanic words, while French supplied terms of law, government, war, fashion, food, and refinement:

Germanic: `kingly`, `ask`, `cow`, `sheep`, `house`  
French/Latin: `royal`, `question`, `beef`, `mutton`, `mansion`

That is why English often has pairs or triples of near-synonyms with different tones:  
`help`, `aid`, `assist`;  
`begin`, `commence`;  
`freedom`, `liberty`.

**5. Middle English: A Language Remade**

Between roughly 1100 and 1500, Old English became **Middle English**. Grammar lost many older endings. Word order became more fixed. French vocabulary poured in. The language of Geoffrey Chaucer, writing in the late 1300s, is recognizable but still misty to modern eyes.

The opening of *The Canterbury Tales* already sounds partly familiar, partly distant: spring, pilgrimage, people, stories, motion.

**6. Early Modern English: Printing, Shakespeare, and Expansion**

From about 1500 to 1700, English entered its **Early Modern** period. The printing press, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped stabilize spelling and spread the London dialect as a written standard.

At the same time, English pronunciation shifted dramatically in what is called the **Great Vowel Shift**. Long vowels changed their sounds, which helps explain why English spelling can feel haunted by older pronunciations. Words like `name`, `time`, `house`, and `food` did not always sound as they do now.

This was also the age of Shakespeare, the King James Bible, Renaissance scholarship, and heavy borrowing from Latin and Greek. English grew more expansive, more elastic.

**7. Modern English: Empire, Science, and Global Contact**

From the 17th century onward, English traveled through colonization, trade, migration, slavery, missionary activity, science, and later mass media. It absorbed words from many languages:

From Hindi/Urdu: `shampoo`, `bungalow`, `pajamas`  
From Arabic: `algebra`, `coffee`, `sugar`  
From Spanish: `canyon`, `tobacco`, `mosquito`  
From Indigenous American languages: `tomato`, `potato`, `canoe`

English spread to North America, the Caribbean, Africa, South Asia, Australia, and beyond. In each place it changed shape. American English, Indian English, Nigerian English, Singapore English, Australian English, and many others are not just “versions” of one frozen language; they are living branches.

**In Short**

English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain by Anglo-Saxon settlers. It was shaped by Celtic surroundings, Latin Christianity, Norse settlement, Norman French rule, Renaissance learning, printing, empire, migration, and global exchange.

Its character comes from that mixture: a Germanic grammar core, a vast Romance and classical vocabulary, and a long habit of borrowing. English is less a pure inheritance than a crowded old city, with Roman stones, Saxon foundations, Norse beams, Norman halls, and modern glass built into the same streets.
