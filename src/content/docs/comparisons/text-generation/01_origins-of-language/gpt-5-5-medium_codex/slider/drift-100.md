English begins as a Germanic language carried to Britain in the 5th century by Anglo-Saxon settlers: Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related peoples from the North Sea coast. Their dialects replaced or pushed aside much of the earlier Celtic speech in what became England, though Celtic languages survived in Wales, Cornwall, Scotland, and Ireland.

Old English, roughly 450-1100, was much more like modern German or Old Norse than modern English. It had grammatical gender, case endings, stronger verb inflections, and words like *cyning* for “king,” *hus* for “house,” and *scip* for “ship.” You can still feel that world in basic vocabulary: *mother, father, bread, water, earth, night, hand, come, go*. These are the bones of English.

Then the Vikings arrived. From the late 8th century onward, Norse-speaking settlers in northern and eastern England influenced English deeply. They gave English ordinary words like *sky, egg, window, take, give, they, them,* and *their*. Incidentally, pronouns are usually resistant to borrowing, so the adoption of *they/them/their* from Norse shows how intense contact must have been.

The next huge turning point was 1066: the Norman Conquest. The ruling elite spoke Norman French, while most common people continued speaking English. For centuries, England was linguistically layered: English in the fields and homes, French in law and aristocracy, Latin in church and scholarship. That is why English often has word triplets: *kingly* from Germanic English, *royal* from French, *regal* from Latin. Same general idea, different social atmosphere.

Middle English, about 1100-1500, was the result of this mixing. English lost many Old English endings, word order became more important, and French vocabulary poured in: *court, judge, government, beauty, color, money, war, peace, language*. Chaucer’s English in the 1300s is recognizably English, but still strange to modern ears.

Then comes Early Modern English, about 1500-1700. The printing press helped standardize spelling and grammar, especially around London English. The Renaissance brought a flood of Latin and Greek vocabulary: *education, temperature, encyclopedia, democracy, species*. Shakespeare wrote during this period, when English was expanding rapidly and still very flexible.

There was also the Great Vowel Shift, a major pronunciation change between roughly the 1400s and 1700s. Long vowels moved upward in the mouth: words like *time, house,* and *name* changed pronunciation while spelling often stayed older. That is one reason English spelling feels so mismatched today. It preserves fossils of earlier speech.

Modern English then spread through British colonization, trade, science, migration, and later American cultural and technological power. It absorbed words everywhere it went: *shampoo* from Hindi, *coffee* from Arabic/Turkish routes, *robot* from Czech, *piano* from Italian, *tsunami* from Japanese, *kindergarten* from German.

So English is not a “pure” language, which is true of basically all languages if you look long enough. It is Germanic in grammar and core vocabulary, heavily French and Latin in learned and official vocabulary, Norse-marked in everyday speech, and globally porous in modern usage. Its history is less like a straight family tree and more like a river system: Germanic source waters, Norse tributaries, a French flood, Latin canals, and then countless later streams.
