**Surface Answer**
English began as a West Germanic language brought to Britain by Anglo-Saxon settlers in the 5th century. Over time, it absorbed major influences from Latin, Old Norse, Norman French, and later global languages through empire, trade, science, and migration.

Its history is usually divided into four broad stages:

1. **Old English**: c. 450-1150  
2. **Middle English**: c. 1150-1500  
3. **Early Modern English**: c. 1500-1700  
4. **Modern English**: c. 1700-present  

**Deeper Development**

**1. Before English: Celtic and Roman Britain**
Before English arrived, Britain was home to Celtic languages, ancestors of Welsh, Cornish, and Breton. After the Roman conquest in 43 CE, Latin became important in administration, military life, Christianity, and urban culture, but it did not replace the Celtic languages among most people.

When Roman authority declined in the early 5th century, Britain became more vulnerable to migration and invasion.

**2. Old English: Germanic Roots**
From around the 5th century, Angles, Saxons, Jutes, and related Germanic peoples settled in Britain. Their dialects formed **Old English**, also called Anglo-Saxon.

Old English was very different from present-day English. It had grammatical gender, complex noun cases, and many inflections, closer in structure to modern German or Icelandic than to modern English.

Examples of Old English words that survived include:

- `house`
- `bread`
- `water`
- `earth`
- `night`
- `king`
- `wife`
- `child`

Christian missionaries later brought Latin religious vocabulary, giving English words such as `altar`, `school`, `priest`, and `monk`.

**3. Norse Influence: Vikings and Simplification**
From the late 8th century onward, Viking settlers brought **Old Norse**, another Germanic language. In areas of northern and eastern England, Norse and Old English speakers lived side by side.

This contact deeply affected English. Many common words come from Norse, including:

- `sky`
- `egg`
- `window`
- `they`
- `their`
- `them`
- `take`
- `get`

Norse influence may also have helped simplify English grammar, because related but different languages in contact often lose complex endings in favor of clearer word order.

**4. Middle English: The Norman Conquest**
The biggest turning point came in **1066**, when William of Normandy conquered England. For centuries afterward, the ruling elite used Norman French, the Church and scholars used Latin, and ordinary people continued speaking English.

This created a layered vocabulary:

- Germanic words for everyday life: `cow`, `sheep`, `house`, `work`
- French words for law, government, cuisine, and aristocratic life: `court`, `judge`, `parliament`, `beef`, `mutton`, `beauty`
- Latin words for religion, learning, and abstraction: `scripture`, `education`, `virtue`

By the time of Chaucer in the 14th century, English had become recognizable as **Middle English**, though still quite different from today.

**5. Early Modern English: Printing, Shakespeare, and Sound Change**
Around 1500, English entered its Early Modern phase. Three developments mattered especially.

First, the **printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and grammar, especially around the London dialect.

Second, the **Renaissance** brought many Latin and Greek words into English, especially in science, medicine, philosophy, and literature.

Third, the **Great Vowel Shift** changed the pronunciation of long vowels. This is one reason English spelling often looks strange today: many spellings were fixed before pronunciation finished changing.

Shakespeare and the King James Bible belong to this period. Their language is mostly understandable to modern readers, but still has older forms like `thou`, `thee`, `hath`, and `dost`.

**6. Modern English: Empire, Science, and Global Spread**
From the 17th century onward, English spread through colonization, trade, migration, and later American political, economic, scientific, and cultural power.

English borrowed words from many languages, including:

- Hindi: `shampoo`, `pajamas`, `bungalow`
- Arabic: `algebra`, `coffee`, `sugar`
- Spanish: `canyon`, `ranch`, `tornado`
- Indigenous American languages: `canoe`, `tobacco`, `hammock`
- Japanese: `tsunami`, `karaoke`, `emoji`

Modern English also diversified into many major varieties: British, American, Canadian, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Singaporean, Caribbean Englishes, and many others.

**Why English Looks the Way It Does**
English is unusual because it has a **Germanic grammatical core** but a huge **Romance and Latin vocabulary**.

That is why English often has pairs like:

- `ask` / `inquire`
- `kingly` / `royal`
- `begin` / `commence`
- `help` / `assist`
- `freedom` / `liberty`

The first word is often Germanic and more everyday; the second is often French or Latin and more formal.

**Bottom Line**
English developed through repeated contact: Germanic settlement, Christian Latin, Viking Norse, Norman French, Renaissance scholarship, global empire, and modern international exchange. Its history is not a straight line but a series of layers, which is why English has simple-ish grammar, irregular spelling, and an enormous mixed vocabulary.
