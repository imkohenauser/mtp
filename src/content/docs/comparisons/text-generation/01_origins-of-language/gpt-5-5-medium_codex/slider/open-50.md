English developed through several major historical layers rather than from one clean source.

**1. Germanic Roots**
English began as a **West Germanic language** brought to Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries by Anglo-Saxon groups: Angles, Saxons, and Jutes. Their dialects formed **Old English**, related to modern German, Dutch, and Frisian. Words like `house`, `bread`, `water`, `strong`, `night`, and `mother` come from this Germanic base.

**2. Celtic and Latin Background**
Before English, Britain had Celtic languages. They left relatively few everyday English words, though many place names survive, such as `Avon`, `Kent`, and `Thames`. Latin also entered early through Roman Britain and later Christianity, giving words connected to religion, learning, and administration.

**3. Viking Influence**
From the 8th to 11th centuries, Norse-speaking Vikings settled in parts of Britain. Old Norse strongly affected English vocabulary and grammar. Common words like `sky`, `egg`, `they`, `them`, `their`, `take`, and `law` reflect Norse influence. This contact may also have helped simplify English grammar, since related but different Germanic speakers had to communicate.

**4. Norman French**
The Norman Conquest of 1066 transformed English. For centuries, French was the language of government, law, aristocracy, and high culture in England. English absorbed thousands of French words: `court`, `judge`, `royal`, `beef`, `beauty`, `government`, `parliament`, and many others. This produced a layered vocabulary: Germanic words often feel plain and everyday, while French or Latin words may sound formal or abstract.

**5. Middle English**
Between roughly 1100 and 1500, Old English became **Middle English**. Grammar simplified, word endings weakened, and vocabulary expanded. Chaucer’s *Canterbury Tales* is the famous example: recognizable as English, but still distant from modern usage.

**6. Modern English**
From about 1500 onward, **Modern English** emerged. The printing press helped standardize spelling and grammar, especially around the London dialect. The **Great Vowel Shift** changed pronunciation dramatically, which is one reason English spelling often seems mismatched with speech.

**7. Global Expansion**
From the 17th century onward, British colonialism, trade, science, and later American cultural and economic power made English global. It borrowed widely from languages around the world: `shampoo` from Hindi, `ketchup` from Chinese-related sources, `canyon` from Spanish, `yoga` from Sanskrit, and many more.

So English is basically a Germanic language with heavy Norse, French, Latin, and global influence. Another way to see it is as a language whose core grammar remained Germanic while its vocabulary became unusually hybrid. A related question is whether English’s openness to borrowing made it flexible, or whether historical accident simply made it messy.
