English is a West Germanic language that developed through repeated contact, conquest, migration, and cultural change. Its history is usually divided into four broad periods: Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English.

**Origins**

English began with the Germanic-speaking peoples who migrated to Britain in the 5th century CE after Roman authority collapsed there. These groups are traditionally identified as the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes, from areas around present-day northern Germany, Denmark, and the Netherlands.

Their dialects replaced or pushed aside many of the Celtic languages spoken in much of lowland Britain. The name “English” comes from the Angles: *Englisc* meant the language of the Angles, and *England* meant the land of the Angles.

**Old English, c. 450-1100**

Old English was a Germanic language, closer in structure to Old Frisian, Old Saxon, and Old Norse than to modern English. It had grammatical gender, noun cases, more complex verb endings, and a vocabulary that was mostly Germanic.

A famous example is *Beowulf*, written in Old English. To modern readers, Old English looks almost like a different language:

```text
Hwæt! Wē Gār-Dena in geārdagum...
```

Old English was shaped by several influences:

- **Latin**, first through Roman Britain and later through Christianity after missionaries arrived in the late 6th century.
- **Old Norse**, brought by Viking settlement from the 8th to 11th centuries, especially in northern and eastern England. Norse influence gave English words such as `sky`, `egg`, `they`, `them`, `their`, `take`, and `window`.
- **Celtic languages**, which had less direct vocabulary influence on English than one might expect, though they survived strongly in Wales, Ireland, Scotland, Cornwall, and place names.

**Middle English, c. 1100-1500**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 transformed English. William the Conqueror and the Norman elite spoke Norman French, a variety of Old French. For centuries, England had a layered linguistic society:

- Latin was used in church, scholarship, and official records.
- French was used by the ruling class, law, administration, and high culture.
- English remained the everyday language of most people.

This contact changed English deeply. Thousands of French and Latin words entered the language, especially in law, government, art, religion, cuisine, and social rank: `court`, `judge`, `parliament`, `justice`, `royal`, `beef`, `pork`, `beauty`, `virtue`.

During this period, English grammar simplified. Many Old English case endings disappeared, word order became more important, and grammatical gender largely vanished. By the time of Geoffrey Chaucer in the 14th century, English was recognizably closer to the modern language, though still very different in spelling and pronunciation.

**Early Modern English, c. 1500-1700**

Early Modern English is the language of Shakespeare, the King James Bible, and the Renaissance. Several major developments occurred:

- **The printing press**, introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, helped standardize spelling and grammar, especially around the London dialect.
- **The Great Vowel Shift** changed the pronunciation of long vowels. This is one reason English spelling often seems inconsistent today: spelling became partly fixed while pronunciation kept changing.
- **Renaissance learning** brought many Latin and Greek words into English, such as `education`, `democracy`, `philosophy`, `temperature`, and `encyclopedia`.
- **Exploration and trade** introduced words from many other languages.

Shakespeare’s English is Early Modern English. It is mostly understandable today, but it still contains older pronouns and verb forms like `thou`, `thee`, `hath`, and `dost`.

**Modern English, c. 1700-present**

Modern English continued to expand through science, empire, commerce, migration, and media. The British Empire spread English to North America, the Caribbean, Africa, South Asia, Australia, New Zealand, and elsewhere. In many places, English mixed with local languages, producing new dialects, creoles, and national varieties.

American English developed from colonial English but was also influenced by Indigenous languages, Spanish, Dutch, French, German, West African languages, and later immigrant languages. Words such as `raccoon`, `moose`, `cookie`, `prairie`, `rodeo`, and `jazz` reflect this layered history.

In the 20th and 21st centuries, the United States, global business, aviation, science, computing, entertainment, and the internet helped make English a global lingua franca. Today, more people use English as a second or additional language than as a native language.

**Why English Has So Many Words**

English vocabulary is unusually mixed. It has:

- A Germanic core: `house`, `bread`, `water`, `child`, `love`, `come`, `go`.
- Norse influence: `they`, `sky`, `take`, `same`, `law`.
- French influence: `government`, `court`, `beauty`, `noble`, `crime`.
- Latin and Greek influence: `science`, `literature`, `biology`, `justice`, `manual`.
- Borrowings from many world languages: `tea`, `coffee`, `sugar`, `shampoo`, `bungalow`, `robot`, `karaoke`.

This layered history explains many English word pairs: a plain Germanic word beside a more formal French or Latin one, such as `ask` and `inquire`, `kingly` and `royal`, `begin` and `commence`.

In short, English began as a Germanic language in early medieval Britain, was reshaped by Norse and Norman French, standardized during the printing and Renaissance periods, and then spread globally through empire, migration, trade, science, and modern media.
