### Linguistic Evolution and Milestones

**Old English (450–1150 AD)**
*   **Proto-Germanic Roots:** English descends from the Ingvaeonic (North Sea Germanic) branch.
*   **The Anglo-Saxon Settlement:** The language began as a collection of dialects (Northumbrian, Mercian, West Saxon, and Kentish).
*   **Old Norse Influence:** Following Viking invasions in the 9th century, Old Norse contributed core vocabulary (e.g., *they*, *them*, *their*, *sky*, *egg*) and influenced the simplification of grammatical endings.

**Middle English (1150–1500 AD)**
*   **The Norman Conquest (1066):** After the Battle of Hastings, French became the language of the ruling class and law, while English remained the tongue of the peasantry.
*   **Linguistic Hybridity:** Approximately 10,000 French words entered the lexicon, particularly in law, government, and high culture (e.g., *justice*, *parliament*, *beef* vs. Germanic *cow*).
*   **Grammatical Simplification:** During this period, the complex inflectional system of Old English (cases and genders) largely collapsed, shifting English toward a fixed word order (Subject-Verb-Object).

**Modern English (1500–Present)**
*   **The Great Vowel Shift (c. 1400–1700):** A major phonological change where the pronunciation of long vowels shifted upwards in the mouth (e.g., *bite* shifted from /biːt/ to /baɪt/).
*   **Standardization:** The introduction of the printing press by William Caxton in 1476 and the publication of dictionaries (e.g., Samuel Johnson’s in 1755) standardized spelling and grammar.
*   **Scientific and Colonial Expansion:** The Renaissance added thousands of Latin and Greek roots (e.g., *biological*, *atmosphere*), while the British Empire spread the language globally, incorporating loanwords from hundreds of other languages.

### Core Terminology
*   **Synthetic Language:** A language that expresses grammatical relationships through inflections (suffixes/prefixes). Old English was highly synthetic.
*   **Analytic Language:** A language that expresses relationships through word order and helper words (prepositions). Modern English is primarily analytic.
*   **Inflection:** A change in the form of a word (typically the ending) to express a grammatical function, such as tense, mood, person, number, case, and gender.
