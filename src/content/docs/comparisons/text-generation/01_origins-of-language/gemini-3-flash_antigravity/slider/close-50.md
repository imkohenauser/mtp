### Old English (c. 450–1100 AD)
English originated from a group of West Germanic dialects brought to Britain by the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes in the 5th century. This early form, known as Old English, was highly inflected (similar to Modern German) and used a runic alphabet before transitioning to Latin script. ### Middle English (c. 1100–1500)
The Norman Conquest of 1066 was the most transformative event in English history. French became the language of the ruling class and administration, while English remained the tongue of the common people. Over time, the two merged. English adopted thousands of French loanwords (particularly related to law, cuisine, and government) and lost much of its complex inflectional system. By the time of Geoffrey Chaucer’s *The Canterbury Tales*, the language had become recognizable as Middle English.

### Early Modern English (c. 1500–1800)
The transition to Early Modern English was marked by the Great Vowel Shift— a massive change in pronunciation—and the invention of the printing press, which helped standardize spelling and grammar. The Renaissance brought a surge of Latin and Greek loanwords as scholars rediscovered classical texts. This period is most famous for the works of William Shakespeare and the King James Bible, both of which profoundly influenced the vocabulary and idioms used today.

### Late Modern English (1800–Present)
Late Modern English differs from its predecessor primarily in its vastly expanded vocabulary. The Industrial Revolution created a need for new technical terms, while the expansion of the British Empire introduced words from across the globe (e.g., "shampoo" from Hindi, "bungalow" from Bengali). In the 20th and 21st centuries, American English and the rise of digital technology have further accelerated the language’s evolution, making it the most widely spoken lingua franca in the world.

The history of English is a testament to its remarkable adaptability. Its ability to absorb foreign vocabulary while simplifying its structural complexity has allowed it to thrive across diverse cultures and eras.
