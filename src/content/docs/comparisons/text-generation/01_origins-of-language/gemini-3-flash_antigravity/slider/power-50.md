### 1. The Germanic Foundation: Old English (450–1100 AD)
**English originated as a collection of West Germanic dialects brought to Britain by invaders from present-day Germany and Denmark.** These tribes—the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes—displaced the indigenous Celtic languages, establishing "Englisc" as the dominant tongue.

*   **Structure:** Old English was a highly inflected language with complex case systems and genders, similar to modern German.

### 2. The Norman Transformation: Middle English (1100–1500 AD)
**The Norman Conquest of 1066 is the single most transformative event in English history, introducing a massive French vocabulary that bifurcated the language's register.** For nearly 300 years, French was the language of the elite, while English remained the tongue of the peasantry.

*   **Lexical Doubling:** This period created the unique English phenomenon of synonyms with different registers. For example, the Germanic *cow, pig,* and *sheep* (the animals tended by peasants) gained French counterparts *beef, pork,* and *mutton* (the meat eaten by the Norman nobility).
*   **Structural Simplification:** During this era, English lost the majority of its Germanic inflections, shifting from a synthetic language (relying on word endings) to an analytic one (relying on word order and prepositions).

English is a Germanic language that has been "re-lexified" by Latinate influence, but its functional heart remains Saxon.
