### 1. Proto-Indo-European and Germanic Origins
English belongs to the **Indo-European** family of languages. Specifically, it is a **West Germanic** language. Before the 5th century, the precursors to English were spoken by tribes in what is now Denmark and northern Germany. These speakers shared common Germanic features, such as specific consonant shifts (Grimm's Law) and a simplified system of verb tenses.

### 2. Old English (450–1100 AD)
English began when three Germanic tribes—the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes—migrated to the British Isles in the 5th century.
*   **Anglo-Saxon Foundation:** Old English was highly inflected (similar to Modern German), with complex grammar and gendered nouns.
*   **Viking Influence:** From the 8th to the 11th centuries, Old Norse-speaking Vikings invaded. This introduced common words like *sky*, *egg*, *get*, and *take*, and began the process of simplifying English grammar.

### 3. Middle English (1100–1500 AD)
The turning point for English was the **Norman Conquest of 1066**.
*   **French Influence:** For nearly 300 years, French was the language of the ruling class and law, while English remained the language of the peasantry. This "linguistic layering" gave English its dual vocabulary (e.g., the Germanic *cow* vs. the French-derived *beef*).
*   **Grammar Simplification:** During this period, English lost most of its complex case endings, moving toward the fixed word order (Subject-Verb-Object) we use today.

### 4. Early Modern English (1500–1700 AD)
This era was defined by two major events that transformed how English sounded and was perceived.
*   **The Great Vowel Shift:** A massive change in pronunciation occurred where long vowel sounds shifted "upward" in the mouth. This is why English spelling often doesn't match its pronunciation (e.g., the "ee" sound in *meet*).
*   **The Renaissance and Printing:** The invention of the printing press by William Caxton in 1476 helped standardize the London dialect. The Renaissance brought thousands of Latin and Greek loanwords into the language to describe new scientific and philosophical concepts.

### 5. Modern English (1700–Present)
By the 18th century, the grammar and spelling of English were largely fixed.
*   **Standardization:** The publication of Samuel Johnson’s *Dictionary* (1755) and early grammars established "correct" usage.
*   **Global Expansion:** The growth of the British Empire and, later, the cultural and economic influence of the United States spread English worldwide. Today, it continues to evolve rapidly, incorporating technical jargon and slang from diverse global cultures.

**Key Assumption:** This summary focuses on the "external" history (migration and events) and "internal" history (linguistic changes) of the dominant dialects that became Standard English, though many regional dialects persisted throughout this timeline.
