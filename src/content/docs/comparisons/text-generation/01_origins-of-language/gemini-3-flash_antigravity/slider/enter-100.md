### Phase I: Old English (450–1150)
*Requirement: Establishing the Germanic core.*

1.  **Germanic Invasion**: Following the Roman withdrawal from Britain, Germanic tribes (Angles, Saxons, and Jutes) settled in the 5th century. Their dialects merged into "Anglo-Saxon," the root of Old English.
2.  **Linguistic Structure**: Unlike modern English, Old English was highly inflected (similar to modern German), using complex cases, genders, and verb conjugations.
3.  **Viking Impact**: From the late 8th century, Norse invaders introduced Old Norse words and began a process of grammatical simplification. Common words like *sky*, *get*, *take*, and the pronoun *they* are Norse in origin.

### Phase II: Middle English (1150–1500)
*Design: The French-Latin overlay.*

1.  **The Norman Conquest (1066)**: William the Conqueror brought Norman French to England. For nearly 300 years, French was the language of the elite, while English remained the tongue of the common people.
2.  **Vocabulary Expansion**: English absorbed over 10,000 French words, particularly in law (*court*, *jury*), cuisine (*beef*, *mutton*), and government (*parliament*, *sovereign*).
3.  **Grammatical Shift**: The complex inflectional system of Old English largely collapsed, shifting the language toward its modern fixed word order (Subject-Verb-Object).

*Implementation: The Great Vowel Shift and the Printing Press.*

1.  **The Great Vowel Shift**: A massive change in the pronunciation of long vowels occurred. This transition is the reason English spelling often seems disconnected from its modern sound.
2.  **The Printing Press**: Introduced to England by William Caxton in 1476, the press helped standardize spelling and established the London dialect as the national standard.
3.  **The Renaissance**: A surge in classical learning led to "inkhorn terms"—thousands of new words borrowed directly from Latin and Greek to describe science, philosophy, and the arts.

3.  **Global Lingua Franca**: In the 20th and 21st centuries, American cultural influence and the digital revolution solidified English as the primary language of international science, aviation, and the internet.
