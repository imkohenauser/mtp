The English language is a West Germanic language that originated from the dialects brought to Britain by Germanic invaders in the 5th century AD. Its development is characterized by three major phases of linguistic evolution and significant external influences from Latin, Old Norse, and French.

### Historical Periods of English

The history of English is conventionally divided into four primary periods based on structural and vocabulary shifts.

| Period | Dates | Primary Linguistic Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Old English** | 450–1150 AD | Highly inflected (synthetic); heavily Germanic vocabulary; use of the runic and then Latin alphabets. |

### Key Linguistic Definitions

*   **Proto-Indo-European (PIE):** The hypothesized common ancestor of the Indo-European languages, including English, Latin, Greek, and Sanskrit.
*   **West Germanic:** A branch of the Germanic language family that includes English, German, and Dutch.
*   **The Great Vowel Shift:** A series of changes in the pronunciation of English long vowels between the 15th and 18th centuries, which is the primary reason Modern English spelling often does not match its pronunciation.
*   **Inflection:** The modification of a word (usually via suffixes) to express grammatical categories such as tense, case, or number.

### Stages of Development

#### 1. Old English (450–1150)
English began when Germanic tribes—the **Angles, Saxons, and Jutes**—migrated from present-day Germany and Denmark to Britain. They spoke closely related dialects that merged into what we now call Old English.
*   **Viking Influence:** In the 8th and 9th centuries, Norse invasions introduced Old Norse words into the language (e.g., *sky, leg, they, their*).
*   **Literary Support:** The epic poem *Beowulf* is the most significant surviving work of this period.

#### 2. Middle English (1150–1470)
The **Norman Conquest of 1066** introduced Old French as the language of the ruling class, while English remained the language of the common people.
*   **Bilingual Influence:** Over 10,000 French words entered English, particularly in fields of law, government, and cuisine (e.g., *court, judge, beef, pork*).
*   **Literary Support:** Geoffrey Chaucer’s *The Canterbury Tales* reflects the transition toward a more recognizable form of English.

#### 3. Early Modern English (1470–1700)
This period was marked by two transformative events: the invention of the **printing press** (William Caxton, 1476) and the **Great Vowel Shift**.
*   **Standardization:** The printing press helped stabilize spelling and grammar.
*   **Renaissance:** An explosion of new words derived from Greek and Latin occurred as scholars rediscovered classical texts.
*   **Literary Support:** The works of William Shakespeare and the King James Bible (1611) provided the foundational vocabulary and idioms for Modern English.

#### 4. Modern English (1700–Present)
The primary difference between Early Modern and Modern English is the expansion of vocabulary.
*   **Industrial Revolution:** The need for new words for new technologies led to a massive increase in technical terminology.
*   **Colonialism:** As Britain expanded its empire, English adopted words from languages worldwide (e.g., *shampoo* from Hindi, *kangaroo* from Guugu Yimithirr).
*   **Standardization:** The publication of Samuel Johnson’s *A Dictionary of the English Language* (1755) and later the *Oxford English Dictionary* fixed the meanings and spellings of the lexicon.
