The history of the English language is categorized into four primary periods: Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, and Modern English.

### Old English (450–1100 AD)
English originated from West Germanic dialects brought to Britain by tribes including the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes. This period, known as Anglo-Saxon, utilized a complex inflectional system and a vocabulary primarily of Germanic origin. The Viking invasions starting in the 8th century introduced Old Norse influences, affecting basic vocabulary and grammar.

### Middle English (1100–1500 AD)
Following the Norman Conquest in 1066, French became the language of the ruling class and administration, while Latin remained the language of the church and scholarship. Over several centuries, thousands of French and Latin words were integrated into English. Grammatical structures simplified as many inflectional endings were lost. The works of Geoffrey Chaucer characterize the late Middle English period.

### Early Modern English (1500–1800 AD)
The transition to Early Modern English was marked by the Great Vowel Shift, a major change in the pronunciation of long vowels. The invention of the printing press by William Caxton in 1476 facilitated the standardization of spelling and grammar. The Renaissance introduced a large number of Greek and Latin loanwords. William Shakespeare’s writings represent a peak of linguistic development in this era.

### Modern English (1800–Present)
Modern English is characterized by further expansion of vocabulary through industrialization, scientific advancement, and the influence of the British Empire. This period saw the establishment of English as a global lingua franca. Differences in regional dialects emerged, leading to variations such as American English and Australian English, while the core grammar remained relatively stable.
