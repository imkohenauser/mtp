````markdown
### Old English (c. 450–1100 AD)
*   **Origins:** Developed from Ingvaeonic (North Sea Germanic) dialects spoken by the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes who migrated to Roman Britain.
*   **Influences:** Primarily West Germanic. Significant lexical influence from Old Norse due to Viking raids and settlement (Danelaw). Celtic influence was minimal.
*   **Characteristics:** Highly inflected grammar with four or five cases, three genders, and complex verbal morphology.

### Middle English (c. 1100–1500 AD)
*   **Origins:** Followed the Norman Conquest of 1066. 
*   **Influences:** Old French became the language of the ruling class, administration, and law, contributing approximately 10,000 words to English. Latin remained the language of the Church and scholarship.
*   **Characteristics:** Gradual loss of many Old English inflections (leveling of endings). The syntax became more reliant on word order. The Great Vowel Shift began late in this period (c. 1400).

### Early Modern English (c. 1500–1700 AD)
*   **Origins:** Coincided with the English Renaissance and the introduction of the printing press (William Caxton, 1476).
*   **Influences:** Massive influx of Latin and Greek loanwords (inkhorn terms). Increased literacy and the publication of the King James Bible and the works of William Shakespeare standardized the language.
*   **Characteristics:** Completion of the Great Vowel Shift, resulting in modern pronunciation of long vowels. Stabilization of spelling.

### Modern English (c. 1700–Present)
*   **Origins:** Defined by the expansion of the British Empire and the Industrial Revolution.
*   **Influences:** Global loanwords from over 350 languages. Scientific and technological advancement required a vast new vocabulary.
*   **Characteristics:** Shift toward American English as a dominant global variety in the 20th century. Minimal remaining inflectional morphology. Extremely large and diverse lexicon.
````
