The development of the English language is divided into four major phases:

### 1. Pre-English and Old English (c. 450–1100)
English originated from the dialects of three Germanic tribes (Angles, Saxons, and Jutes) who migrated to Britain in the 5th century. This "Anglo-Saxon" language replaced the indigenous Celtic languages in most of the region.
- **Linguistic characteristics**: Highly inflected (using case endings for nouns and adjectives) with a core Germanic vocabulary.
- **External influence**: The 8th-century Viking invasions introduced Old Norse terms (e.g., *sky*, *give*, *they*), which began the process of simplifying the inflectional system.

### 2. Middle English (c. 1100–1500)
The Norman Conquest of 1066 introduced Old French as the language of the ruling class, administration, and law.
- **Linguistic characteristics**: A massive influx of French and Latin vocabulary (approximately 10,000 words). English shifted from a synthetic (inflected) language to an analytic language, relying on word order and prepositions rather than case endings.
- **Literary milestone**: Geoffrey Chaucer’s *The Canterbury Tales* demonstrated the emergence of a standardized London dialect.

### 3. Early Modern English (c. 1500–1800)
This period was defined by two major transitions: the Great Vowel Shift and the invention of the printing press.
- **The Great Vowel Shift**: A massive change in the pronunciation of long vowels (e.g., the "ee" sound in *sheep* shifted from an "ay" sound). This created the discrepancy between modern English spelling and pronunciation.
- **The Printing Press**: William Caxton brought the printing press to England in 1476, which stabilized the London dialect as the standard for written English and fixed spelling.
- **External influence**: The Renaissance brought a second wave of Latin and Greek loanwords for scientific and philosophical concepts.

### 4. Modern English (c. 1800–Present)
Modern English is characterized by the expansion of the British Empire and the subsequent dominance of American cultural and technological influence.
- **Linguistic characteristics**: Integration of vocabulary from across the globe. The language has become increasingly standardized through mass media, while simultaneously diversifying into various global dialects (World Englishes).
- **Technological influence**: The Industrial Revolution and the Digital Age have necessitated the creation or repurposing of thousands of technical terms.
