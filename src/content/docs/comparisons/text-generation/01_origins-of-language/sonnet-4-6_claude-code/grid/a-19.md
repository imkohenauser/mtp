Anglo-Saxon. Which is actually two words doing the work of one, which somehow feels appropriate for a language that has always been this acquisitive creature, this magpie tongue picking up shiny things from everywhere it lands.

You start with Germanic tribes — Angles, Saxons, Jutes — crossing from what is now Denmark and northern Germany into Britain around the 5th century. But then you think about the Britons who were already there, and the Romans who had been there before that, and suddenly "origins" becomes a strange word because every origin has its own origin underneath it, turtles all the way down.

Old English sounds almost nothing like what we speak now. *Hwæt!* — the opening word of Beowulf — is sometimes translated as "Listen!" but might be more like a clearing of the throat, an attention-getter with no clean modern equivalent. The whole poem is in this dense, alliterative Germanic form that feels closer to Norwegian than to anything you'd recognize as English.

Then the Vikings arrived, and that's fascinating because instead of Old English simply dying, the two languages — Old Norse and Old English — were similar enough that speakers half-understood each other, and they just... merged pragmatically. Pronouns like "they," "them," "their" are Norse. So is the word "sky." And "egg." There's something quietly funny about the sky and eggs both being Viking contributions.

And then 1066, which every English schoolchild knows, though knowing a year is different from knowing what it meant — French flooding in, not replacing English but sitting on top of it, creating this strange sociolinguistic layering where the animals in the field kept their Anglo-Saxon names (cow, pig, sheep) and the meat on the table took French ones (beef, pork, mutton). The peasants tended the animals; the Norman lords ate them. The language still carries that class division like a scar.

Incidentally, this is partly why English has so many synonyms. "Begin," "commence," "start" — Germanic, French, Old Norse, more or less. You can shade the same idea with different social registers just by choosing your etymology. Lawyers still reach for Latin-French hybrids. Poets often reach back to the Germanic.

Shakespeare is Middle English shading into Early Modern. The King James Bible calcified certain constructions into permanence. Then the printing press — Caxton setting up shop in Westminster in 1476 — standardizes spelling just as the Great Vowel Shift is quietly rearranging how everything sounds, which is why English spelling makes so little phonetic sense now; it fossilized at the wrong moment.

And from there it just keeps going, expanding with empire, contracting with it, fracturing into American and Australian and Singaporean and Nigerian Englishes, each one its own argument about what the language fundamentally is. Some of them are converging toward global media English. Some are deliberately not.

There's a version of this story that's triumphalist, about how English became the world's lingua franca. There's another version that's a story about displacement and loss — all the Welsh and Irish and Scots Gaelic speakers for whom English wasn't an acquisition but an imposition. Both stories are true at the same time, which is uncomfortable, and maybe that's where you'd want to sit with it for a while rather than reaching for a tidy ending.
