**Origins and Historical Development of the English Language**

**Pre-History: Indo-European Roots (~3000 BCE)**
English descends from Proto-Indo-European (PIE), a reconstructed language spoken on the Pontic steppe. PIE gave rise to the Germanic branch, from which English ultimately derives.

**Old English / Anglo-Saxon (450–1150 CE)**
Germanic tribes — Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — migrated from present-day Denmark and northern Germany to Britain after the Roman withdrawal. Their dialects merged into what is called Old English. Viking raids and settlement (793–1000 CE) introduced Norse vocabulary. Old English was a heavily inflected language, structurally distant from modern English. Example: *Hwæt! We Gardena in geardagum…* (Beowulf).

**Middle English (1150–1500 CE)**
The Norman Conquest of 1066 introduced a massive influx of Old French and Latin vocabulary into English. The ruling class spoke Norman French; the common population spoke English. Over two centuries, the languages blended. Inflectional endings simplified. Geoffrey Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (~1390) is the central literary monument of this period.

**Early Modern English (1500–1700 CE)**
The printing press (introduced to England by Caxton, 1476) standardized spelling and accelerated literacy. The Renaissance brought Latin and Greek borrowings. The Great Vowel Shift (1400–1700) systematically altered long vowel pronunciation. The King James Bible (1611) and Shakespeare's works (1590s–1610s) fixed literary norms.

**Modern English (1700–present)**
The British Empire spread English globally, absorbing vocabulary from hundreds of contact languages. American English diverged as a distinct variety in the 18th–19th centuries. The 20th century produced further standardization through broadcasting and, later, the internet. Current English has the largest vocabulary of any language, estimated at over one million words.
