## Origins and Historical Development of the English Language

English belongs to the **West Germanic branch** of the Indo-European language family. Its development is conventionally divided into four periods.

---

### Old English (c. 450–1150 CE)

**Origin event:** Anglo-Saxon tribes (Angles, Saxons, Jutes) migrated from the North Sea coast of present-day Germany and Denmark to Britain beginning around 449 CE, displacing the Celtic-speaking Britons.

The resulting language — called *Englisc* — was a heavily inflected Germanic tongue. Nouns had four grammatical cases; verbs conjugated by person and number. The vocabulary was almost entirely Germanic.

Key source texts: *Beowulf* (c. 700–1000 CE), the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle.

---

### Middle English (c. 1150–1500)

**Origin event:** The Norman Conquest of 1066. William the Conqueror installed a French-speaking ruling class, flooding English with Old French and Latin vocabulary — especially in law, religion, cuisine, and governance.

Critical structural change: the inflectional case system collapsed. Word order (Subject–Verb–Object) replaced case endings as the primary grammatical signal. This is the single most important structural shift in the language's history.

Key source text: Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (c. 1390).

---

### Early Modern English (c. 1500–1700)

**Two defining events:**

| Event | Effect |
|---|---|
| Printing press (Caxton, 1476) | Standardized spelling around the London dialect |
| The Great Vowel Shift (c. 1400–1700) | Systematic raising of long vowels, explaining most spelling/pronunciation mismatches today (e.g., *name* once rhymed with *comma*) |

The Renaissance also drove deliberate Latin and Greek borrowing for scientific and philosophical vocabulary (*atmosphere*, *encyclopedia*, *skeleton*).

Key source text: Shakespeare's plays; the King James Bible (1611).

---

### Modern English (c. 1700–present)

Grammatical change slowed dramatically. The primary engine of change shifted to **vocabulary expansion** via:

- **Colonial contact** — borrowings from hundreds of languages (*jungle* ← Hindi, *tobacco* ← Taino, *kangaroo* ← Guugu Yimithirr)
- **Scientific coinage** — systematic Latin/Greek compounding (*oxygen*, *photograph*, *semiconductor*)
- **American divergence** — distinct spellings and lexical choices codified by Noah Webster's *An American Dictionary* (1828)

---

**Core takeaway:** English grammar is fundamentally Germanic; its vocabulary is roughly 30% Germanic, 30% French/Norman, 30% Latin/Greek, with the remainder from global contact languages. The Norman Conquest is the single event most responsible for this hybrid character.
