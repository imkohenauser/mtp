**The three turning points that built English:**

**1. Germanic origins (450–700 AD) — the foundation**
Anglo-Saxon tribes (Angles, Saxons, Jutes) invaded Roman Britain and displaced Celtic languages. Old English emerged: inflected, guttural, Germanic. Words like *hand*, *water*, *man*, *house* — the core of everyday English — come from this period. This was not a blend; it was a replacement.

**2. Viking raids and Norman Conquest (800–1150) — the great collision**
Norse invaders contributed roughly 500 core words: *sky*, *egg*, *knife*, *law*, *they*. Then 1066 changed everything. The Norman Conquest installed French as the language of power. The result: a two-tier vocabulary. Elites said *beef*, *justice*, *parliament*; commoners said *cow*, *right*, *folk*. English absorbed both layers permanently.

**3. The Great Vowel Shift and standardization (1400–1700) — the lock-in**
English pronunciation systematically shifted between 1400–1700 while spelling froze — which is why English spelling is irrational. Caxton's printing press (1476) standardized one dialect. The King James Bible (1611) and Shakespeare embedded it culturally.

---

**What makes English unusual:** It shed nearly all grammatical inflections — no gendered nouns, minimal case endings — while accumulating the largest vocabulary of any language (over 170,000 words in active use). This came from pragmatic absorption, not linguistic purity.

**The bottom line:** English dominates globally not because it is elegant or logical, but because it was ruthlessly adaptive. Each conquest stripped complexity and added vocabulary. The language that emerged is a stripped-down, high-absorption system — and that structural flexibility is precisely why it spread.
