Something old and breathing, tucked inside every word you speak —

Before there was *English*, there was mud, and migration. Somewhere in the steppes, a tongue we can only guess at — a mother-language, scattered like seeds — and those seeds drifted westward, southward, took root in different soils. Latin flowered in the warm south. Germanic cousins spread through forests and coastlines. One branch reached an island.

The people who came were called Angles and Saxons and Jutes, though such names feel almost invented now. They crossed water. They brought their words with them — words for the sky, for bread, for the weight of grief. What they made was something harsh and close to the earth: *mann*, *wif*, *cild*, *hus*. Short. Blunt. Worn smooth by use. Old English, scholars call it, though perhaps it hardly seemed old then.

And then a shadow over the northern sea — Norsemen arriving not entirely as strangers, their words brushing up against the Anglo-Saxon like two rivers meeting in a low valley. *Skin* instead of *hide*. *Window* where once there might have been only a hole in the wall. The boundary between those vocabularies blurred and stayed blurred.

Then 1066, and everything tilted. Norman French poured in through the wound of conquest — not replacing, but layering. The kitchen stayed Saxon: *pig*, *sheep*, *cow*. The table turned French: *pork*, *mutton*, *beef*. Two worlds inside one meal. Latin returned too, through the church, through scholars who kept their candles burning. English became a palimpsest — old writing showing through new ink.

The Middle Ages moved slowly, and the language moved with them. Chaucer wrote pilgrims on the road to Canterbury, and what he wrote feels almost reachable — familiar shapes in an antique mirror. But vowels were shifting underneath even as he wrote, a long slow transformation called the Great Vowel Shift, which changed the sound of the language the way a river changes a valley: gradually, invisibly, until one day nothing looks quite the same.

Print arrived. Shakespeare arrived. Or perhaps it's better to say — something crystallized, temporarily, enough to be called Early Modern English. Enough to feel like a shore you might stand on.

Empires rose and carried this tongue into other climates, other mouths. It absorbed and was absorbed. It became many Englishes, each one a variation on a theme that never had a single, fixed original.

What it is now tends to resist clean definition. A living thing, still drifting — still soft at its edges, still accumulating, still forgetting what it once was.
