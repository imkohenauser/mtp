## ① Proto-Indo-European and Germanic Origins (~3000–500 BCE)

English descends from **Proto-Indo-European (PIE)**, a reconstructed language spoken around 4000–3000 BCE on the Pontic-Caspian steppe (modern Ukraine/Russia). PIE is the ancestor of most European and many South Asian languages.

From PIE, a branch called **Proto-Germanic** emerged around 500 BCE, distinguished by a systematic consonant shift known as **Grimm's Law** (e.g., PIE *p* → Germanic *f*: Latin *pater* → English *father*). Proto-Germanic eventually split into North Germanic (Old Norse, Swedish), East Germanic (Gothic), and **West Germanic** — the branch that would produce English, Dutch, and German.

---

## ② Old English (Anglo-Saxon) — 450–1150 CE

**Trigger:** The migration of Angles, Saxons, and Jutes from northern Germany and Denmark to Britain beginning around 450 CE, displacing or absorbing Celtic-speaking Britons.

**Character:** Old English was a fully inflected language with grammatical gender, four noun cases, and complex verb conjugations. It bore little surface resemblance to Modern English.

Key influences:
- **Latin** — two waves: early Christian missionaries (597 CE, Augustine of Canterbury) introduced ecclesiastical vocabulary (*bishop*, *monk*, *school*).
- **Old Norse** — Viking raids and settlement (789–1066 CE) contributed everyday vocabulary (*sky*, *they*, *knife*, *window*) and simplified some inflectional endings through contact leveling.
- **Milestone text:** *Beowulf* (~700–1000 CE) is the canonical Old English literary monument.

---

## ③ Middle English — 1150–1500 CE

**Trigger:** The Norman Conquest (1066 CE). William the Conqueror's French-speaking Norman court imposed **Old French** as the language of law, administration, and prestige, while English continued as the vernacular.

**Result — a stratified vocabulary:** English acquired thousands of French and Latin loanwords layered over Germanic roots. This produced the characteristic Modern English doublets:

| Germanic (informal) | French (formal) |
|---|---|
| *ask* | *inquire* |
| *begin* | *commence* |
| *cow* (animal) | *beef* (food) |

**Structural shift:** The collision of Old English and Old French accelerated the **loss of inflectional endings**, pushing English toward the analytic (word-order-dependent) grammar it has today.

**Milestone text:** Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (~1390 CE) represents mature Middle English.

---

## ④ Early Modern and Modern English — 1500–present

**Early Modern English (1500–1700):**
- The **printing press** (Caxton, 1476) standardized spelling and accelerated literacy.
- The **Great Vowel Shift** (1400–1700) systematically raised long vowel sounds, explaining why English spelling and pronunciation diverged so dramatically (e.g., *name* once rhymed with modern *calm*).
- The **Renaissance** flooded the language with Latin and Greek vocabulary for science, medicine, and philosophy (*democracy*, *anatomy*, *hypothesis*).
- The **King James Bible** (1611) and Shakespeare's works (~1590–1613) fixed many idioms and phrases still in use.

**Modern English (1700–present):**
- **British colonial expansion** seeded loanwords from hundreds of languages: *jungle* (Hindi), *tattoo* (Tahitian), *algebra* (Arabic), *chocolate* (Nahuatl).
- **American English** diverged from British English in vocabulary and pronunciation, with Noah Webster's 1828 dictionary codifying spelling differences (*color* vs. *colour*).
- The **20th–21st centuries** brought global English as a lingua franca, with major varieties — Indian, Caribbean, African, Australian — each contributing neologisms and grammatical patterns.

---

**Summary:** English is the product of four major contact events — Germanic migration, Viking settlement, Norman conquest, and global colonialism — each of which left a distinct structural and lexical layer. The result is a language unusually rich in synonyms, with minimal inflection but complex orthography, and a vocabulary drawn from over 350 source languages.
