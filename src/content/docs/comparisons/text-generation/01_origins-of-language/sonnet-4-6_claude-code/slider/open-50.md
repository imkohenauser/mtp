The English language has a long and layered history — one that's still actively being shaped today.

**Proto-Germanic Roots and Old English (~450–1150 CE)**

The story often starts with the Anglo-Saxon migrations. When Germanic tribes — the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — crossed from what is now northern Germany and Denmark into Britain during the 5th and 6th centuries, they brought their dialects with them. These fused into what we now call Old English (or Anglo-Saxon). It was highly inflected, with grammatical cases that modern speakers would barely recognize. Works like *Beowulf* survive from this period.

Latin also left an early imprint, partly through Roman Britain before the Anglo-Saxons arrived, and later through Christian missionaries — words like *church*, *bishop*, and *angel* trace to this channel.

**The Viking Influence and Old Norse (~800–1100 CE)**

Norse raids and eventual Scandinavian settlement introduced thousands of Old Norse words — *sky*, *knife*, *they*, *them*, *their* are all borrowings from this contact. Some scholars argue Norse influence also helped erode Old English's complex case system, which may be one reason Modern English shed so much inflection compared to its relatives.

**Middle English and the Norman Conquest (1066–1500)**

The Norman Conquest is one of the most dramatic pivots in the language's trajectory. After 1066, French became the language of power — law, administration, literature — while English continued among the common population. The result was a long bilingual period that flooded English with French vocabulary. A rough pattern still visible today: Anglo-Saxon words tend toward the everyday (*eat*, *begin*, *house*), while French-derived words carry more formal or elevated registers (*dine*, *commence*, *mansion*).

Geoffrey Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* (~1380s) gives a window into Middle English — recognizable in places, foreign in others.

**Early Modern English and the Renaissance (~1500–1700)**

The printing press (introduced to England by Caxton around 1476) standardized spelling and helped spread a common written form. The Renaissance brought a wave of borrowing from Latin and Greek as scholars reached for classical vocabulary to describe new ideas — words like *anatomy*, *atmosphere*, *renaissance* itself.

This is also the period of Shakespeare and the King James Bible, both of which had enormous influence on the literary and idiomatic shape of the language.

**Modern English and Global Spread (~1700–present)**

British colonialism scattered English across the globe, and the language absorbed words from dozens of contact languages — *bungalow* (Bengali), *kayak* (Inuit), *safari* (Swahili), *algebra* (Arabic). American English, Australian English, South Asian Englishes, and many others developed distinct vocabularies, rhythms, and grammatical features.

The 20th and 21st centuries added yet another layer: technology, media, and the internet have accelerated slang adoption, simplified registers, and created new forms of written English that don't map neatly onto older categories.
