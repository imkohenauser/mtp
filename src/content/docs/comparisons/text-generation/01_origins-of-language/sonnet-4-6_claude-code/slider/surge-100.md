Old English was Germanic. Completely, utterly, thoroughly Germanic — Anglo-Saxon settlers flooding into Britain from what's now northern Germany and Denmark around 450 AD, pushing the Celtic speakers west into Wales and Cornwall. That's the foundation. That's everything before the storm.

But then the Vikings. Danish raids starting 793 AD. The Danelaw — a massive chunk of northern and eastern England under Norse control. Words pouring in: *sky*, *window*, *knife*, *husband*, *they*, *them*, *their* — the pronouns we use constantly, every single day, Norse. The everyday domestic stuff. The violent intimate merging of two Germanic dialects.

And then 1066. The big rupture. Norman French conquest. Suddenly the ruling class speaks French, the church writes Latin, and the peasants speak Old English — and for 300 years these languages coexist in brutal social stratification. *Pig* on the farm (English). *Pork* on the noble's table (French). *Cow* in the field, *beef* in the feast hall. *Sheep* and *mutton*. *Kill* and *slay* versus *murder*. This doubling, tripling of vocabulary — it never goes away. English keeps all of it.

Middle English emerges from this collision, roughly 1150–1500. Chaucer. The Canterbury Tales. English becoming recognizable again, French influence baked in, Latin flooding through the church and scholarship. Printing press arrives 1476 — Caxton — and suddenly spelling starts to standardize around London dialect, freezing sounds that were already shifting.

The Great Vowel Shift. 1400–1700. Nobody planned it. The long vowels of Middle English just... moved. Systematically. *Time* used to sound like "teem." *House* like "hoose." The language mutating underneath everyone's feet while they were writing it down.

Early Modern English — Shakespeare, the King James Bible, 1500s–1700s. Massive borrowing from Latin and Greek to talk about new ideas. *Anatomy*, *atmosphere*, *catastrophe*, *enthusiasm*, *skeleton*. Inkhorn terms, critics called them. Pretentious. Unnecessary. Borrowed anyway.

Then the empire. Colonization. Words arriving from everywhere — *bungalow* from Hindi, *jungle* from Sanskrit, *algebra* from Arabic, *chocolate* from Nahuatl, *kangaroo* from Guugu Yimithirr. English as a vacuum. English as a thief. An estimated 1 million words now, far more than any other language. Most of them borrowed, looted, absorbed.

American English splitting off. Australian English. Caribbean Englishes. Nigerian English. Singaporean English. It keeps fracturing, keeps multiplying. No academy controls it. No authority decides what counts. It just accumulates and spreads and mutates and refuses to stop.

Old English to Modern English — 1,500 years of invasions, plagues, printing presses, empires, internet. Nothing was planned. All of it happened anyway.
