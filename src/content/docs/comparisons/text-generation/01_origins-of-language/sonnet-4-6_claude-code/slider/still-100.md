**Origins and Historical Development of the English Language**

**Proto-Germanic roots (~500 BCE)**
English belongs to the Indo-European language family, descending through the Germanic branch.

**Old English (450–1150 CE)**
Anglo-Saxon tribes — Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — migrated from continental Europe to Britain, displacing the Celtic-speaking population. Their dialects merged into Old English. Viking invasions (8th–11th centuries) introduced Old Norse vocabulary. Texts: *Beowulf*, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle.

**Middle English (1150–1500)**
The Norman Conquest (1066) imposed French as the language of court and administration. English absorbed thousands of Latin and French words. Over two centuries, Old English grammar simplified substantially — grammatical gender and most inflectional endings eroded. Texts: Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales*.

**Early Modern English (1500–1700)**
The Renaissance brought mass Latin and Greek borrowing for scientific and literary vocabulary. The printing press (introduced to England c. 1476) standardized spelling. The Great Vowel Shift (1400–1700) systematically altered long vowel pronunciation. Texts: the King James Bible, Shakespeare.

**Modern English (1700–present)**
British colonialism spread English globally, spawning distinct regional varieties (American, Australian, Indian, etc.). The Industrial Revolution and later the digital era produced continuous vocabulary expansion. English absorbed loanwords from hundreds of languages worldwide.
