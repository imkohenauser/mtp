## Origins: Proto-Germanic to Old English (450–1100 CE)

English descends from the West Germanic dialects brought to Britain by the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes who migrated from what is now Denmark and northern Germany starting around 450 CE, displacing the existing Celtic languages (which survive today as Welsh, Irish, and Scottish Gaelic) to the fringes.

Old English — exemplified by *Beowulf* — was a fully inflected language: nouns had four cases, verbs conjugated for person/number, and word order was flexible because endings carried grammatical meaning. About 30% of core modern English vocabulary traces to this period: *man, woman, child, eat, drink, sleep, love, earth, heaven.*

Viking raids (793 CE onward) and settlement added a significant Old Norse layer, responsible for roughly 500 high-frequency words — *sky, skin, egg, knife, take, want, they/them/their* (the third-person plural pronouns are entirely Norse, not Old English).

---

## The Decisive Break: Middle English (1100–1500 CE)

**The Norman Conquest of 1066 is the single most important event in English linguistic history.** William the Conqueror's court spoke Old Norman French, which became the language of law, government, the church, and the aristocracy for roughly 300 years.

The result: a systematic vocabulary split that persists today.

| Domain | Germanic word | French/Latin word |
|---|---|---|
| Livestock | cow, pig, sheep | beef, pork, mutton |
| Social hierarchy | king | sovereign |
| Legal | guilt | felony |
| Intellectual | think | consider |

Critically, English also lost most of its inflectional endings during this period — partly through contact with French speakers who didn't use them. Word order became fixed (Subject-Verb-Object) to compensate. By Chaucer's time (late 1300s), English looked recognizably like a simplified, massively French-enriched version of Old English.

---

## Standardization and Expansion: Early Modern English (1500–1700 CE)

Three forces shaped Early Modern English:

1. **The printing press (1476, Caxton)** — standardized spelling and spread the London dialect as the prestige form.
2. **The Renaissance** — scholars borrowed heavily from Latin and Greek to name new concepts: *democracy, atmosphere, skeleton, telescope*. About 28% of English vocabulary is now Latinate, much of it from this period.
3. **The King James Bible (1611) and Shakespeare** — between them, they coined or first recorded thousands of phrases still in use, and established a literary standard.

---

## Global English: The Colonial Period to Present (1600–now)

British colonial expansion spread English to North America, Australia, South Asia, and Africa, where it absorbed local vocabulary: *jungle, bungalow, avatar* (Hindi); *kangaroo, boomerang* (Aboriginal Australian); *jazz, okra* (West African languages via enslaved people).

The result is a language with the **largest vocabulary of any on Earth** (Oxford English Dictionary tracks ~600,000 words), minimal inflection, highly inconsistent spelling (reflecting five centuries of phonological change after spelling was frozen by print), and enormous global variation.

---

**The through-line:** English's strangeness — its vast synonym sets, its spelling-pronunciation gaps, its tolerance for borrowing — all follow directly from these specific historical collisions. It did not "naturally" simplify; it was broken and rebuilt by contact.
