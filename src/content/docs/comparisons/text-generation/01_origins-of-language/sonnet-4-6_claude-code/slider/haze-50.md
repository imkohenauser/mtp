The English language might be thought of less as a thing built and more as something that kept arriving — wave after wave, each one leaving traces the next didn't entirely wash away.

It seems to begin somewhere in the migrations of Germanic tribes — Angles, Saxons, Jutes — crossing into Britain around the 5th century, settling over a land already layered with Celtic speech and the fading echo of Latin. What they brought became what we call Old English, a language with a roughness to it, an inflected grammar that carried gender and case the way Latin did. *Beowulf* swims in that water — dense, alliterative, nothing quite like what we'd recognize as English today, yet somehow the same river.

Then the Vikings arrived, and something softened. Norse and Old English were cousins of a kind, close enough that speakers might have understood each other imperfectly, trading words across the confusion. *Sky*, *egg*, *they* — these drifted in, quietly, the way borrowed words tend to do.

1066 is the year that tends to stand out, the Norman Conquest. French became the language of courts and castles, Latin the language of churches, Old English something spoken closer to the ground. For a few centuries the language lived in this stratified arrangement — which might explain why English ended up with so many synonym pairs: *begin* and *commence*, *ask* and *inquire*, *pig* and *pork*. The Saxon animal in the field, the French animal on the table.

Middle English is where the grammar begins to loosen its grip on inflection and lean more on word order instead. Chaucer belongs to this middle stretch — already more legible, though still asking something of the reader. The vowels at this point were arranged differently too, and then, between roughly 1400 and 1700, they shifted — the Great Vowel Shift, a slow rotation through the mouth that still isn't entirely understood, that turned the long vowels into something approaching what they sound like now.

The printing press arrived around the middle of this drift, and with it came something like standardization — London English, roughly, becoming the model. Spelling started to freeze even as pronunciation kept moving, which might be one reason the two feel so unrelated today.

Shakespeare and the King James Bible sit near the edge of what tends to get called Early Modern English. By this point the essential shape is recognizable: articles, auxiliary verbs, a relatively fixed word order. And the vocabulary was expanding — borrowing heavily from Latin and Greek as humanist scholarship moved words into common use.

After that, empire carried English outward, and it began to accumulate differently, picking up words from Hindi, Arabic, Malay, the Americas. It might be more accurate now to speak of Englishes — each one evolving at its own pace, shaped by what it was spoken alongside.

The language hasn't finished arriving, probably. New vocabularies keep forming at the edges — digital ones, subcultural ones — and what counts as standard keeps shifting, as it tends to do.
