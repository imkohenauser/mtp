English traces its origins to the Germanic dialects brought to Britain by the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes in the 5th century AD. This early form — Old English — bears little resemblance to modern usage. *Beowulf* was written in it.

The Norman Conquest of 1066 introduced a massive influx of French and Latin vocabulary. Legal, governmental, and ecclesiastical language became largely Romance in origin. A rough pattern emerged: Germanic words for common, everyday things; French and Latin for abstraction, prestige, or power.

By around 1400, Middle English had developed. Chaucer wrote in it. The language was still unstable — spelling was inconsistent, dialects varied sharply by region.

The Great Vowel Shift (roughly 1400–1700) changed how long vowels were pronounced across England. The written language didn't follow, which is why English spelling is so mismatched with pronunciation.

The printing press arrived in England in 1476. It standardized the London dialect and fixed spelling conventions that have remained largely unchanged since.

Early Modern English — the language of Shakespeare and the King James Bible — is the direct ancestor of what is spoken today. Colonial expansion from the 16th century onward spread English globally, accelerating borrowing from hundreds of other languages.

Modern English is notable for the size of its lexicon, the near-collapse of its grammatical case system, and its extreme resistance to formal regulation.
