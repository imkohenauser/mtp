The origins of English are a fascinating tangle — though depending on where you enter the story, you might frame it quite differently.

**One thread: the Germanic roots**

Around the 5th–6th centuries CE, Angles, Saxons, and Jutes migrated into what is now Britain. The dialects they brought — collectively called Old English — were closely related to Old Frisian and Old Saxon. But one might ask: is that really the "origin," or is it better described as a transplantation of something that already existed on the continent?

**Another thread: the layering problem**

Old English was then reshaped by waves of Scandinavian settlement (the Danelaw), then dramatically altered by the Norman Conquest of 1066, which introduced massive French (and through it, Latin) vocabulary. Some linguists treat Middle English as so different from Old English that it might almost count as a separate language. Others see continuity. The question of "how different is different enough to be a new language" doesn't have a clean answer.

**A third entry point: Latin's double role**

Latin influenced English twice — once indirectly through Norman French, and once directly through the Renaissance, when scholars deliberately borrowed Latin and Greek terms for science, philosophy, and theology. So the "classical influence" isn't a single event but two overlapping waves, which complicates any tidy timeline.

**Some materials worth comparing:**

- David Crystal's *The Stories of English* — emphasizes dialect diversity over a single standard history
- Melvyn Bragg's *The Adventure of English* — more popular, treats English as an almost competitive organism
- The Old English corpus itself (Beowulf, the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle) vs. Chaucer's Middle English — the contrast is striking firsthand

A question that might reframe this: if English had never encountered French after 1066, would it look more like modern German or more like modern Frisian — and does that change how you think about what "English" fundamentally is?
