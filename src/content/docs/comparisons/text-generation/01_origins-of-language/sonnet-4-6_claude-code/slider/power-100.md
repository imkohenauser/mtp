**English is Germanic at its core — everything else is layering on top.**

Here is the development, from roots to modern form:

---

**1. Old English (450–1100 AD) — The Foundation**

Anglo-Saxon settlers from northern Germany and Denmark brought West Germanic dialects to Britain in the 5th century. These dialects fused into Old English. It was highly inflected — nouns had four cases, verbs had complex conjugations. The vocabulary was almost entirely Germanic. *Beowulf* is the landmark text.

Viking invasions (793–1000 AD) added a decisive layer: Old Norse contributed roughly **1,000 core words**, including pronouns like *they*, *them*, *their* — words so fundamental they replaced native forms.

**2. Middle English (1100–1500) — The Fracture**

The Norman Conquest of 1066 split English. French became the language of power; English survived among commoners. The result: English shed most of its inflections (simplifying dramatically) and absorbed **10,000+ French/Latin words** — law, government, cuisine, art. Two vocabularies now exist in parallel: *begin/commence*, *ask/inquire*, *kingly/royal*.

**3. Early Modern English (1500–1700) — The Explosion**

The Renaissance and printing press standardized spelling and spread literacy. Latin and Greek poured in to name new ideas — *anatomy*, *atmosphere*, *hypothesis*. Shakespeare wrote in this period. The King James Bible (1611) locked in thousands of phrases still in use today.

**4. Modern English (1700–present) — Global Reach**

British colonialism exported English worldwide, spawning American, Australian, Indian, and Caribbean variants. Each absorbed local vocabulary. The Industrial Revolution and then the digital age added thousands of technical terms. English now has the **largest vocabulary of any language** — over 600,000 words in major dictionaries.

---

**The key force throughout: English never purged foreign vocabulary.** It accumulated. That is why it has three registers for almost every concept — Germanic (plain), French (formal), Latin/Greek (technical). The language is not diluted by borrowing. It is powerful because of it.
