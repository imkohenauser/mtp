The English language has a history that's almost more interesting than the language itself — a series of collisions and absorptions that produced something gloriously inconsistent.

---

**Old English (circa 450–1150 CE)**

The story begins with the Anglo-Saxons, Germanic tribes — Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — who migrated to Britain from what is now Denmark and northern Germany after the Roman withdrawal in the 5th century. Their dialects merged into what we now call Old English, or Anglo-Saxon. It was a heavily inflected language with gendered nouns and complex case endings. *Beowulf* was composed in it. If you could time-travel and listen to it spoken, you wouldn't recognize a word.

The Vikings complicated things considerably. Their Old Norse raids and settlements (8th–11th centuries) left English a trove of everyday words: *sky*, *window*, *knife*, *they*, *them*, *their*. Scandinavian gave English its third-person plural pronouns, which is actually remarkable — pronouns almost never get borrowed between languages.

---

**The Norman Conquest and Middle English (1066–1500)**

1066 changed everything. William the Conqueror's Norman French became the language of the court, law, and church. The peasants kept speaking English; the elite spoke French. The result, over centuries, was a language stratified by vocabulary: Anglo-Saxon words tend to be earthy and concrete (*house*, *eat*, *swine*), while French borrowings cluster around prestige and abstraction (*mansion*, *dine*, *pork*). That's where the peculiar English split comes from — pigs become pork on the table, cows become beef.

By the way, Chaucer wrote in Middle English — and it's genuinely readable with a bit of effort, which is not something you can say of Old English at all.

---

**Early Modern English (1500–1700)**

The printing press (introduced to England by Caxton in 1476) standardized spelling around the London dialect. The Renaissance brought an enormous influx of Latin and Greek vocabulary as scholars reached back to classical learning. Writers and intellectuals sometimes called this "inkhorn terms" — fancy borrowings that felt artificial. Many stuck anyway: *atmosphere*, *catastrophe*, *democracy*.

The King James Bible (1611) and Shakespeare's plays both shaped what became standard written English, fixing idioms and phrasings into the language's bedrock.

---

**Modern English and its global scatter**

By the 18th century, English was being carried across the world through trade and empire. Each destination left its own deposits. From India: *shampoo*, *bungalow*, *jungle*. From the Caribbean and West Africa: influences on American English and its many varieties. From Australia, the Americas, and elsewhere: thousands of words for local plants, animals, and landscapes.

English also never stopped absorbing. It borrows with almost no resistance from any language it touches, which is why it has more words than almost any other language — and also why its spelling is so chaotic: words preserve the phonological rules of the languages they arrived from rather than conforming to a single system.

---

There's something almost improvisational about the whole arc — not a designed structure but a series of historical accidents, each one depositing a layer. The language we use now is essentially a palimpsest, with Roman Latin underneath, Anglo-Saxon above that, Norse threaded through, Norman French draped over it, and centuries of further additions piled on top.
