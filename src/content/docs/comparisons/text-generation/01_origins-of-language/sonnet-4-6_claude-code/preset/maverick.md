The word "English" is a curious starting point — because it arrives late. The language itself was well underway before anyone called it that.

The Germanic tribes who crossed the North Sea (Angles, Saxons, Jutes, Frisians — a loose coalition, really) brought something we now call Old English, though they wouldn't have recognized the label. And it was already a mosaic. Inflected, guttural, heavily case-marked — closer to modern German or Dutch than to anything you'd recognize on a street sign today.

Then Norse arrived. Not diplomatically. The Danelaw left behind a quiet residue: *sky*, *egg*, *knife*, *they* — basic pronouns and everyday objects. Worth noting that "they" replacing the Old English third-person plural might be one of the most intimate grammatical invasions in linguistic history. A foreign pronoun that stuck.

And incidentally — there's an interesting question about what 1066 actually *changed*, linguistically. The Norman Conquest is usually presented as the great rupture. But Old English was already shifting. The Norman French influence layered prestige vocabulary over Germanic roots, which is why in English you can *eat* (Germanic) or *dine* (Romance), *buy* (Old English) or *purchase* (Old French), *kingly* or *royal*. The bifurcation runs deep, almost like a class system encoded into the lexicon itself.

Middle English — Chaucer's English — starts to look readable to modern eyes, but it was still wildly variable. There was no standard spelling, no dictionary, no enforced grammar. Each scribe wrote roughly as they heard it. Regional dialects were essentially different languages.

Then printing. Caxton setting up his press in Westminster around 1476 is often cited as the moment standardization begins to crystallize — not because Caxton *intended* standardization, but because you can't print a thousand copies of a book in four different dialects simultaneously. London/East Midlands English started winning by default.

The Great Vowel Shift — running roughly through the 15th and 16th centuries — is one of those events that's almost impossible to fully explain. All the long vowels of English rearranged themselves systematically, which is part of why English spelling looks so disconnected from English pronunciation. The spelling froze (more or less) while the sounds kept moving.

And then empire, colonization, and the 18th-century prescriptivists who wanted to fix English, regulate it, maybe even create an academy like the Académie française (Swift and Dryden pushed for this). They failed, which perhaps explains something about English's character — it absorbed rather than purified, sprawled rather than crystallized.

There's another way to look at all of this, though: not as a continuous development of *one* language, but as a series of distinct speech communities that happened to be called by the same name across time. Is the English of Beowulf the "same language" as the English of *Ulysses*? In what sense?
