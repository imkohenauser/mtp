## Origins and Historical Development of English

### Proto-Indo-European Roots (~4000–3000 BCE)
English traces back to Proto-Indo-European (PIE), a reconstructed ancestral language spoken by peoples on the Pontic steppe. PIE eventually split into branches including Germanic, from which English descends.

### Old English / Anglo-Saxon (~450–1100 CE)
When Germanic tribes — Angles, Saxons, and Jutes — invaded Britain after Roman withdrawal (5th century), they brought their dialects. This fusion became **Old English**. It was a heavily inflected language, almost unrecognizable to modern readers. *Beowulf* was composed in this period. Viking invasions (8th–10th c.) added Norse vocabulary: *sky*, *knife*, *egg*, *they*.

### Middle English (~1100–1500)
The Norman Conquest (1066) was transformative. French-speaking Normans became England's ruling class, flooding the language with French and Latin vocabulary — especially in law, cuisine, and nobility. English lost most of its inflections during this period, shifting toward word-order grammar. Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* represents this era.

### Early Modern English (~1500–1700)
The Renaissance brought massive Latin and Greek borrowing for science and philosophy. The printing press (1476 in England) standardized spelling. The Great Vowel Shift (~1400–1700) dramatically changed pronunciation. Shakespeare and the King James Bible (~1611) codified the literary register.

### Modern English (~1700–present)
The British Empire spread English globally, absorbing words from hundreds of languages (*bungalow*, *jungle*, *algebra*, *pyjamas*). The Industrial Revolution and then the digital age generated enormous technical vocabulary. American English diverged as a major dialect by the 18th century.

### Why English is Unusual
English is a linguistic hybrid — a Germanic core with a massive Romance overlay — which is why it has near-synonyms from different sources: *kingly* (Anglo-Saxon) vs. *royal* (French) vs. *regal* (Latin). This layering also explains its enormous vocabulary (~170,000 words in current use vs. ~185,000 total) and notoriously inconsistent spelling.
